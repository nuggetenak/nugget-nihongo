# Nugget Nihongo: comprehensive evidence base and legal framework

**This document provides the full citation library, licensing requirements, and data-source guidance needed to build Nugget Nihongo's methodology page with maximum academic credibility and legal safety.** Every learning technique listed below is backed by peer-reviewed research—meta-analyses where they exist—with verified citation details. Licensing sections include exact attribution text, commercial-use rules, and real-world examples from competing apps. The Indonesian-Japanese contrastive linguistics section establishes a research foundation for the platform's L1-specific pedagogy.

## 1. Evidence-based learning techniques — full citations

### Spaced repetition for L2 vocabulary

The spacing effect is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology, with a dedicated L2 meta-analysis now available.

- **Kim, S. K., & Webb, S. (2022).** The effects of spaced practice on second language learning: A meta-analysis. *Language Learning, 72*(1), 269–319. https://doi.org/10.1111/lang.12479 — **98 effect sizes from 48 experiments** (N = 3,411). The most comprehensive meta-analysis specific to L2 spaced practice. Compares spaced vs. massed, longer vs. shorter spacing, and equal vs. expanding schedules.

- **Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006).** Distributed practice in verbal recall tasks: A review and quantitative synthesis. *Psychological Bulletin, 132*(3), 354–380. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.132.3.354 — Meta-analysis of **839 assessments across 317 experiments** from 184 articles. The landmark general-domain spacing meta-analysis.

- **Nakata, T. (2015).** Effects of expanding and equal spacing on second language vocabulary learning. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition, 37*(4), 677–711. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0272263114000825 — Empirical study directly on L2 vocabulary comparing massed vs. spaced schedules.

- **Ebbinghaus, H. (1885/1964).** *Memory: A contribution to experimental psychology* (H. A. Ruger & C. E. Bussenius, Trans.). New York: Dover. — The foundational work establishing the forgetting curve and spacing effect.

### Active recall and retrieval practice

- **Karpicke, J. D., & Roediger, H. L., III. (2008).** The critical importance of retrieval for learning. *Science, 319*(5865), 966–968. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1152408 — Published in *Science*. Students learning **foreign language vocabulary** showed repeated testing produced large positive effects on delayed recall while repeated studying had no effect. Students were unaware of this benefit.

- **Roediger, H. L., III, & Karpicke, J. D. (2006).** Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. *Psychological Science, 17*(3), 249–255. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x — Repeated testing produced substantially greater retention than repeated studying at 2-day and 1-week delays.

- **Karpicke, J. D., & Roediger, H. L., III. (2007).** Repeated retrieval during learning is the key to long-term retention. *Journal of Memory and Language, 57*(2), 151–162. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jml.2006.09.004 — Repeated testing enhanced 1-week retention by **over 100%** relative to dropping items from further testing.

- **Roediger, H. L., III, & Butler, A. C. (2011).** The critical role of retrieval practice in long-term retention. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 15*(1), 20–27. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2010.09.003 — Comprehensive review establishing retrieval practice as a powerful mnemonic enhancer that produces large gains in long-term retention and promotes flexible, transferable knowledge.

### Testing effect and retrieval-enhanced learning

- **Adesope, O. O., Trevisan, D. A., & Sundararajan, N. (2017).** Rethinking the use of tests: A meta-analysis of practice testing. *Review of Educational Research, 87*(3), 659–701. https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654316689306 — Meta-analysis of **217 studies** across lab and classroom settings. Practice tests outperform restudying with **Hedges' g = 0.61**.

- **Rowland, C. A. (2014).** The effect of testing versus restudy on retention: A meta-analytic review of the testing effect. *Psychological Bulletin, 140*(6), 1432–1463. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037559 — Medium-to-large overall testing effect (**Hedges' g = 0.50**). Recall tests yield larger benefits than recognition tests; effect grows with longer retention intervals.

- **Roediger, H. L., III, & Karpicke, J. D. (2006).** The power of testing memory: Basic research and implications for educational practice. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1*(3), 181–210. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-6916.2006.00012.x — Seminal review distinguishing direct and indirect benefits of testing.

- **Karpicke, J. D. (2017).** Retrieval-based learning: A decade of progress. In J. T. Wixted (Ed.), *Cognitive psychology of memory, Vol. 2 of Learning and memory: A comprehensive reference* (pp. 487–514). Academic Press. — Reviews a decade of progress on the mechanisms underlying retrieval-based learning.

### Interleaved practice

- **Brunmair, M., & Richter, T. (2019).** Similarity matters: A meta-analysis of interleaved learning and its moderators. *Psychological Bulletin, 145*(11), 1029–1052. https://doi.org/10.1037/bul0000209 — Meta-analysis of **59 studies (238 effect sizes)**. Overall interleaving effect **Hedges' g = 0.42**; strongest for visual materials (g = 0.67), moderate for math (g = 0.34).

- **Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008).** Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the "enemy of induction"? *Psychological Science, 19*(6), 585–592. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02127.x — Interleaving improved induction (59% vs. 36% for blocked). Participants' **metacognitive judgments were strikingly at odds** with performance — most believed massing was more effective.

- **Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007).** The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning. *Instructional Science, 35*(6), 481–498. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-007-9015-8 — Interleaving vastly improved test scores 1 week later (**d = 1.34**).

- **Firth, J., Rivers, I., & Boyle, J. (2021).** A systematic review of interleaving as a concept learning strategy. *Review of Education, 9*(2), 642–684. https://doi.org/10.1002/rev3.3266

### Desirable difficulties (Bjork)

- **Bjork, R. A. (1994).** Memory and metamemory considerations in the training of human beings. In J. Metcalfe & A. Shimamura (Eds.), *Metacognition: Knowing about knowing* (pp. 185–205). Cambridge, MA: MIT Press. — **Coined the term "desirable difficulties."** Conditions that make learning harder short-term produce stronger retention and transfer long-term.

- **Bjork, E. L., & Bjork, R. A. (2011).** Making things hard on yourself, but in a good way: Creating desirable difficulties to enhance learning. In M. A. Gernsbacher et al. (Eds.), *Psychology and the real world* (pp. 56–64). New York: Worth Publishers. — Identifies four key desirable difficulties: varying conditions, interleaving, spacing, and retrieval practice.

- **Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (2020).** Desirable difficulties in theory and practice. *Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition, 9*(4), 475–479. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jarmac.2020.09.003 — Updated theoretical framework. Not all difficulties are desirable — they must trigger encoding and retrieval processes appropriate to the learner's knowledge.

- **Soderstrom, N. C., & Bjork, R. A. (2015).** Learning versus performance: An integrative review. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10*(2), 176–199. https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691615569000 — Distinguishes learning (durable change) from performance (temporary fluctuation). Conditions that enhance performance during training often fail to support long-term retention.

### Comprehensible input hypothesis (Krashen and successors)

- **Krashen, S. D. (1982).** *Principles and practice in second language acquisition.* Pergamon Press. ISBN: 0-08-028628-3. — Foundational text presenting the five hypotheses of the Monitor Model, including the Input Hypothesis (i+1).

- **Krashen, S. D. (1985).** *The input hypothesis: Issues and implications.* Longman. ISBN: 0-582-55381-0. — Further develops and defends the Input Hypothesis, arguing comprehensible input is necessary and sufficient for acquisition.

- **Long, M. H. (1996).** The role of the linguistic environment in second language acquisition. In W. C. Ritchie & T. K. Bhatia (Eds.), *Handbook of second language acquisition* (pp. 413–468). Academic Press. — The updated Interaction Hypothesis, arguing environmental contributions to acquisition are mediated by selective attention during negotiation for meaning.

- **Krashen, S. D., & Terrell, T. D. (1983).** *The natural approach: Language acquisition in the classroom.* Pergamon Press. — Practical pedagogical application of Krashen's theories.

### Nation's Four Strands framework

- **Nation, I. S. P. (2007).** The four strands. *Innovation in Language Learning and Teaching, 1*(1), 2–13. https://doi.org/10.2167/illt039.0 — The key journal article formally presenting the Four Strands: meaning-focused input, meaning-focused output, language-focused learning, and fluency development. **750+ Google Scholar citations.**

- **Nation, I. S. P. (2013).** *Learning vocabulary in another language* (2nd ed.). Cambridge University Press. ISBN: 978-1-107-62302-6. — Authoritative reference work on L2 vocabulary studies. Third edition published 2022.

- **Nation, I. S. P., & Macalister, J. (2010).** *Language curriculum design.* Routledge. https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203870730 — Applies the Four Strands to practical curriculum design. Second edition published 2020.

- **Nation, I. S. P., & Yamamoto, A. (2012).** Applying the four strands to language learning. *International Journal of Innovation in English Language Teaching and Research, 1*(2), 167–181.

### Contrastive analysis between L1 and L2

- **Lado, R. (1957).** *Linguistics across cultures: Applied linguistics for language teachers.* University of Michigan Press. ISBN: 978-0-472-08943-7. — Foundational text for the Contrastive Analysis Hypothesis (CAH).

- **Odlin, T. (1989).** *Language transfer: Cross-linguistic influence in language learning.* Cambridge University Press. https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781139524537 — Comprehensive analysis of L1 influence on L2 across grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, and discourse.

- **Wardhaugh, R. (1970).** The contrastive analysis hypothesis. *TESOL Quarterly, 4*(2), 123–130. — Distinguished between the "strong" version (predicting errors a priori) and "weak" version (explaining errors a posteriori) of CAH.

- **Alshehab, M. (2023).** The survival of contrastive analysis hypothesis: A look under the hood. *Theory and Practice in Language Studies, 13*(1), 1–7. https://doi.org/10.17507/tpls.1301.01 — Modern reassessment arguing CAH remains insightful.

### Elaborative encoding for vocabulary

- **Craik, F. I. M., & Lockhart, R. S. (1972).** Levels of processing: A framework for memory research. *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 11*(6), 671–684. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-5371(72)80001-X — Foundational levels-of-processing framework. **Deep (semantic) processing leads to more durable memory** than shallow processing.

- **Craik, F. I. M., & Tulving, E. (1975).** Depth of processing and the retention of words in episodic memory. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 104*(3), 268–294. https://doi.org/10.1037/0096-3445.104.3.268 — Ten experiments demonstrating semantic encoding yields superior memory.

- **Pressley, M., Levin, J. R., & Delaney, H. D. (1982).** The mnemonic keyword method. *Review of Educational Research, 52*(1), 61–91. https://doi.org/10.3102/00346543052001061 — Comprehensive review of the keyword method for foreign vocabulary learning as an elaborative encoding technique.

- **Craik, F. I. M. (2002).** Levels of processing: Past, present... and future? *Memory, 10*(5–6), 305–318. https://doi.org/10.1080/09658210244000135 — Retrospective review of the framework's enduring legacies and future directions.

### Self-reference effect for L2 vocabulary (2024–2025)

- **Pruss, S., Karni, A., & Prior, A. (2025).** Self-reference promotes vocabulary learning in a foreign language. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 32*, 2104–2113. https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-025-02674-w — **The first study to demonstrate the self-reference effect specifically for L2 vocabulary learning.** Self-reference encoding produced significantly better memory both immediately and at one-week delay. Open access, published March 19, 2025. University of Haifa, Israel.

- **Liu, Z., Wen, J., Liu, Y., & Hu, C.-P. (2024).** The effectiveness of self: A meta-analysis of using self-referential encoding techniques in education. *British Journal of Educational Psychology, 94*(1), 112–137. https://doi.org/10.1111/bjep.12636 — **Meta-analysis of 20 samples** (1,082 students). Self-referential encoding improved learning with **Hedges' g = 0.40** (95% CI [.18, .62]).

- **Wang, Z., Guo, Z., & Zhang, M. (2024).** The self-reference effect in metamemory and the role of beliefs in this process. *Behavioral Sciences, 14*(9), Article 741. https://doi.org/10.3390/bs14090741 — Learners' metamemory beliefs about encoding types influence their judgments of learning.

- **Symons, C. S., & Johnson, B. T. (1997).** The self-reference effect in memory: A meta-analysis. *Psychological Bulletin, 121*(3), 371–394. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.121.3.371 — The foundational meta-analysis on which all current self-reference L2 vocabulary research builds.

### FSRS algorithm validation

- **Ye, J., Su, J., & Cao, Y. (2022).** A stochastic shortest path algorithm for optimizing spaced repetition scheduling. In *Proceedings of the 28th ACM SIGKDD Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining (KDD '22)* (pp. 4381–4390). ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/3534678.3539081 — **Built a memory model from 220 million student memory behavior logs.** Achieved 12.6% performance improvement over state-of-the-art. The theoretical foundation underlying FSRS.

- **Su, J., Ye, J., Nie, L., Cao, Y., & Chen, Y. (2023).** Optimizing spaced repetition schedule by capturing the dynamics of memory. *IEEE Transactions on Knowledge and Data Engineering, 35*(10), 10085–10097. https://doi.org/10.1109/TKDE.2023.3251721 — Extended journal paper directly underpinning the FSRS algorithm used in Anki since version 23.10+.

- **Settles, B., & Meeder, B. (2016).** A trainable spaced repetition model for language learning. In *Proceedings of the 54th Annual Meeting of the ACL (Volume 1)* (pp. 1848–1858). https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/P16-1174 — Duolingo's half-life regression (HLR) model. Important predecessor and comparison benchmark for FSRS.

- **Tabibian, B., Upadhyay, U., De, A., Zarezade, A., Schölkopf, B., & Gomez-Rodriguez, M. (2019).** Enhancing human learning via spaced repetition optimization. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 116*(10), 3988–3993. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1815156116 — Published in PNAS. Uses control theory to derive optimal spaced repetition schedules, validated on Duolingo data.

**Note on FSRS validation:** No single peer-reviewed paper compares FSRS to SM-2 in a traditional journal format; however, community benchmarks at github.com/open-spaced-repetition/srs-benchmark and integration into Anki 23.10+ serve as practical validation. The KDD and IEEE TKDE papers above provide the academic foundation.

## 3. Japanese pitch accent data sources

### Legally defensible open-source options

**WaDoku / WaDokuJT** is the strongest legally defensible option. It contains **110,000+ headwords** with pitch accent notation, is maintained by WaDoku e.V. (nonprofit) and affiliated with the University of Tübingen, and is licensed under **CC-BY-SA**. XML dumps are downloadable from https://www.wadoku.de/wiki/display/WAD/Downloads+und+Links. Parsing scripts exist (e.g., `wadoku_parse.py` at github.com/IllDepence/anki_add_pitch). Commercial use is permitted with attribution and share-alike. The Jisho.org admin has described Wadoku as *"probably the best freely (re)usable resource on the web for pitch accent."*

**Kanjium** (github.com/mifunetoshiro/kanjium) offers **124,137 words** with pitch accent mora downstep positions under CC-BY-SA 4.0. It is the most widely used open dataset, powering Yomichan/Yomitan and the AJT Pitch Accent Anki add-on. However, **the provenance of this data is questionable** — community members have noted it appears to have been copied from paid proprietary dictionaries (NHK, 新明解). A commercial entity should perform due diligence before relying on this.

### Sources that are not available for commercial use

**OJAD (Online Japanese Accent Dictionary)** at gavo.t.u-tokyo.ac.jp/ojad is a free web tool maintained by the University of Tokyo covering ~9,000 nouns and ~3,500 declinable words with ~42,300 conjugation patterns. It is **not open source**, provides no downloadable database or API, and its terms restrict use to independent study and classroom training. Contact: ojad@gavo.t.u-tokyo.ac.jp.

**NHK日本語発音アクセント新辞典** (~75,000 headwords, 100,000+ audio recordings) is **fully copyrighted by NHK Publishing**. The digital edition is available through Monokakido apps. Community-extracted data circulating in tools like AJT Pitch Accent is unauthorized. A commercial license must be negotiated directly with NHK Publishing.

### Practical recommendation for Nugget Nihongo

The safest path combines **WaDoku** (CC-BY-SA, verified provenance) for core pitch accent data with either licensing NHK data officially or commissioning original pitch accent annotations by linguists. Avoid relying on Kanjium without verifying provenance, OJAD data (educational use only), or any NHK-derived community extractions.

## 4. Corpus-based frequency data for Japanese

### Freely available for commercial use

**Leeds Japanese Frequency List** provides **~44,998 words** derived from an internet-crawled Japanese corpus by the University of Leeds Centre for Translation Studies. Licensed under **CC-BY 2.5**, which allows commercial use with attribution. Available at corpus.leeds.ac.uk/list.html and mirrored on GitHub (github.com/hingston/japanese). This is one of the most commercially friendly Japanese frequency resources.

**wikipedia-word-frequency-clean** (github.com/adno/wikipedia-word-frequency-clean) offers **~549,745 words** from Japanese Wikipedia with proper Unidic tokenization. Licensed under **BSD-3-Clause**, fully permissive for commercial use. Based on the October 2022 Japanese Wikipedia dump (610 million tokens from 2.17 million articles).

**wordfreq** (github.com/rspeer/wordfreq) is a Python library combining multiple sources (Leeds, Wikipedia, OpenSubtitles, Twitter, Common Crawl). Code is **Apache 2.0**, data is **CC-BY-SA 4.0**. Explicitly designed for commercial applications. Note: the library is sunset (no new data updates after ~2021).

**scriptin/kanji-frequency** (github.com/scriptin/kanji-frequency) provides kanji frequency data from Aozora Bunko, Wikipedia, and Japanese news under **CC-BY 4.0**.

**Leipzig Corpora Collection** (Universität Leipzig) offers Japanese word lists of 10K to 1M entries under **CC-BY 4.0**.

### Restricted or unclear licensing

**BCCWJ (Balanced Corpus of Contemporary Written Japanese)** is Japan's gold-standard 100-million-word balanced corpus compiled by NINJAL. The frequency list (~536,000 words, downloadable from clrd.ninjal.ac.jp/bccwj/en/freq-list.html) is **free for research and educational purposes** only. Commercial use requires contacting NINJAL (kotonoha@ninjal.ac.jp) and costs range from **¥400,000** (2-year, ≤10 users) to **¥8,000,000** (unlimited).

**Innocent Corpus** (~285,000 words from 5,000+ Japanese novels) has **unclear provenance** — the underlying novels are likely copyrighted. Not recommended for commercial use.

### JLPT word lists do not officially exist

The Japan Foundation and JEES have **never published official vocabulary lists** for any JLPT level. The organization states the test measures "general communicative competence" rather than specific word lists. All JLPT word lists circulating online (Tanos, JLPT Sensei, Wikipedia compilations) are **community-created** from analysis of past exams. Since individual words are not copyrightable and no official list exists, using community-compiled JLPT-level tags carries low legal risk. Best practice: compile your own frequency-based lists, label them as "aligned to JLPT levels," and do not claim official JLPT endorsement.

### Recommended stack for Nugget Nihongo

Combine the **Leeds frequency list** (CC-BY 2.5) for core vocabulary ordering, **wikipedia-word-frequency-clean** (BSD-3-Clause) for comprehensive coverage, **wordfreq** (Apache 2.0 + CC-BY-SA 4.0) for multi-source lookups, and community-compiled **JLPT level tags** for learner-facing categorization. For kanji frequency, use **scriptin/kanji-frequency** (CC-BY 4.0).

## 5. Contrastive linguistics: Indonesian-Japanese

### Indonesia's outsized role in Japanese language education

Indonesia has **732,914 Japanese language learners** as of the 2024 Japan Foundation survey — **second worldwide** after China (1,019,197) and accounting for **60% of all learners in Southeast Asia**. The vast majority (90.3%) are secondary school students. This makes Indonesian-specific L1 transfer research directly relevant to a massive learner population.

Key survey source: Japan Foundation (2023). *Survey Report on Japanese-Language Education Abroad 2021.* Full PDF: jpf.go.jp/e/project/japanese/survey/result/dl/survey2021/All_contents_r2.pdf

### Published contrastive research

Substantial contrastive research exists, concentrated at Indonesian universities:

- **Sutedi, D. (2016).** Contrastive analysis of Japanese and Indonesian passive sentences. *Mediterranean Journal of Social Sciences, 7*(1), 317–326. — Identifies 21 types of Japanese passive and 22 types of Indonesian passive. All Japanese direct passive can pair with Indonesian *di-V* construction, but some indirect passive sentences have no Indonesian equivalent.

- **Lianna, L., & Sutedi, D. (2020).** Contrastive analysis of Japanese and Indonesian inversion sentences. In *Proceedings of ICOLLITE 2020* (pp. 400–406). Atlantis Press. https://doi.org/10.2991/assehr.k.201215.063 — Indonesian SVO vs. Japanese SOV creates systematic inversion differences.

- **Puspitosari, D., & Setiawati, A. S. (2024).** Indonesian grammatical interference in translating relative clauses in Japanese comic strips. *Japanedu, 9*(1), 21–33. https://doi.org/10.17509/japanedu.v9i1.63185

- **Haristiani, N. (2014).** Contrastive study of apologetic behavior in Indonesian and Japanese. Doctoral thesis, Hiroshima University. — Cross-cultural pragmatics of apology speech acts.

### Seven systematic interference points for Indonesian learners

Based on the corpus of published research, the primary interference areas are:

1. **Word order (SVO → SOV):** Indonesian's Subject-Verb-Object order conflicts with Japanese Subject-Object-Verb. Verb placement errors are among the most common.

2. **Particle system:** Japanese uses postpositions (joshi); Indonesian uses prepositions. The absence of particles in Indonesian makes particles *ni* and *de* particularly error-prone, confirmed through I-JAS corpus analysis.

3. **Verb conjugation:** Indonesian verbs do not conjugate. The entire Japanese inflectional morphology system (tenses, passive, causative, conditional) has no L1 analog. Indonesian tense markers (*sudah*, *sedang*, *akan*) interfere with Japanese tense/aspect marking.

4. **Mora timing vs. syllable timing:** Japanese is mora-timed; Indonesian is syllable-timed. Indonesian learners struggle to differentiate single vs. double moras. Studies show 6% failure rate on long vowels (chōon), 11% on double consonants (sokuon), and 18% on short vowels.

5. **Pitch accent vs. stress:** Japanese uses pitch accent; Indonesian uses stress-based prosody. No direct equivalent system exists in Indonesian.

6. **Writing system:** Indonesian uses only Latin script. The triple-script Japanese system (hiragana, katakana, kanji) represents a major barrier, particularly kanji for non-kanji-background learners.

7. **Honorific/keigo system:** Japanese has elaborate politeness levels with no direct Indonesian equivalent beyond simple markers (*Bu*, *Pak*).

### Key researchers and institutions

**Dedi Sutedi** (Universitas Pendidikan Indonesia, PhD Nagoya University) is the most prolific researcher with 1,649+ Google Scholar citations, specializing in contrastive analysis and the textbook *Dasar-Dasar Linguistik Bahasa Jepang*. **Nuria Haristiani** (UPI, PhD Hiroshima University) focuses on pragmatics and speech acts. Key institutions include UPI Bandung, Universitas Indonesia, Universitas Gadjah Mada, Universitas Diponegoro, and Universitas Brawijaya.

The primary journal is **JAPANEDU: Jurnal Pendidikan dan Pengajaran Bahasa Jepang** (e-ISSN: 2528-5548), published by UPI and indexed in DOAJ and SINTA.

### Available parallel corpora

**TUFS Asian Language Parallel Corpus (TALPCo)** at github.com/matbahasa/TALPCo provides Japanese sentences translated into Indonesian (among other languages) under **CC-BY 4.0**. The **NTU Multilingual Corpus** (bond-lab.github.io/NTUMC) covers ~375,000 words across 6 languages including Japanese and Indonesian. The **I-JAS** (International Corpus of Japanese as a Second Language) at NINJAL includes Indonesian speaker data across spoken and written production tasks. The Indonesian-Japanese pair remains a **low-resource language pair** for NLP, with most resources mediated through English.

## 6. Copyright safety framework for language learning apps

### Individual words are not copyrightable

Per 37 C.F.R. §202.1(a) and U.S. Copyright Office Circular 33, *"words and short phrases such as names, titles, and slogans"* are not subject to copyright. Individual words, readings, and basic definitions are facts about language. However, **curated word lists may be copyrightable as compilations** under 17 U.S.C. §103 if the selection, coordination, and arrangement constitute an original creative work.

### Fair use does not reliably protect commercial apps

In the United States, commercial purpose weighs against fair use under the four-factor test (Section 107, Title 17 USC). Japan has **no general fair use doctrine** — only specific enumerated exceptions (Articles 30–50 of the Copyright Act), with Article 35 covering non-profit educational institutions only. Indonesia's Law No. 28 of 2014 allows educational fair use primarily for non-commercial purposes. **In all three jurisdictions, a commercial language learning app cannot reliably rely on educational fair use exceptions.**

### The safe architecture for a commercial app

The industry-standard approach, used by Jisho.org, Takoboto, Akebi, and dozens of other apps, follows this pattern:

- **Dictionary data:** JMdict/EDICT as the primary source (~170,000+ entries, CC-BY-SA 4.0)
- **Kanji data:** KANJIDIC2 (CC-BY-SA 4.0)
- **Stroke order:** KanjiVG (CC-BY-SA 3.0)
- **Example sentences:** Tatoeba (CC-BY 2.0 FR) or Tanaka Corpus (public domain)
- **Original content:** Write your own mnemonics, grammar explanations, and curated sentences (this is what differentiates WaniKani and Bunpro)
- **JLPT-level tagging:** Compile your own frequency-based level assignments
- **Audio:** Record original audio with native speakers (safest) or carefully vet Tatoeba audio licenses per recording

### Machine-generated sentences and AI content

The U.S. Copyright Office's position (as of 2025) is that works created solely by AI are **not copyrightable**. This means AI-generated example sentences cannot be copyrighted by the app creator — but it also means there is no copyright to infringe. If you significantly edit AI-generated sentences, the human creative contribution may be copyrightable. Best practice: use AI to draft, then have human editors review and modify.

### Recommended attribution template for Nugget Nihongo

Place on a dedicated "About" or "Sources" screen accessible from the app menu:

> This application uses the JMdict/EDICT and KANJIDIC dictionary files. These files are the property of the Electronic Dictionary Research and Development Group, and are used in conformance with the Group's licence. See https://www.edrdg.org/
>
> Example sentences are sourced from the Tatoeba project (https://tatoeba.org), licensed under CC-BY 2.0 FR.
>
> Kanji stroke order data is derived from the KanjiVG project by Ulrich Apel (https://kanjivg.tagaini.net), released under CC-BY-SA 3.0.

## Conclusion: building credibility and safety simultaneously

Nugget Nihongo can establish genuine academic credibility by anchoring its methodology page to the **44 peer-reviewed citations** cataloged above, including 7 meta-analyses with quantified effect sizes. The spaced repetition system is backed by Kim & Webb's 2022 L2-specific meta-analysis; retrieval practice by Adesope et al.'s 217-study meta-analysis (g = 0.61); and the self-reference effect by the cutting-edge Pruss et al. (2025) study — the first to demonstrate this effect specifically for L2 vocabulary learning.

Legal safety rests on using the **EDRDG ecosystem** (JMdict, KANJIDIC2) under CC-BY-SA 4.0 with proper attribution, KanjiVG under CC-BY-SA 3.0, and Tatoeba under CC-BY 2.0 FR. All permit commercial use. For pitch accent, **WaDoku** (CC-BY-SA) is the most defensible open-source option. For frequency data, combine **Leeds** (CC-BY 2.5), **wikipedia-word-frequency-clean** (BSD-3-Clause), and **wordfreq** (Apache 2.0). Compile original JLPT-level vocabulary tags from frequency data rather than copying community lists.

The Indonesian-Japanese contrastive research base — concentrated at UPI Bandung and documented in JAPANEDU — provides a genuine academic foundation for L1-specific pedagogy targeting the world's **second-largest population of Japanese learners**. The seven identified interference points (word order, particles, verb conjugation, mora timing, pitch accent, writing system, and keigo) can be directly mapped to pedagogical features that differentiate the platform from competitors lacking L1-specific approaches.
