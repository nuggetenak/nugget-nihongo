### §17.1 — Andragogy: The Adult Learning Model

Adult language learners are not scaled-down versions of child learners. The dominant theoretical framework distinguishing adult from child learning is Malcolm Knowles's andragogy — a model that has direct and non-trivial implications for every design decision in Nugget Nihongo, from card content framing to onboarding flow.

---

**[AL-01]** Knowles, M. S. (1980). *The Modern Practice of Adult Education: From Pedagogy to Andragogy* (2nd ed.). Cambridge Book Company.

**Core contribution:** Knowles's andragogy rests on four interlocking assumptions about the adult learner that together constitute a systematic departure from pedagogical (teacher-directed, subject-centered) models. First, adults are *self-directed*: they prefer to manage the pace, sequence, and focus of their own learning. Second, adults are *goal-oriented*: before investing cognitive effort, they require a clear and satisfying answer to "why does this matter for me now?" Third, adults are *experience-centered*: they bring a rich reservoir of prior knowledge and life experience that simultaneously enriches and filters incoming information — new material is processed through, and sometimes distorted by, existing schemas. Fourth, adults are *relevance-oriented*: learning must be immediately applicable to a life role, professional context, or personal aspiration, not deferred to a future that feels abstract. Knowles contrasts andragogy with pedagogy, where authority over learning resides with the teacher and relevance is assumed rather than demonstrated. The shift from a pedagogical to an andragogical design philosophy is, in Knowles's account, a shift from delivering content to facilitating meaning.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The SSW (Specified Skilled Worker) learner base is paradigmatically andragogical — adult workers with urgent, role-specific language needs who are managing their own study time outside formal instruction. All four principles translate directly into platform obligations: self-direction requires learner control over deck customization and daily quota; goal-orientation requires explicit contextualisation of every card within SSW workplace scenarios; experience-orientation requires card design that activates and builds on Indonesian L1 knowledge rather than ignoring it; relevance-orientation requires that every card signal, as a design primitive, *why this item matters for your work context*.

---

**[AL-02]** Cross, K. P. (1981). *Adults as Learners: Increasing Participation and Facilitating Learning*. Jossey-Bass.

**Core contribution:** Cross synthesises the empirical participation literature and proposes the Characteristics of Adults as Learners (CAL) model, which identifies two clusters of variables shaping adult learning engagement: personal characteristics (physical, psychological, and sociocultural attributes that change over the lifespan) and situational characteristics (part-time vs. full-time study, voluntary vs. compulsory participation, proximate vs. distance setting). A critical finding is that adults engage in learning when they perceive it as immediately useful and when the situational barriers — time scarcity, financial cost, scheduling constraints — are low. Cross also identifies a motivational profile specific to adults: they are strongly driven by occupational advancement and secondarily by social relationships, with abstract intellectual curiosity ranking lower than is often assumed in instructional design. This learner profile is distinct from the student learner profile that much instructional technology implicitly targets.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The CAL model grounds the Nugget Nihongo design premise that voluntary, mobile, micro-session study with immediate applicability is not merely a UX preference but the empirically appropriate modality for adult learners facing situational constraints. The SSW learner's motivational profile — occupational advancement as primary driver — directly validates the platform's SSW-contextualised card content strategy.

---

**[AL-03]** Mezirow, J. (1991). *Transformative Dimensions of Adult Learning*. Jossey-Bass.

**Core contribution:** Mezirow argues that adult learning is not merely additive (acquiring new facts within existing frameworks) but can be *transformative* — involving fundamental shifts in the meaning perspectives through which learners interpret experience. Transformative learning is triggered by a "disorienting dilemma" that cannot be resolved by assimilating new information into existing schemas; the learner must revise or replace the meaning structure itself. For adult language learners, Mezirow's framework identifies a qualitatively distinct challenge: acquiring a new language in a professional migration context is not simply instrumental vocabulary acquisition but involves identity reorientation — the learner is not adding a skill but shifting into a new social persona. The process of becoming a competent Japanese speaker in an SSW workplace involves meaning perspective transformation around professional identity, social role, and linguistic self-concept. Mezirow's model connects directly to Dörnyei's L2 Motivational Self System (§8 MV-03+) as a related but distinct theoretical account — where L2MSS identifies the motivational dynamics, transformative learning theory describes the cognitive-interpretive process through which identity change occurs.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Transformative learning theory cautions against designing Nugget Nihongo as purely a vocabulary delivery system. Cards that frame Japanese workplace language within the learner's evolving professional identity — "you as an SSW worker using this phrase in a real care facility conversation" — are not rhetorical decoration but reflect how adult language acquisition actually works at the deeper level of meaning transformation.

**Design Implication:** Every card in Nugget Nihongo should carry an explicit relevance signal — a brief SSW-workplace contextualisation that answers the andragogical "why does this matter for me now?" question (AL-01). Cards without relevance signals are pedagogically designed objects inserted into an andragogically designed platform, producing a mismatch that undermines adult motivation. The combined AL-01/AL-02/AL-03 cluster establishes that this signal is not optional annotation but a structural requirement of adult instructional design.

### §17.2 — Self-Regulated Learning (SRL)

If andragogy describes the *characteristics* of the adult learner, Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) theory describes the *processes* by which effective adult learners manage their own cognition, motivation, and behavior over time. SRL is particularly relevant to Nugget Nihongo because the platform's SRS mechanism *externally implements* the spacing and retrieval cycle that expert SRL practitioners would ideally self-impose — but which most learners, including adults, are not equipped to implement without scaffolding.

---

**[AL-04]** Zimmerman, B. J. (2000). Attaining self-regulation: A social cognitive perspective. In M. Boekaerts, P. R. Pintrich, & M. Zeidner (Eds.), *Handbook of Self-Regulation* (pp. 13–39). Academic Press.

**Core contribution:** Zimmerman's chapter presents the definitive social cognitive framework for SRL as a cyclical, three-phase process. The *forethought phase* precedes learning and involves task analysis (goal-setting, strategic planning) and self-motivational beliefs (self-efficacy assessments, outcome expectations, intrinsic interest). The *performance phase* involves self-control mechanisms (self-instruction, attention focusing, task strategies) and self-observation (self-monitoring, self-recording). The *self-reflection phase* follows performance and involves self-judgment (self-evaluation against standards, causal attributions) and self-reaction (self-satisfaction or dissatisfaction, adaptive vs. defensive responses). Crucially, the model is cyclical: self-reflection feeds forward into forethought for the next learning episode. Zimmerman demonstrates empirically that high-achieving learners run this cycle continuously and flexibly, whereas struggling learners either skip phases (especially forethought) or respond to negative self-reflection with defensive rather than adaptive self-reactions. The social cognitive framing distinguishes SRL from metacognition: SRL encompasses motivational and behavioral self-regulation, not only cognitive monitoring.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The SRS review queue *is* an externally scaffolded performance phase: it sequences practice trials, monitors outcomes (card grading), and computes adaptive scheduling. But the forethought and self-reflection phases remain entirely with the learner. Platform features such as goal-setting onboarding, daily quota nudges (→ §10 HB-03/HB-04 implementation intentions), and post-session summaries are SRL scaffolding interventions targeting the forethought and self-reflection phases that SRS alone does not address.

---

**[AL-05]** Zimmerman, B. J. (2002). Becoming a self-regulated learner: An overview. *Theory Into Practice, 41*(2), 64–70. https://doi.org/10.1207/s15430421tip4102_2

**Core contribution:** This article provides the most accessible synthetic statement of Zimmerman's SRL model and is the primary citation for the three-phase cyclical framework. Zimmerman addresses a question directly relevant to adult learning contexts: can SRL be taught, or is it a stable individual difference? His evidence strongly supports teachability — SRL improves with guided practice, modelling, and feedback. Critically, Zimmerman distinguishes *self-regulated learners* (who use the full cycle proactively) from *socially regulated learners* (who rely on teachers or peers to initiate and monitor) and argues that most learners, including adults, fall into the social regulation category without explicit SRL instruction or scaffolding. This has a direct implication for platform design: a self-study tool cannot assume SRL-sophisticated users. The article also identifies self-efficacy (addressed in AL-07) as the motivational engine that enables the forethought phase — without belief in one's capacity to improve, learners do not set challenging goals or plan strategically.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The majority of Nugget Nihongo's SSW user base are unlikely to be SRL-sophisticated; they are socially regulated learners accustomed to classroom instruction. The platform therefore bears an obligation to substitute externally what expert SRL practitioners do internally — a function that SRS scheduling, session streak mechanics (→ §10 HB-06/HB-08), and goal-setting prompts collectively serve.

**[AL-06]** Pintrich, P. R. (2000). The role of goal orientation in self-regulated learning. In M. Boekaerts, P. R. Pintrich, & M. Zeidner (Eds.), *Handbook of Self-Regulation* (pp. 451–502). Academic Press.

**Core contribution:** Pintrich's chapter extends SRL theory to adult educational contexts by examining how goal orientation — the type of goal a learner pursues — shapes the quality of self-regulatory engagement. Learners operating under a *mastery goal orientation* (developing competence, learning for its own sake) engage in deeper cognitive processing, more adaptive attribution patterns, and more sustained self-regulatory effort than learners under a *performance goal orientation* (demonstrating competence relative to others, avoiding judgments of incompetence). This distinction differs from the SDT intrinsic/extrinsic motivation axis (§8 MV-01/MV-02) in that it concerns the internal standard against which performance is evaluated, not the locus of causality. Pintrich also specifically addresses adult SRL contexts, noting that adult learners typically differ from student learners in two respects: their goal structures are intrinsically mastery-oriented (competence for real-world use, not grades), but their time and energy budgets create situational pressures that can force superficial processing strategies even when mastery goals are present. The interaction between goal orientation and resource availability is therefore a central tension in adult SRL.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Pintrich's framework supports designing Nugget Nihongo's feedback mechanisms as mastery-referenced rather than comparative. Progress displays should communicate "you are improving" rather than "you rank here relative to other users." Competitive leaderboards (flagged as need-thwarting in §10 GE cluster) undermine mastery orientation; personal-best metrics and visible vocabulary growth curves reinforce it.

**[AL-07]** Bandura, A. (1997). *Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control*. W. H. Freeman.

**Core contribution:** Bandura's monograph presents the definitive empirical and theoretical account of self-efficacy — the belief in one's capacity to execute a specific course of action to produce a given outcome. Self-efficacy is domain- and task-specific, not a global self-esteem measure. Bandura identifies four sources: *mastery experiences* (prior success on the task), *vicarious modelling* (observing similar others succeed), *social persuasion* (verbal encouragement from credible others), and *physiological/affective states* (interpreting arousal as indicating competence or incompetence). Critically for SRL theory (AL-04, AL-05), self-efficacy is the prerequisite for the forethought phase: a learner who doubts their capacity to improve does not set challenging goals, does not plan strategically, and responds to failure with defensive rather than adaptive self-reactions. Low language-learning self-efficacy is therefore not merely a motivational problem but a structural barrier to SRL engagement — the regulatory cycle breaks at its initiating node.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The SRS card-grading interface is a real-time self-efficacy intervention. Each "Easy" or "Good" response is a mastery experience; each successful recall of a previously difficult item is evidence of improvement that can raise language-learning self-efficacy. Platform design choices that maximise early-session success rates — including appropriate initial card difficulty and generous early-review scheduling — are empirically grounded in Bandura's mastery-experience mechanism.

**Design Implication:** SRL scaffolding in Nugget Nihongo operates across all three phases: goal-setting prompts and daily quota selection support the forethought phase (AL-04/AL-05); the SRS review mechanism and session interface support the performance phase; post-session vocabulary-growth summaries and streak continuation feedback support the self-reflection phase. The platform should assume socially regulated users (AL-05) and be designed to progressively build SRL capacity over time rather than presupposing it. Mastery-oriented feedback (AL-06) and early success rate management (AL-07) are the two highest-leverage design levers for building the self-efficacy that makes genuine SRL engagement possible.

### §17.3 — Autonomy in Language Learning

The SDT autonomy-need addressed in §8 (MV-01/MV-02) is a *psychological need* for the experience of self-determination — the feeling that one's actions are self-initiated and volitionally endorsed. Learner autonomy as theorised in the language education literature is a related but distinct construct: it is a *capacity* — a learner's ability to take charge of their own learning — that develops through instruction, practice, and supportive conditions. The two constructs are consistent and mutually reinforcing, but conflating them produces design errors: SDT autonomy-need can be partially satisfied by giving learners choice within a constrained system; Benson/Holec language autonomy requires developing the metacognitive and strategic capacity to manage the entire learning enterprise.

---

**[AL-08]** Holec, H. (1981). *Autonomy and Foreign Language Learning*. Pergamon Press. (Council of Europe.)

**Core contribution:** Holec's monograph, produced for the Council of Europe's Modern Languages Project, is the founding text of the learner autonomy tradition in language education. Holec defines autonomous learning as "the ability to take charge of one's own learning" — specifically, the ability to determine objectives, define content and progression, select methods and techniques, monitor the acquisition process, and evaluate what has been acquired. This definition is deliberately comprehensive: autonomy is not merely choosing what to study but encompasses the full range of executive decisions that teachers typically make on learners' behalf. Holec is careful to distinguish autonomous learning as a *capacity* (which can be more or less developed) from self-instruction as a *mode* (studying without a teacher). A learner can be self-instructing without being autonomous (following a fixed published syllabus) and can be non-self-instructing but developing autonomy (within a classroom that systematically builds learner decision-making). This distinction has direct implications for self-study platform design: providing a self-instruction mode does not automatically develop learner autonomy unless the platform also supports learners in building the executive capacities Holec enumerates.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Nugget Nihongo currently provides self-instruction (a structured SRS deck with preset content) but does not yet fully support autonomy development. Features that scaffold learners toward Holec's five autonomous-learning capacities — particularly the ability to define content progression and select methods — would represent a principled development roadmap grounded in the foundational autonomy text.

---

**[AL-09]** Little, D. (2007). Language learner autonomy: Some fundamental considerations revisited. *Innovation in Language Learning and Teaching, 1*(1), 14–29. https://doi.org/10.2167/illt040.0

**Core contribution:** Little revisits the theoretical foundations of learner autonomy 25 years after Holec's landmark definition, incorporating subsequent SLA research to produce a more nuanced account. Little's key contribution is the argument that learner autonomy is not a fixed psychological trait but a *developing capacity* that is inherently co-constructed through social interaction — it cannot be delivered by giving learners choices, but must be fostered through a pedagogical relationship in which the instructor or platform progressively withdraws scaffolding as learner executive capacity develops. Little also integrates SLA findings to argue that authentic engagement with target language use — not just metalinguistic awareness of one's learning process — is constitutive of autonomy development. This positions autonomous language learning as both a process goal (the learner is progressively taking more control) and an outcome (the learner can sustain purposeful language study independently). Little's account bridges the SRL literature (§17.2) and the autonomy literature by identifying self-monitoring and goal-revision — Zimmerman's self-reflection phase — as central autonomy competencies.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Little's developmental framing supports a progressive-disclosure design philosophy for Nugget Nihongo: early learners receive a constrained, high-scaffold experience; intermediate learners receive increasing deck customisation options; advanced learners receive full editorial control over card selection and tag management. Releasing all customisation features at onboarding does not build autonomy — it overwhelms learners who have not yet developed the executive capacity to use it.

---

**[AL-10]** Benson, P. (2011). *Teaching and Researching Autonomy in Language Learning* (2nd ed.). Pearson/Longman. (Applied Linguistics in Action series.)

**Core contribution:** Benson's monograph is the field's definitive theoretical and methodological treatment, surveying 30 years of autonomy research across instructional contexts, learner populations, and methodological traditions. Benson identifies five versions of autonomy: technical (self-access learning), psychological (an internal capacity), political (the right to determine one's own educational ends), sociocultural (autonomy as co-constructed through collaborative practice), and cognitive (metacognitive control of learning processes). These five dimensions are not competing definitions but emphasise different aspects of a complex construct; effective autonomy-supporting design must address multiple dimensions simultaneously. Benson's most important contribution for instructional technology is his empirical review demonstrating that *none* of the five dimensions can be developed through the provision of self-study resources alone — all require pedagogical mediation, whether from human instructors, peer interaction, or designed scaffolding systems. He also explicitly addresses digital and CALL-based learning environments, making this the most applicable theoretical source for mobile SRS platform design in the autonomy tradition.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Benson's multi-dimensional framework establishes that learner autonomy is not reducible to "giving users choices in the settings menu." Genuine autonomy support requires addressing at minimum the technical dimension (self-access infrastructure ✓), psychological dimension (scaffolded executive capacity building — partially addressed), and cognitive dimension (metacognitive prompts, learning reflection features — largely not yet implemented). The AL-08/AL-09/AL-10 cluster together constitute the theoretical case for a Nugget Nihongo autonomy-development roadmap.

**[AL-11]** Dickinson, L. (1987). *Self-Instruction in Language Learning*. Cambridge University Press.

**Core contribution:** Dickinson's monograph addresses the practical conditions under which autonomous, self-instructed language learning is effective — providing empirical grounding for what Holec theorised abstractly. Dickinson distinguishes three modes of self-directed learning along a continuum of autonomy degree: *self-instruction* (learner manages logistics but follows externally set curriculum), *semi-autonomy* (learner negotiates goals and selects resources within a framework), and *full autonomy* (learner controls all executive dimensions). Crucially, Dickinson provides evidence that effective self-instruction requires three preconditions: adequate materials availability, sufficient metalinguistic awareness to monitor progress, and motivational stability to sustain effort without external social accountability. Each precondition has direct technological implications. Dickinson also identifies a key failure mode in self-instruction: *method rigidity* — learners who commit to a single study technique (e.g., vocabulary list review) and fail to adapt when it stops producing gains, often attributing plateau to personal inability rather than methodological mismatch.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Method rigidity (Dickinson's failure mode) is precisely the trap that SRS-only study risks: a learner who uses Nugget Nihongo exclusively without integrating listening, speaking, or reading practice may plateau and attribute the plateau to incapacity. The platform's design should explicitly position itself within a broader study ecosystem — framing SRS as vocabulary infrastructure rather than a complete learning solution — to prevent method-rigidity attribution errors that damage self-efficacy (AL-07).

**Design Implication:** Learner agency over deck customisation — card order, daily quota, topic selection, tag visibility — is not a UX preference or engagement feature. It is pedagogically grounded simultaneously in andragogical self-direction principles (AL-01), SRL forethought-phase scaffolding (AL-04/AL-05), and the autonomy-development literature (AL-08/AL-09/AL-10). The SDT autonomy-need construct (§8 MV-01) provides the motivational mechanism; the present AL cluster provides the pedagogical warrant. Critically, the developmental arc identified by Little (AL-09) and Benson (AL-10) requires that autonomy-supporting features be progressively introduced as learner capacity develops — not pre-loaded at onboarding — and that the platform include metacognitive scaffolding (Dickinson's AL-11 metalinguistic awareness precondition) rather than assuming it.

### §17.4 — Adult Language Learning Motivation

#### The Structural Distinctiveness of Adult Motivation

Adult language learning motivation is not merely an intensified or weakened version of the motivational profile observed in secondary or tertiary students — it is structurally different. The adult learner enters the L2 acquisition process with a formed social identity, a calculus of competing temporal demands (employment, family, community obligations), and a concrete purpose that either is or is not served by the language being acquired. These structural features combine to produce a motivational profile that standard classroom-based motivational frameworks underspecify.

The foundational taxonomic distinction in this domain is Gardner's (1985) bifurcation of motivational orientation into **instrumental** and **integrative** types. Instrumental motivation is defined by the desire to acquire the L2 for practical, utilitarian purposes — employment access, qualification attainment, economic advancement. Integrative motivation is defined by genuine interest in and identification with the target-language community and its culture. Gardner's Attitude/Motivation Test Battery (AMTB) was designed to operationalize both orientations and to assess their contribution to language achievement. His socio-educational model holds that integrative motivation predicts achievement more reliably than instrumental motivation, because it sustains engagement beyond the point at which instrumental goals are met or abandoned **[AL-14]**.

This finding has been widely replicated but also substantially qualified. The socio-educational model was developed with heritage-language and foreign-language classroom learners in North American bilingual contexts — a population for whom the target language community is physically proximate and cultural integration is a plausible aspiration. For learners who are geographically and culturally remote from the target community, Gardner's integrative orientation is phenomenologically thinner: it may manifest as a generalized positive affect toward Japanese culture rather than identification with a community the learner has never encountered. More critically, Gardner's framework must be distinguished from Dörnyei's L2 Motivational Self System (§8 MV-03+), which supersedes it in theoretical scope: L2MSS reconceptualises the integrative motive as the Ideal L2 Self — the learner's vision of themselves as a competent L2 user — decoupled from identification with any specific target-language community. Where Gardner's integrative orientation requires a referent community, the Ideal L2 Self requires only a sufficiently vivid and elaborated imagined identity. For SSW learners whose Japanese contact is pre-departure and mediated, the L2MSS framework is theoretically more applicable; Gardner's instrumental/integrative distinction remains useful as a first-order classification but should be treated as an antecedent framework, not a current theoretical claim (§8 MV-03+ cross-reference).

**[AL-14]** Gardner, R. C. (1985). *Social psychology and second language learning: The role of attitudes and motivation.* Edward Arnold. No DOI (monograph).

- **Core contribution:** Gardner's socio-educational model is the foundational empirical and theoretical treatment of motivation in SLA. The model establishes instrumental motivation (L2 as instrumental means) and integrative motivation (L2 as vehicle of community identification) as distinct constructs with different predictive relationships to long-term achievement. The AMTB operationalizes both constructs and allows empirical discrimination between them. Gardner's central finding — that integrative motivation predicts achievement more robustly, particularly over longer learning trajectories — established the research consensus that instrumental motivation is less sustaining than intrinsic community-oriented motivation. The model also introduces the construct of **language anxiety** as a mediating variable, anticipating the FLCA research cluster (§13 ID-01–ID-07 cross-reference). Gardner's theoretical framework predates and is more limited than Dörnyei's L2MSS (§8 MV-03+), which reconceptualizes the integrative motive as the Ideal L2 Self; §17.4 uses Gardner as the historical anchor and L2MSS as the current framework.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The SSW learner profile is instrumentally dominant (visa eligibility, employment access) with typically low intrinsic or integrative motivation at entry. Gardner's finding that instrumental motivation sustains engagement through short-term milestones but tends to attrit after qualification attainment is directly relevant to platform retention design: Nugget Nihongo must build intrinsic motivation structures (cultural content, cultural curiosity) alongside the instrumental JFT-Basic/N4 preparation track, or risk attrition after the qualifying examination is passed. The AL-14 framework also justifies framing the 日本の文化 deck as motivational infrastructure, not supplementary content.

#### Learner Strategies and the Adult Motivational Plateau

Wenden (1991) offers the most comprehensive treatment available of **learner strategy use in adult language learning** — that is, the cognitive, metacognitive, and social-affective behaviors adult learners deploy to manage their own learning process. Wenden's central argument is that adult learners are not passive recipients of instruction but active agents who develop beliefs about language learning, form hypotheses about learning effectiveness, and deploy deliberate strategies in response to perceived progress and difficulty. The adult learner's motivational profile is inseparable from their strategy repertoire: motivation shapes strategy selection, and strategy outcomes (success, failure, plateau) feed back into motivational state **[AL-15]**.

The most consequential insight Wenden develops for adult self-directed learning contexts is the analysis of **plateau abandonment** — the tendency of adult learners to disengage at the first significant learning plateau rather than modifying strategy. This pattern is structurally different from child or adolescent learning failure: adult learners typically have meta-awareness of the plateau (they can identify that progress has stalled), but their response is more likely to be withdrawal than strategic adaptation. Wenden attributes this to the intersection of three adult-specific factors: (1) **opportunity cost consciousness** — adults are acutely aware that study time competes with employment, family, and rest; (2) **outcome-referenced self-evaluation** — adults evaluate progress against concrete external benchmarks (test scores, comprehension tasks) rather than abstract proficiency gain, making slow improvement near a plateau highly visible; and (3) **incomplete metacognitive toolkit** — many adult self-directed learners have not developed the strategy-switching behaviors required to move through a plateau, particularly for skills (like phonology or reading fluency) where improvement is slow and non-linear.

**[AL-15]** Wenden, A. (1991). *Learner strategies for learner autonomy: Planning and implementing learner training for language learners.* Prentice Hall. No DOI (monograph).

- **Core contribution:** Wenden's monograph is the foundational treatment of metacognitive strategy use in adult language learners. It distinguishes cognitive strategies (mnemonics, pattern analysis, vocabulary mapping) from metacognitive strategies (planning, monitoring, evaluating) and argues that adult learners systematically over-rely on cognitive strategies while under-developing metacognitive regulation. The book introduces the concept of learner beliefs about language learning as a determinant of strategy selection — beliefs that are often counterproductive (e.g., "vocabulary must be memorized in isolation"; "grammar rules must be internalized before speaking") and resistant to change without explicit metalinguistic intervention. Wenden's account of plateau abandonment — the adult learner's tendency to disengage rather than adapt strategy when progress stalls — is the most theoretically explicit treatment available of the specific adult learning breakdown pattern. The framework also anticipates SRL theory (§17.1–§17.3 cross-reference, AL-01–AL-13) by framing metacognitive strategy use as a precondition for autonomous adult learning.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The FSRS review curve creates predictable plateau experiences — particularly in the Early Mature phase (cards entering long intervals) where subjective recall certainty is high but long-term retention is not yet consolidated. Wenden's analysis implies that the platform must provide metacognitive scaffolding at these points: progress visualizations that make interval progression legible as improvement (not stagnation), explicit explanations of the spacing curve rationale, and strategic guidance for learners who find that their "easy" cards are being encountered too infrequently. The SSW learner's high opportunity-cost consciousness (20–40 minutes/day in fragmented sessions; §17.6) makes plateau disengagement a primary retention risk.

#### Sustaining Motivation Under Instrumental Pressure: The SSW Challenge

The specific challenge for SSW learner motivation is the combination of high instrumental stakes with low intrinsic baseline. The JFT-Basic or JLPT N4 qualification is a non-negotiable visa prerequisite — the stakes could not be more concrete. Yet the language being acquired is functionally distant from the learner's current life context (they are studying Japanese while living in Indonesia, with limited authentic exposure), and the intrinsic dimension of integrative motivation is structurally thin (the target community is not accessible pre-departure). This configuration — high extrinsic pressure, low intrinsic pull — is the motivationally least sustainable profile in Gardner's framework and in SDT theory (§8 MV-01–MV-02 cross-reference).

Crookes and Schmidt's (1991) reframing of motivation research is particularly useful here. Arguing that the Gardner tradition had operationalized motivation almost exclusively at the level of **attitude** (relatively stable orientations toward the L2 and its community) rather than at the level of **behavior** (what learners actually do, persistently, to advance their L2 competence), Crookes and Schmidt proposed a four-level model of motivation that incorporates interest, relevance, expectancy, and satisfaction as operationalizable, intervenable constructs. Crucially, they argue that even learners with low integrative attitude can sustain motivated behavior if task design maintains **relevance** (the task connects to a goal the learner holds) and **expectancy** (the learner believes effort will produce success) — both of which are designable rather than dispositional **[AL-16]**.

**[AL-16]** Crookes, G., & Schmidt, R. W. (1991). Motivation: Reopening the research agenda. *Language Learning, 41*(4), 469–512. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-1770.1991.tb00690.x

- **Core contribution:** Crookes and Schmidt's article is the pivotal intervention that shifted SLA motivation research from attitude-level constructs (Gardner) toward behavioral and pedagogical operationalization. Their four-level model (micro: task interest and attention; classroom: relevance, expectancy, satisfaction; syllabus: goal clarity and variety; outside-class: intrinsic motivation development) reconnects SLA motivation theory with educational psychology and instructional design. The article argues that motivation is not merely a precondition for learning but a product of well-designed learning environments — making motivation a designable outcome rather than an input variable. Their relevance–expectancy–satisfaction triad anticipates Keller's ARCS model and is directly applicable to mobile learning interface design. The article also introduces the concept of motivational fluctuation across the learning trajectory, distinguishing initial motivation from sustained maintenance motivation from reactivation after attrition — a tripartite structure directly relevant to SSW learner engagement over the multi-month JFT-Basic preparation period.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The relevance–expectancy–satisfaction triad maps directly onto three designable platform features: (1) **relevance** — SSW deck card content grounded in authentic workplace contexts (§15 VS-01–VS-04 cross-reference) ensures that every card reviewed is perceptibly connected to the learner's instrumental goal; (2) **expectancy** — FSRS interval progression and progress statistics make improvement visible, maintaining the learner's belief that effort produces advancement; (3) **satisfaction** — the 日本の文化 deck provides cultural reward alongside instrumental drilling, building cumulative positive affect toward Japanese. The Crookes–Schmidt framework justifies the full deck architecture, not just the SSW component.

### §17.5 — Language Ego and Adult L2 Identity

#### The Language Ego Construct: Foundations

The concept of **language ego** was introduced by Guiora and colleagues in a series of studies examining the relationship between ego structure, empathy capacity, and second language phonological approximation. The foundational paper, Guiora, Brannon, and Dull (1972), reports an experimental finding of striking theoretical consequence: individuals who score higher on a psychodramatic measure of empathy — their capacity to temporarily inhabit another's perspective — produce more native-like L2 phonological approximations. Guiora et al.'s interpretation of this finding is that L2 phonological production requires a temporary **relaxation of ego boundaries**: the learner must be willing to sound unlike themselves, to inhabit a sound-system that encodes a foreign identity, and to expose that tentative identity to social evaluation. Empathy capacity indexes the ease with which the individual can relax ego boundaries in this way **[AL-17]**.

The theoretical implication developed across Guiora's subsequent work is that the self-concept — the adult speaker's integrated sense of who they are, expressed through language — is not merely a psychological correlate of L1 competence but is partly *constituted* by the phonological, prosodic, and pragmatic patterns of the first language. The L1 is not simply a communication tool that the adult has learned to use well; it is, for adults, an identity substrate. This means that L2 acquisition at the adult stage is not merely a cognitive-linguistic challenge but an **identity challenge**: to produce authentic L2 phonology, the adult must temporarily decenter from their L1-grounded self-concept, and this decentering is ego-costly in ways it is not for children, whose identity is still forming and whose ego boundaries are less crystallized.

**[AL-17]** Guiora, A. Z., Brannon, R. C. I., & Dull, C. Y. (1972). Empathy and second language learning. *Language Learning, 22*(1), 111–130. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-1770.1972.tb00069.x

- **Core contribution:** Guiora et al. (1972) is the foundational empirical paper introducing the language ego construct and the language ego permeability hypothesis. The paper reports an experimental demonstration that empathy — operationalized through a standardized psychodramatic measure — significantly predicts the quality of L2 phonological approximation in adult learners. The interpretation is that adult L2 phonological production requires temporary relaxation of ego boundaries (ego permeability), and that individuals who are dispositionally more permeable achieve better phonological outcomes. The paper establishes the core theoretical claim that runs through §17.5: adult phonological resistance is not primarily a cognitive–phonological deficit (though CPH effects are real; §13 ID-22–ID-28 cross-reference) but an **identity-protective behavior** — the adult resists sounding foreign because sounding foreign is ego-threatening in a way it is not for children. This reframing has direct implications for instruction and feedback design.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The language ego permeability hypothesis reframes adult phonological resistance as a design challenge rather than a cognitive inevitability. The platform's audio card format — where learners hear native Japanese pronunciation and are expected to internalize mora timing, pitch accent, and vowel devoicing patterns (§11 PH-01–PH-07 cross-reference) — encounters the ego permeability barrier directly. SSW learners reviewing 介護 vocabulary must not only perceive the phonological targets but be willing to approximate them in their inner rehearsal and any production practice. Low-ego-threat card design strategies are therefore phonologically consequential, not merely affectively palliative.

#### Ego Permeability and Adult Phonological Resistance

Schumann's (1975) analysis of affective factors in adult SLA provides the most direct theoretical bridge between Guiora's language ego construct and the observable adult phonological resistance pattern. Schumann argues that adult learners' **social distance** from the target community and **psychological distance** from the target-language identity combine to reinforce ego boundaries, making phonological approximation socially costly even for learners who are cognitively aware of their accent. The critical distinction Schumann draws is between **acculturation failure** — where the learner does not wish to approximate the target culture — and **ego threat avoidance** — where the learner actively perceives native-like phonological production as a threat to their existing identity, regardless of cultural attitude. Adult learners who report strongly positive attitudes toward Japan may nonetheless exhibit strong phonological resistance because native-like Japanese pronunciation is perceived as entailing the abandonment or dilution of their L1 identity **[AL-18]**.

This mechanism has a specific implication for adult Indonesian learners of Japanese. Indonesian phonology (§5 CP cluster cross-reference for Indonesian–Japanese contrastive analysis) lacks pitch accent, has a five-vowel system without vowel devoicing, and has syllable-timed rhythm rather than mora-timed rhythm. The phonological adjustments required to approximate Japanese — mora timing, high vowel devoicing, pitch accent discrimination — are not only perceptually difficult (CPH effects; §13 ID-22–ID-28 cross-reference) but also identity-marking: a learner who successfully approximates Japanese mora timing and devoices vowels in the appropriate phonological environments sounds different, and this difference is audible to Indonesian interlocutors as well as Japanese ones. The ego threat is thus bidirectional: the learner risks both failing to sound Japanese (to Japanese interlocutors) and sounding strange (to Indonesian interlocutors and self-monitors). This double ego-threat architecture is specific to adult learners and contributes to the observed resistance.

**[AL-18]** Schumann, J. H. (1975). Affective factors and the problem of age in second language acquisition. *Language Learning, 25*(2), 209–235. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-1770.1975.tb00242.x

- **Core contribution:** Schumann's paper connects Guiora's language ego concept to the broader affective and social dimensions of adult L2 learning. It introduces the distinction between acculturation failure (unwillingness to approximate the target culture) and ego-threat avoidance (perception of target-like production as identity-threatening), arguing that many adult learners exhibit phonological fossilization not because they cannot perceive the L2 targets (cognitive hypothesis) but because they actively resist producing them (identity-protection hypothesis). The paper also introduces the concept of psychological distance as a variable moderating adult phonological approximation — a construct that maps onto the L2MSS (§8 MV-03+) notion of the Ideal L2 Self: learners with a well-elaborated, positively valenced Ideal L2 Self have reduced psychological distance and show less ego-protective resistance. Schumann's framework thus creates a direct theoretical connection between motivation (§17.4) and phonological acquisition (§11 PH-01–PH-07 cross-reference), mediated by ego permeability.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The platform's audio-inclusive card format must contend with ego-protective avoidance as a real behavioral risk. Learners who skip audio replay, reduce inner rehearsal effort on phonologically demanding items, or habitually mark mora-timing errors as "known" rather than engaging with them may be exhibiting ego-protective behavior rather than genuine mastery. FSRS button behavior (Again, Hard, Good, Easy) does not capture this mechanism directly. A design implication is that audio cards should be systematically separated from reading/meaning cards in the review queue and tracked independently — enabling the platform to detect patterns where audio review is systematically underperformed relative to orthographic review on the same vocabulary items.

#### Ego Permeability, Individual Differences, and Feedback Design

Ehrman (1999) develops the most practically applicable model of ego boundaries in adult L2 learning. Drawing on Hartmann's (1991) boundary strength continuum, Ehrman argues that learners vary dispositionally in the **rigidity versus permeability** of their ego boundaries — that this variation is relatively stable across contexts, and that it moderates how adult learners respond to L2 instruction and feedback. Thin-boundaried learners (high permeability) are more comfortable with the ambiguity and identity flexibility that L2 acquisition requires; they tolerate phonological approximation, accept interim forms without distress, and engage more readily with the emotional content of authentic L2 texts. Thick-boundaried learners (low permeability) require more structured, predictable, controlled learning environments; they respond poorly to open-ended production tasks, experience visible error as ego-threatening, and tend toward over-reliance on metalinguistic analysis as a way of maintaining cognitive control over a process that otherwise feels personally destabilizing **[AL-19]**.

**[AL-19]** Ehrman, M. E. (1999). Ego boundaries revisited: Toward a model of personality and learning. In J. Arnold (Ed.), *Affect in language learning* (pp. 68–86). Cambridge University Press. No DOI (book chapter).

- **Core contribution:** Ehrman's chapter extends the Guiora language ego construct into a practical individual difference variable using Hartmann's boundary strength continuum. The chapter argues that ego boundary permeability is a stable learner characteristic that moderates response to instruction, feedback, and error exposure. Thick-boundaried learners — the majority of adult learners in high-stakes, performance-accountable contexts — exhibit systematic avoidance of production situations where error is likely and visible, prefer deductive over inductive instruction, and show greater sensitivity to explicit failure feedback. Ehrman's framework predicts the specific pattern most likely in SSW learners: high instrumental motivation co-occurring with thick ego boundaries, producing a profile where the learner drills vocabulary intensively but avoids production, preferring recognition tasks (identifying a kana or reading a word) over recall tasks (producing a form or approximating a pronunciation). This profile is characteristically under-served by card designs that do not differentiate recognition from recall.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The recognition/recall distinction in Nugget Nihongo card design (reading-only vs. audio-forward cards; passive recognition vs. active production prompts) maps directly onto Ehrman's boundary permeability typology. Thick-boundaried SSW learners will systematically overperform on receptive cards and underperform on productive cards. Card sequence design should therefore interleave recognition and recall reviews rather than allowing the learner to self-select review mode, preventing the thick-boundaried learner from consolidating a recognition-only learning strategy that will fail in authentic workplace communication.

#### Language Ego and the FSRS Failure Feedback Problem

Arnold and Brown's (1999) comprehensive survey of affect in language learning synthesizes the theoretical terrain connecting language ego, affective filter theory (cross-reference §1 CI-01: Krashen input hypothesis), anxiety (§13 ID-01–ID-07 cross-reference), and feedback design. Their central practical argument is that **explicit failure feedback is the highest-threat event in adult L2 learning**: it activates the ego-protection mechanism (Guiora, AL-17), raises language anxiety (§13 ID-01), and reduces the available working memory capacity for L2 processing (§13 ID-02, MacIntyre & Gardner; §13 ID-15–ID-21 WM cluster cross-reference). The design implication is not that failure feedback should be eliminated — it is functionally essential for FSRS calibration and for Noticing (§12 OT-06 cross-reference) — but that its presentation must be carefully managed to minimize ego activation while maximizing learning signal **[AL-20]**.

**[AL-20]** Arnold, J., & Brown, H. D. (1999). A map of the terrain. In J. Arnold (Ed.), *Affect in language learning* (pp. 1–24). Cambridge University Press. No DOI (book chapter).

- **Core contribution:** Arnold and Brown's chapter provides the most comprehensive synthesis available of affective variables in language learning — anxiety, ego, self-esteem, inhibition, risk-taking, empathy, and motivation — and their interaction with instructional feedback. The chapter explicitly argues that the highest-threat affective event in L2 learning is **public error exposure** and that even private error exposure (seeing a wrong answer confirmed as wrong) activates ego-protective responses when the learner has strong investment in their L2 competence. The chapter proposes three design principles for reducing ego threat in feedback contexts: (1) **normalization of error** — presenting error as a necessary and expected component of the learning process rather than a deviation from expected competence; (2) **attributional framing** — linking error to the learning system (the item was scheduled too early; the interval was too aggressive) rather than to the learner's ability; (3) **incremental mastery tracking** — making cumulative progress visible alongside per-item failure, so that a single "Again" rating does not dominate the learner's self-assessment.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** FSRS's "Again" button is, from a language ego perspective, the highest-risk element of the review interface. For SSW learners with thick ego boundaries (AL-19), the "Again" outcome — particularly on items that the learner expected to remember, or on items that have been reviewed many times — activates ego-threat and may trigger avoidance behavior (session abandonment, review delay, marking "Hard" as "Good" to avoid explicit failure). The three Arnold-Brown principles translate directly into interface design: (1) onboarding should explicitly normalize "Again" as the system working as intended; (2) the "Again" animation/feedback should be neutral and process-attributional, not evaluative; (3) the statistics dashboard should lead with retention trajectory (cumulative mature cards, total minutes studied) before per-session accuracy metrics. Cross-reference §1.11 FSRS for algorithm-level design decisions — do NOT re-cite §1 FSRS sources.

### §17.6 — The SSW Worker Learner Profile: A Synthesis

#### Introduction: Toward a Research-Grounded Learner Profile

The preceding subsections of §17 — §17.1–§17.3 (andragogy, SRL, and autonomy; AL-01–AL-13) and §17.4–§17.5 (adult motivation, language ego; AL-14–AL-20 above) — have built a theoretical framework for adult language learning applicable to self-directed contexts. This capstone subsection draws on that framework, together with §15 (SSW and vocational Japanese context; VS-01–VS-28), §13 (individual differences; ID-01–ID-43), §11 (phonology; PH-01–PH-44), §14 (sociolinguistics and keigo; SC-01–SC-28), and §8 (platform and motivation; MV-01–MV-03+) to construct a **concrete, research-grounded learner profile** for Nugget Nihongo's primary user — the Indonesian SSW candidate preparing for JFT-Basic or JLPT N4 while targeting placement in a Japanese care, food service, or construction sector.

Adult learning theory since Mezirow (1991) has emphasized that the most powerful driver of adult learning engagement is not merely relevance (Knowles' andragogic principle, AL-01–AL-03 cross-reference) but **transformative perspective**: adults invest most deeply in learning when they can see the learning as part of a fundamental life reorientation — a change in who they are and what futures are available to them. The SSW candidate's learning context is archetypal in this respect: the Japanese language is not being acquired as an academic exercise or a cultural enrichment activity but as a **gateway to a different life trajectory** — employment in Japan, remittances, family mobility, professional identity. The design of Nugget Nihongo must honor this transformative stakes dimension: the platform is not a gamified vocabulary trainer but a preparation tool for a life transition **[AL-21]**.

**[AL-21]** Mezirow, J. (1991). *Transformative dimensions of adult learning.* Jossey-Bass. No DOI (monograph).

- **Core contribution:** Mezirow's monograph is the foundational text of transformative learning theory — the adult education framework that explains why and how adults commit to deep, effortful learning in the absence of external institutional enforcement. Mezirow argues that adult learning is most durably motivated when it is connected to a **disorienting dilemma** — a life situation that challenges the learner's existing frame of reference and requires perspective transformation to resolve. The SSW candidate's situation is a paradigmatic disorienting dilemma: they face a labor market and life trajectory that requires Japanese language competence, and the acquisition of that competence will transform their practical identity (worker, migrant, professional). Mezirow's framework explains why the SSW learner's instrumental motivation, though fragile by Gardner's analysis (AL-14), has an emotionally deep and life-committal quality that is absent from much classroom language learning — and which, if activated by platform design, can sustain engagement through the most demanding phases of the learning curve.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Platform onboarding, progress messaging, and milestone celebrations should explicitly acknowledge the transformative stakes dimension of the SSW learner's investment. Framing card review completions as steps toward the JFT-Basic pass, deck completion milestones as competence thresholds, and 日本の文化 cultural content as preparation for authentic life in Japan — rather than abstract cultural enrichment — activates the transformative motivation Mezirow describes. This framing is not marketing; it is a pedagogically grounded design decision rooted in adult motivation theory.

#### Autonomy, Motivation, and the SSW Learner's Self-Regulatory Capacity

Ushioda's (2011) synthesis of motivational and autonomy research provides the most direct theoretical bridge between the SRL/autonomy cluster (AL-01–AL-13 cross-reference) and the motivation cluster (AL-14–AL-16) for adult self-directed learners. Ushioda argues that autonomy and motivation are not independent constructs operating in parallel but are **co-constitutive**: learners who experience greater agency in their learning process develop more sustaining intrinsic motivation, while learners with stronger intrinsic motivation are more willing to exercise the effortful self-regulation that autonomy requires. For adult learners in high-stakes self-directed contexts, this co-constitution creates a potential virtuous cycle (autonomy → motivation → greater autonomy) or a vicious cycle (low autonomy → extrinsic-only motivation → abandonment when external pressure recedes) **[AL-22]**.

**[AL-22]** Ushioda, E. (2011). Why autonomy? Insights from motivation theory and research. *Innovation in Language Learning and Teaching, 5*(2), 221–232. https://doi.org/10.1080/17501229.2011.577536

- **Core contribution:** Ushioda's article synthesizes SRL, SDT, and autonomy research to argue that self-directed adult learning is most durable when the learner experiences **perceived internal locus of causality** (ILOC) — the sense that their learning behavior is self-authored rather than externally compelled. The article distinguishes between **controlled self-regulation** (the learner studies because of guilt, anxiety, or external pressure) and **autonomous self-regulation** (the learner studies because of genuine engagement with the material and process), arguing that only autonomous self-regulation is reliably sustaining across the multi-month trajectory of adult language study. For SSW learners, whose initial motivation is almost entirely extrinsic (visa requirement), the design challenge is to create the conditions under which autonomous self-regulation can emerge — through interest activation, competence experience, and meaningful choice within the learning process.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The distinction between controlled and autonomous self-regulation maps directly onto FSRS study behavior patterns. A learner in controlled self-regulation mode studies to reduce anxiety about the JFT-Basic deadline and stops when the deadline pressure lifts; a learner who has shifted to autonomous self-regulation continues because they are genuinely engaged with the material and the process. Platform design can facilitate this shift through: (1) **interest-activation** via the 日本の文化 deck, which rewards exploration beyond the instrumental core; (2) **competence signaling** through visible retention statistics that confirm growing mastery; (3) **choice provision** through sub-deck selection that allows the learner to study their target SSW sector first, building perceived relevance from session start.

#### The SSW Worker Learner Profile: Dimensions

Drawing on the full §17 framework (AL-01–AL-22), §13 (ID-01–ID-43), §15 (VS-01–VS-28), §11 (PH-01–PH-44), §14 (SC-01–SC-28), and §8 (MV/CC/MALL clusters), the following integrated learner profile specifies Nugget Nihongo's primary user across the dimensions most consequential for platform design.

**Age range (22–35):** The typical SSW applicant is a young adult in the early-to-mid career stage. This places them in a developmental phase characterized by high risk tolerance alongside significant responsibility load — adult autonomy is established, but family and economic obligations are forming. Critical period effects (§13 ID-22–ID-28) are in full force for implicit phonological acquisition; explicit learning capacity is at its peak. The andragogic profile (AL-01–AL-13 cross-reference) is fully activated: goal orientation, relevance demand, and experience filter are all structurally present.

**Prior Japanese exposure (JLPT N5 passive familiarity):** Indonesian secondary school Japanese electives produce a learner who has encountered hiragana, katakana, and basic phrases, but typically without productive competence or systematic grammar internalization. The prior learning is predominantly recognition-level vocabulary and script familiarity — which means the learner arrives with some scaffolding for kana acquisition but limited syntactic or phonological foundation. The contrastive linguistics gap between Indonesian and Japanese is structurally large (§5 CP cluster cross-reference): agglutinative morphology, SOV word order, topic-marking particles, evidentiality, and the full keigo system (§14 SC cross-reference) are all L1-absent features requiring explicit instruction.

**Time available (20–40 minutes/day in fragmented sessions):** This is the design constraint that most severely limits pedagogical ambition. The 20–40 minute estimate is drawn from the SSW candidate's typical daily structure: full-time employment or education, plus household responsibilities, leaves short blocks available during commute (bus/transit), lunch, and pre-sleep. Sessions are rarely contiguous; a 30-minute study day may consist of three 10-minute blocks. FSRS's review-queue model is well-adapted to this fragmented structure — the system delivers exactly the due cards without requiring session continuity — but new card introduction must be calibrated to fragmented session realities. Working memory limitations (§13 ID-15–ID-21 cross-reference) are particularly acute in fragmented sessions: each re-engagement requires context re-establishment, reducing effective encoding time per session.

**Device (mobile-first, AnkiDroid on Android, low-end device):** The platform must perform on Android hardware at the sub-mid-range price point (approximately IDR 1.5–2.5 million at time of writing, equivalent to approximately Rp 1.5M–2.5M or USD 90–150). This means constrained RAM, limited local storage, and variable processing speed. Audio files must be compressed without perceptible quality loss; images must be optimized; JavaScript execution (for the PWA layer; §8 MALL cluster cross-reference) must be lean. AnkiDroid's native performance on low-end Android hardware is the primary constraint on card format complexity.

**Motivational profile (instrumental-dominant, high stakes, moderate anxiety):** By Gardner (AL-14), the SSW learner is prototypically instrumentally motivated — Japan is not the aspired cultural identity but the economic opportunity. By Crookes and Schmidt (AL-16), this is a sustaining motivation profile if and only if relevance and expectancy are maintained — which requires active design. By SDT (§8 MV-01–MV-02 cross-reference), the learner is likely in controlled self-regulation rather than autonomous self-regulation at entry; the platform's task is to support the transition toward autonomy as described by Ushioda (AL-22). FLCA (§13 ID-01–ID-07 cross-reference) is moderate: the stakes are high and the performance consequences real, but the learning environment is private (no public performance) and self-paced, both of which are FLCA-reducing conditions. The primary anxiety vector is test anxiety (JLPT/JFT-Basic deadline) rather than communication apprehension.

**Andragogic profile (goal-oriented, relevance-required, experience filter):** Consistent with Knowles' andragogic model (AL-01–AL-05 cross-reference) and Wenden's strategy analysis (AL-15), the SSW learner is a goal-oriented adult who evaluates every learning activity against its perceived contribution to the JFT-Basic/N4 goal. Cards that cannot be clearly connected to an authentic SSW use case or examination target will be perceived as irrelevant and are at risk of being abandoned or deprioritized. The experience filter — Knowles' principle that adult learners interpret new information through the lens of existing experience — manifests in the SSW learner's tendency to evaluate Japanese vocabulary through Indonesian/Malay semantic equivalents, which creates interference in areas of false cognacy and semantic mismatch.

**Keigo challenge (zero L1 equivalent):** Indonesian has no morphological politeness system equivalent to Japanese keigo (§14 SC cross-reference). The SSW learner must acquire a sociolinguistic system — the ます/です formal register as a minimum, with exposure to 丁寧語 and 敬語 in care sector contexts — that has no L1 scaffolding whatsoever. This is not merely a vocabulary gap but a pragmatic competence gap: keigo is not a set of substitutable words but a systematic encoding of social relationship into grammar, requiring the learner to monitor and adjust their utterances based on social context in ways that Indonesian communication does not require. The care sector's requirement for JLPT N3 progression (§15 VS-06 cross-reference) is partly driven by the fact that authentic care communication demands deployed keigo competence that N4-level learners have not consolidated.

**Phonological challenge (mora timing, pitch accent, vowel devoicing):** Post-critical-period phonological acquisition barriers (§13 ID-22–ID-28 cross-reference) are compounded by ego-threat avoidance (AL-17–AL-18) and the specific Indonesian–Japanese contrastive profile (§5 CP cross-reference; §11 PH-01–PH-07 cross-reference). HVPT training protocols (§11 PH-30–PH-39 cross-reference) are the evidence-based response to post-CPH phonological acquisition difficulty; their integration into Nugget Nihongo's audio card format is theoretically justified by the full combined framework of §13 and §17.5.

**SRL capacity (variable — scaffolding required):** Not all SSW applicants enter the platform as experienced self-directed learners. The SRL literature (§17.1–§17.3 cross-reference, AL-01–AL-13) documents substantial individual variation in metacognitive capacity, strategy repertoire, and goal-setting sophistication — and this variation is not correlated with instrumental motivation intensity. A highly motivated SSW candidate with weak SRL capacity will engage intensively but inefficiently, studying in high-frequency short bursts on easy items, avoiding difficult items, and failing to develop the spaced practice habits that FSRS requires. Platform design must scaffold SRL entry: explicit guidance on FSRS mechanics, recommended daily new card limits, and session-length guidance calibrated to the 20–40 minute availability window.
