## §9.1 — DUAL CODING THEORY (PAIVIO)

### Core Theoretical Background

Dual Coding Theory (DCT), introduced by Allan Paivio in 1971 and substantially revised in 1986, proposes that human cognition operates via two independent but interconnected representational systems: a **verbal system** (language-based, sequential, symbolic) and a **nonverbal/imagistic system** (image-based, synchronous, analog). Both systems can encode, store, and retrieve information, and they interact through **referential connections** — the ability to activate one system from the other (e.g., the word "elephant" activates both the verbal label and a mental image of an elephant).

The foundational learning prediction of DCT is the **dual coding advantage**: information encoded in both systems simultaneously produces more durable memory traces than information encoded in only one system. This is not merely additive — DCT predicts that the two traces are partially independent, so even if one is partially degraded at retrieval, the other may remain accessible. This predicts better **recall** (productive retrieval) as well as better **recognition** (discriminative retrieval).

For vocabulary learning, DCT generates five specific, testable predictions:

1. **Concrete > abstract words**: Concrete nouns (犬, 山, 電車) have high imageability — they reliably activate a nonverbal representation when encountered. Abstract nouns and function words (実は, とにかく, 相変わらず) have lower imageability — they activate primarily verbal representations. DCT predicts concrete words are learned and retained faster, a prediction confirmed empirically across dozens of studies (Paivio et al., 1994; de Groot, 2006).

2. **Imagery value as predictor**: Imagery value (how easily a word evokes a mental image, measured via rating scales) is a stronger predictor of vocabulary retention than word frequency, word length, or even L2 familiarity (Paivio & Desrochers, 1981; Sadoski et al., 1993). This has direct implications for which vocabulary items need visual support on Nugget Nihongo cards.

3. **Picture superiority effect**: Memory for pictures is consistently superior to memory for words (the "picture superiority effect"; Nelson et al., 1976). In vocabulary learning, words learned with pictures are retained better than words learned with definitions alone (Carpenter & Olson, 2012).

4. **Dual-trace redundancy at retrieval**: Because verbal and nonverbal traces are partially independent, recalling a word via the image cue is a distinct retrieval route from recalling it via the verbal cue. SRS cards that prompt retrieval via the image (show image, produce Japanese word) exercise a different route than standard L1→L2 cards.

5. **Imageability and L2 mental lexicon**: Paivio (2010) specifically applies DCT to the mental lexicon, arguing that L2 words achieve fluency when they develop direct nonverbal connections (concept-mediated) rather than remaining mediated through L1 verbal translations. The pedagogical implication: image-word pairing in early stages accelerates the transition to concept-level L2 processing.

### Extended DCT: Embodied Cognition Connections

More recent work by Barsalou (2008) in the tradition of embodied/grounded cognition shares significant overlap with DCT: both systems predict that perceptual and motor simulations support conceptual representation. For action verbs (走る, 食べる, 書く), motor imagery scaffolding (imagining performing the action) constitutes a nonverbal trace analogous to DCT's imagistic system. This extends the dual coding framework to verb vocabulary, not just concrete nouns.

### Design Implications for Nugget Nihongo (§9.1)

*Catatan untuk Crispy — dalam Bahasa Indonesia:*

**Implikasi 1 (Kartu konkret vs. abstrak):** Setiap kartu Juicy harus diklasifikasikan berdasarkan **imageability** target word-nya. **Implikasi 2 (Dua jalur retrieval):** Tambahkan card type dengan front berupa gambar, back berupa kata Jepang (image-cued recall) sebagai pelengkap standard L1→L2 card. Ini bukan duplikasi — ini melatih retrieval route yang berbeda secara neurologis.

**Implikasi 3 (Imageability sebagai kriteria Juicy):** Saat Juicy agent memilih mnemonic, imageability score harus menjadi kriteria eksplisit. Mnemonic yang menghasilkan mental image yang vivid dan konkret > mnemonic verbal yang abstrak.

**Implikasi 4 (Verb cards):** Kata kerja aksi (走る, 食べる) → pertimbangkan prompt "bayangkan Anda sedang melakukan aksi ini" di back card. Groundkan verb ke motor simulation, bukan hanya definisi.

## §9.2 — MAYER'S COGNITIVE THEORY OF MULTIMEDIA LEARNING (CTML)

### Core Theoretical Background

Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML; Mayer, 1991, 2001, 2009, 2019, 2022) is the most empirically validated framework for designing instructional media that combines words and images. Built on three foundational assumptions — **dual channel processing** (Paivio; Baddeley), **limited capacity** (Miller, 1956; Baddeley, 1986), and **active processing** (Wittrock, 1974) — CTML has generated 11 design principles, each supported by replicated experimental evidence across multiple labs and populations.

CTML is distinct from DCT in scope and origin: DCT is a theory of memory and cognition; CTML is a prescriptive theory of instructional design that *applies* the dual channel assumption to produce actionable design rules. Where DCT asks "how does the mind represent information?", CTML asks "how should instructional materials be designed given how the mind works?"

The three cognitive processes that CTML treats as necessary for meaningful learning:
- **Selecting:** Attending to relevant words and images from presented material
- **Organizing:** Building internal verbal and pictorial models
- **Integrating:** Connecting verbal and pictorial models with each other and with prior knowledge

Failure at any of these stages (due to overload, poor design, or misaligned presentation) degrades learning even when the material itself is correct and complete.

### Theoretical Foundations of CTML

| Foundation | Source | Implication for CTML |
|---|---|---|
| Dual channel | Paivio (1986); Baddeley (1986) | Visual/auditory channels are distinct — design must manage each separately |
| Limited capacity | Miller (1956) — 7±2 chunks; Baddeley's working memory model | Each channel has a capacity ceiling — exceeding it = overload = degraded learning |
| Active processing | Wittrock (1974, 1992) | Passive exposure to well-designed materials is insufficient; learners must actively select, organize, integrate |

### Meta-Analytic Overview of CTML

Three high-quality meta-analyses are essential for this section:

**Ginns (2005)** — Modality effect: d = 0.72 across 43 effect sizes from 18 studies. Modality effect is stronger when: (a) presentation pace is system-controlled (not learner-controlled), (b) content complexity is high, (c) learners have lower prior knowledge. All three conditions apply to N5–N4 Japanese vocabulary learning.

**Ginns (2006)** — Spatial and temporal contiguity: spatial contiguity mean d = 0.79 (k = 36 effects); temporal contiguity mean d = 1.14 (k = 13 effects). Temporal contiguity effect is among the largest and most replicable in the CTML literature.

**Mayer (2009) synthesis across principles** — Not a standalone meta-analysis but aggregates 12–15 studies per principle across the book, providing principle-level effect size estimates. The Multimedia principle (d ≈ 1.50) is the largest effect in the entire CTML literature.

## §9.3 — DUAL CODING APPLIED TO JAPANESE SCRIPT LEARNING

### The Unique Script-Acquisition Challenge for Indonesian Learners

Indonesian learners arrive at Japanese with a Latin-script-only L1. Unlike Chinese or Korean learners who have logographic or phonemic-based L2 orthography familiarity respectively, Indonesian learners face a **triple script system** (hiragana, katakana, kanji) with zero L1 orthographic transfer. This has significant dual coding implications that differ from the generic DCT framework:

**For learners whose L1 is Latin-only, kanji has zero L1 dual coding support.** When a Chinese learner sees 山, partial recognition from Chinese (山 = shān = mountain) provides a verbal bridge. For an Indonesian learner, 山 arrives as a purely visual stimulus with no pre-existing verbal connection. This means **visual scaffolding is not just helpful — it is the primary encoding route available at the initial learning stage.**

### Script Acquisition Through DCT

**Hiragana and Katakana:** These are phonemic scripts — each character maps to a syllable. The traditional learning challenge is associating visual form with phonological value. DCT predicts that mnemonic strategies linking the **visual shape** of a character to a **concrete image that sounds like the character** will outperform pure rote copying.

- Example: し (shi) → "looks like a fishing hook" (shi-ngling lure) — shape-sound-image triplet
- Example: つ (tsu) → "two lines like a wave of a tsunami" — shape-meaning connection

Research on phonemic script learning via visual mnemonics (Shapiro & Waters, 2005; Bourassa & Treiman, 2001) supports that shape-meaning-sound linkages accelerate character retention compared to rote copying.

**Kanji:** Kanji presents a more complex dual coding opportunity because characters are logographic — form encodes both sound (partially) and meaning. DCT suggests two complementary strategies:

1. **Semantic imagery:** Learn the meaning through the visual decomposition of the character (木 = tree → single tree; 森 = three trees → forest). This is the Heisig/WaniKani approach. The character's form activates the imagistic system directly. This is a strong dual coding strategy.

2. **Keyword method (Atkinson, 1975):** Create a concrete keyword in L1 (Indonesian or English) that sounds like the on-yomi of the kanji, then create an image linking the keyword to the kanji's meaning. Example: 山 (san/yama → "sane" keyword → "the mountain makes you go sane from the peace") — links phonological form to meaning via a concrete visual scene.

The keyword method was originally developed for L2 vocabulary (Atkinson, 1975) and was already cited in §1.9 (EE-03, Pressley 1982). **§9.3 connects this to its Dual Coding theoretical foundation and extends it specifically to kanji.**

### L1 Background and Script Acquisition Speed

Wang & Thomas (1995) found that keyword method advantages in vocabulary retention are strong at immediate testing but can attenuate at delayed testing compared to contextual learning — an important boundary condition. The implication for Nugget Nihongo: mnemonics are valuable for initial encoding but should be paired with contextual sentences and SRS-mediated retrieval practice (§1.2 Active Recall) to consolidate long-term retention.

Everson (1998), studying learners of Chinese as FL, found that character recognition strategies strongly predict learning success — learners who develop holistic pattern recognition (treating characters as unified images rather than stroke sequences) outperform those using analytical/stroke-by-stroke strategies. The pedagogical implication: train learners to see kanji as image-wholes, not stroke sequences, which aligns with the DCT approach of activating the nonverbal imagistic system.

## §9.4 — COGNITIVE LOAD THEORY IN SRS CARD DESIGN

### Three Types of Cognitive Load

Cognitive Load Theory (CLT; Sweller, 1988, 1994) identifies three types of cognitive load that compete for limited working memory resources:

**1. Intrinsic Load** — Arises from the inherent complexity of the to-be-learned material and the **element interactivity** between its components. Element interactivity is high when understanding one element requires simultaneously understanding several others. For Nugget Nihongo:
- A N5 card showing 犬 (inu = dog) → LOW element interactivity. Kanji, reading, meaning are straightforwardly connected. Low intrinsic load.
- A N2 card showing a grammar pattern with a nuanced politeness distinction embedded in context → HIGH element interactivity. Multiple interdependent elements must be processed simultaneously. High intrinsic load.
- **Implication:** Card complexity should be calibrated to JLPT level. N5 cards can include more fields simultaneously; N2 cards need careful field reduction.

**2. Extraneous Load** — Arises from **how** information is presented, not from the information itself. Poor design choices (split-attention layouts, redundant text+audio, decorative graphics, excessive visual clutter) impose extraneous load that is entirely preventable. CTML principles (§9.2) are essentially a prescriptive guide to minimizing extraneous load.

**3. Germane Load** — Processing that contributes to schema formation: recognizing patterns, making connections between new and prior knowledge, organizing information into long-term memory structures. Germane load is the only "good" load — learning requires germane processing. The goal of CLT-based design is to minimize extraneous load while preserving germane load.

For Nugget Nihongo flashcard design, the operational framework is:
- **Intrinsic load** is fixed by the material (JLPT level) — manage it by appropriate level targeting
- **Extraneous load** is entirely controllable through card design — apply §9.2 CTML principles to eliminate it
- **Germane load** is promoted by retrieval practice (§1.2), elaborative encoding (§1.9), and generative strategies (§9.5)

### The Expertise Reversal Effect

One of the most practically important findings in CLT for Nugget Nihongo is the **expertise reversal effect** (Kalyuga et al., 1998, 2000; Kalyuga, 2007): instructional supports that are beneficial for novice learners become redundant or even harmful for more advanced learners, because the support information that helps novices now redundantly re-encodes information the expert already has in long-term memory schemas.

Applied to Nugget Nihongo card design:

| Card Field | N5–N4 (Novice) | N3 (Intermediate) | N2–N1 (Advanced) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Romaji pronunciation guide | **Show** — scaffolding | **Optional** — learner preference | **Hide** — becomes extraneous load |
| L1 Indonesian translation (meaning) | **Show** — essential | **Show** — still useful | **Optional/Hide** — can become a crutch |
| L2d (contrastive linguistic note, §5) | **Show** — reduces interference | **Optional** | **Hide** — schema is formed; note is redundant |
| Example sentence translation | **Show full** | **Show partial** — hide translation initially | **Hide** — productive retrieval only |
| Furigana on kanji in example sentence | **Show always** | **Show on demand** | **Hide always** |
| Cultural context note | **Show** | **Show** | **Show** — cultural depth increases with level |

This expertise reversal table is the CLT evidence base for **level-differentiated card templates** in Nugget Nihongo. A single universal card template that shows all fields is optimal for no one: it overloads beginners (too many elements) and insults advanced learners (redundant information imposing extraneous load).

### Pollock et al. (2002) — Isolated Elements First

Pollock, Chandler, & Sweller (2002) demonstrated that when element interactivity is very high, presenting isolated elements first (even without their interactions) and then presenting them integrated is more effective than presenting integrated elements immediately. This supports the **progressive reveal structure** of flashcards: showing the front of the card (isolated: kanji only, or kanji + reading) before revealing the back (integrated: meaning + example + cultural note).

This provides CLT justification for the SRS card flip mechanism itself — the front/back structure is not an arbitrary design convention but an empirically supported method for managing intrinsic load from high-interactivity elements.

## §9.5 — GENERATIVE LEARNING THEORY AND ACTIVE PROCESSING

### From Passive Multimedia to Active Construction

Wittrock's Generative Learning Theory (1974, 1992) proposes that learning is not reception but **construction**: learners build meaning by generating connections between presented information and their existing knowledge. Passive consumption of even optimally designed multimedia is insufficient for deep learning without generative activity.

This is the bridge connecting §9's multimedia design principles back to §1's active recall and testing effect research. CTML tells us how to design the stimulus; Generative Learning Theory tells us why the learner must *do something* with it.

Fiorella & Mayer (2015, 2016) synthesized the empirical evidence for eight generative learning strategies with demonstrated learning benefits:

| Strategy | Effect on Learning | SRS Applicability | Nugget Nihongo Implementation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Summarizing | Medium-High | Indirect | L2d notes as prompted summarization of grammar point |
| 2. Mapping | High | Indirect | Semantic field cards — grouping related vocabulary |
| 3. Drawing | High | Direct | Prompt learner to sketch meaning of concrete words before reveal |
| 4. Imagining | High | Direct | Mnemonic construction prompt ("picture yourself using this word") |
| 5. Self-testing | Very High | **Direct — this IS the flashcard** | Front-of-card recall prompt IS self-testing |
| 6. Self-explaining | High | Direct | L2d contrastive note prompts: "why does this differ from Indonesian?" |
| 7. Teaching | High | Indirect | Out-of-app implementation (study groups, language exchange) |
| 8. Enacting | Medium | Indirect | Physical gesture for action verbs (see §9.1 embodied cognition) |

The critical insight from Generative Learning Theory for SRS design: **the back of the card is not just the answer — it is the generative prompt space.** Cards that include L2d contrastive notes, mnemonic construction hints, and self-explanation prompts are implementing generative strategies 4 and 6, not merely providing information.

### Chi et al. (1994) — Self-Explanation Effect

Chi et al. (1994) demonstrated that prompting learners to explain to themselves why a presented explanation is correct produced significantly better learning than passive reading alone. The self-explanation effect is one of the most replicated findings in educational psychology (effect sizes typically d = 0.55–1.00 across studies). For Nugget Nihongo, this means:

**Cards that include a brief prompt ("なぜこの助詞を使う？" or "Why this particle, not は?") adjacent to the example sentence will produce better grammar retention than cards that simply display the correct form.**

### Schwartz & Bransford (1998) — Preparation for Future Learning

Schwartz & Bransford (1998) demonstrated that initial exploratory attempts (even failed ones) prior to formal instruction improved learning from subsequent multimedia instruction significantly. This "preparation for future learning" (PFL) effect supports the Nugget Nihongo onboarding design: brief unguided exploration before formal study sensitizes learners to the structure they will later learn from CTML-optimized cards.

## §9.6 — IMAGE SELECTION PRINCIPLES FOR L2 VOCABULARY CARDS

### What Makes an Image Effective for Vocabulary Encoding?

Not all images produce equal dual coding benefit. Research on pictures in instruction (Levie & Lentz, 1982; Alesandrini, 1984; Peeck, 1993) has established a typology of effective vs. ineffective instructional images:

**Effective Image Characteristics:**

1. **Prototypicality:** The image should depict the most typical exemplar of the concept. A prototypical 犬 card shows a medium-sized dog in a natural context — not a cartoon dog, not an unusual breed, not a dog in unusual clothing. Prototype theory (Rosch, 1975) predicts that prototypical exemplars anchor category representation most effectively.

2. **Distinctiveness:** The image must clearly differentiate the target concept from semantically similar concepts. 犬 and 猫 images must be visually unambiguous; 電車 and バス images should highlight the defining visual feature of each.

3. **Concrete referent match:** Images work effectively for concrete nouns with clear perceptual referents (食べ物, 動物, 乗り物, 場所). They are less effective for abstract vocabulary (感情 words like 残念, 嬉しい are borderline; function words like のに, ながら are not image-supportable).

4. **Complexity match:** Mayer & Gallini (1990) found that illustrations are most beneficial for high-complexity content (understanding how a system works) and add less value for simple recall items. For kanji with transparent visual morphology (山, 木, 川), a visual illustration may add little beyond the character itself. For kanji with opaque form-meaning relationships (魔, 麗, 響), a visual mnemonic scene is highly valuable.

5. **Cultural congruence:** Images should reflect authentic Japanese cultural contexts, not Western equivalents. A card for 定食 should show a Japanese teishoku tray, not a generic "set meal." This is especially important for cultural vocabulary (茶道, 銭湯, 居酒屋) where the visual prototype itself is culturally specific.

### The Split-Attention Effect and Image-Text Placement

CTML's spatial contiguity principle (§9.2, Principle 2) directly constrains image-text placement in card layout. Chandler & Sweller (1992) demonstrated the **split-attention effect**: when learners must mentally integrate information from two spatially separated sources (e.g., a diagram at the top and a text explanation at the bottom of a page), cognitive load increases substantially compared to physically integrated presentation.

For Nugget Nihongo card template design:
- **Japanese word + image must be spatially adjacent** — not on different visual zones of the card
- **Furigana must appear immediately adjacent to kanji**, not in a separate furigana field below
- **Example sentence and translation should be integrated**, not separated by visual whitespace

### When Images Hurt: The Redundancy Warning

Mayer & Gallini (1990) also found that for **simple, well-known content**, adding illustrations does not improve learning and can actually distract from text-based processing. For very basic N5 vocabulary where the word is already partially familiar (パン, コーヒー, テレビ — loanwords from English/Portuguese recognizable to Indonesian learners), elaborate illustrated images may be extraneous load. A simpler visual treatment (or even no image) may be more efficient.

## §9.7 — AUDIO-VISUAL INTEGRATION IN LANGUAGE VOCABULARY LEARNING

### The Modality Effect in L2 Context

The **modality effect** (Mayer & Moreno, 1998; Ginns, 2005) establishes that for graphic/animation content, audio narration produces better learning than on-screen text — because audio uses the auditory processing channel while graphics use the visual channel. Both can be processed simultaneously without channel overload. On-screen text + graphics compete for the same visual channel.

For Nugget Nihongo vocabulary cards, the modality effect generates a clear recommendation: **Japanese pronunciation audio + kanji/image visual display uses both channels efficiently. This is the optimal delivery mode.**

However, L2 language learning introduces important nuances that do not apply to the standard CTML animation-narration studies:

### The L2 Captioning Exception

Vanderplank (1988) and subsequent L2 captioning research (Sydorenko, 2010; Guichon & McLornan, 2008; Jones, 2006) demonstrates an important boundary condition: the redundancy effect (which penalizes text + audio presenting identical content) is **attenuated for L2 learners who cannot fully decode the audio without textual support.**

This is directly relevant for Nugget Nihongo because:
- An N5 learner hearing a Japanese sentence cannot parse it in real-time without textual support
- For this learner, text + audio simultaneously is not redundant — the text provides phonological scaffolding the learner cannot yet provide internally
- At N3–N2, learners develop enough auditory processing ability that text becomes genuinely redundant
- At N1, audio-only is the target proficiency mode

This creates a level-differentiated recommendation that directly intersects with CLT's expertise reversal effect (§9.4):

### Level-Differentiated Audio-Visual Recommendations

| JLPT Level | Card Delivery Mode | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| N5 | Audio auto-plays + text displayed simultaneously | Text provides scaffolding learner cannot yet internalize; redundancy effect not applicable |
| N4 | Audio auto-plays + text displayed simultaneously | Same as N5; still insufficient auditory parsing ability |
| N3 | Audio auto-plays; full text displayed but **translation hidden initially** | Partial expertise reversal — audio supports but L1 translation should be retrieved, not read |
| N2 | Audio-first; **tap to reveal kanji reading** | Expertise reversal in effect; kanji reading is now extraneous scaffolding |
| N1 | Audio-only mode as default; all text on demand only | Full expertise reversal; target proficiency = audio-only comprehension |

### The Simultaneous Presentation Debate: Subtitles in CALL

A methodological debate in CALL research concerns whether same-language subtitles (text matching audio) help or hurt L2 listening comprehension development. The consensus from Sydorenko (2010) and Nation & Newton (2009) is nuanced:

- **Same-language subtitles aid vocabulary learning** (new word → new sound + new orthography encoded together) — beneficial for form-meaning mapping
- **Same-language subtitles may impede development of auditory processing speed** if learners become dependent on text to compensate for incomplete auditory parsing

For Nugget Nihongo, this suggests **time-limited subtitle scaffolding**: display text initially, but gradually phase out for repeated reviews. This aligns with the SRS scheduling logic — as a card matures, progressively reduce scaffolding (a level-aware algorithm could implement this automatically).

### Audio Format Considerations

Kang et al. (2014) and Field (2008) on L2 listening research note that **phonologically authentic audio** (native speaker, natural speech rate, authentic prosody) is essential for training phonological representations. Synthesized or slowed-down audio builds phonological models that do not transfer to real-world listening.

For Nugget Nihongo: native speaker audio for all vocabulary cards is not merely a UX choice but a learning science requirement.

## §9.10.1 — ORTHOGRAPHIC DEPTH HYPOTHESIS

### Latar Belakang

Mengapa pelajar bahasa Itali dapat membaca teks baru dengan akurasi tinggi dalam beberapa minggu, sementara pelajar bahasa Jepang membutuhkan bertahun-tahun untuk mencapai level fluency yang setara? Orthographic Depth Hypothesis (ODH) memberikan jawaban sistematis: *orthographic depth* — seberapa konsisten hubungan antara bentuk tulisan dan bunyi — secara langsung menentukan kecepatan dan lintasan akuisisi reading.

**Shallow orthographies** (Italia, Spanyol, Finlandia) memiliki korespondensi grapheme-phoneme yang sangat konsisten: hampir setiap kata baru dapat "dibaca" dengan tepat meskipun tidak pernah dilihat sebelumnya. **Deep orthographies** (Inggris, Prancis, Ibrani) memiliki korespondensi yang tidak konsisten: strategi subleksikal sering gagal dan learner harus menyimpan banyak kata sebagai unit leksikal khusus. **Japanese** merupakan kasus ekstrem: kanji tidak memiliki korespondensi grapheme-phoneme yang dapat diprediksi sama sekali pada level karakter individual; hanya whole-word (lexical) lookup yang reliable.

Implikasi untuk pelajar Indonesia sangat spesifik: mereka datang dari bahasa dengan orthografi yang sangat *shallow* — bahasa Indonesia hampir fonemis sempurna. Transfer dari pengalaman orthografis L1 ini adalah **nol** untuk kanji dan **sangat terbatas** bahkan untuk kana. Setiap strategi decoding yang mereka miliki dari pengalaman membaca bahasa Indonesia harus sebagian besar dikalibrasi ulang.

## §9.10.2 — DUAL ROUTE MODEL OF VISUAL WORD RECOGNITION

### Latar Belakang

Bagaimana otak membaca kanji berbeda secara fundamental dari bagaimana ia membaca kana — dan keduanya berbeda dari bagaimana ia membaca bahasa Latin. Dual Route Cascaded (DRC) model (Coltheart et al., 2001) menyediakan kerangka paling berpengaruh untuk menjelaskan perbedaan mekanistik ini.

DRC model berargumen bahwa visual word recognition memiliki dua jalur paralel:
1. **Lexical route** (direct visual-to-meaning): kata dikenali sebagai whole unit berdasarkan visual pattern — langsung ke meaning tanpa intermediate phonological decoding. Cepat, tapi bergantung pada familiar lexical entries.
2. **Sublexical route** (assembled phonology): kata di-decode grapheme-by-grapheme atau mora-by-mora menjadi phonological representation, lalu diakses maknanya secara tidak langsung. Lebih lambat, tapi memungkinkan reading novel words.

Implikasi kritis untuk Japanese: **Kanji harus diproses via lexical route** — tidak ada korespondensi grapheme-phoneme yang reliable untuk kanji yang tidak dikenal. **Kana dapat diproses via sublexical route** — hiragana dan katakana memiliki korespondensi mora-grapheme yang sangat konsisten (bahkan lebih konsisten dari Italian). Ini menciptakan situasi di mana Japanese reader *secara bersamaan* mengoperasikan kedua route dalam satu kalimat, tergantung pada script apa yang sedang dibaca.

*Cross-reference: Wydell & Butterworth (1999) [KS-09 §9.3] memberikan bukti klinis definitif untuk dissociation script-specific route di Japanese melalui studi kasus bilingual dengan kanji-spesifik dyslexia. Tidak di-cite ulang di sini.*

**Implikasi 2 (Hiragana sebelum katakana untuk beginners):** Chikamatsu (2006) [OD-13] mengkonfirmasi hiragana > katakana dalam recognition speed di early learners. Urutan hiragana → katakana di Nugget Nihongo bukan hanya konvensi pedagogis — ada backing empiris.

**Implikasi 3 (Hindari romanization dependency):** OD-13 secara eksplisit menunjukkan bahwa romanization mempercepat recognition jangka pendek tapi menghambat acquisition jangka panjang. Ini adalah justifikasi empiris untuk policy "romaji off setelah kana mastered."

## §9.10.4 — RAPID AUTOMATIZED NAMING & READING SPEED

### Latar Belakang

Automaticity dalam reading tidak dapat diukur hanya dengan *accuracy* — seorang learner mungkin dapat mengidentifikasi kanji dengan benar setelah berpikir selama 3 detik, tapi ini bukan fluency. **Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN)** — kecepatan menamai simbol familiar yang berulang — adalah prediktor reading fluency yang kuat di seluruh orthografi, bahkan setelah mengontrol decoding accuracy.

RAN bekerja sebagai proxy untuk *lexical route automatization*: semakin cepat simbol familiar diberi nama tanpa conscious effort, semakin terotomatisasi proses recognitionnya. Untuk kanji, RAN speed memprediksi berapa banyak attentional resources tersisa untuk comprehension setelah proses recognition selesai.

Implikasi untuk Nugget Nihongo: **response time** pada saat review kartu adalah ukuran yang lebih sensitive daripada sekadar correct/incorrect binary. FSRS saat ini menggunakan ratings (Again/Hard/Good/Easy) yang mengabstraksi fluency; response latency bisa menjadi signal tambahan.

## §9.10.7 — Integration: Phonological Recoding in Kanji Reading (Micro-Addendum)

*See also §11 (PH-01–PH-44) untuk phonological perception science; subseksi ini secara khusus membahas titik integrasi visual-auditori yang unik bagi pembacaan kanji — mekanisme yang menjembatani §9.10 (orthographic development) dan §11 (phonological learning science).*

---

#### Latar Belakang: The Phonological Recoding Debate

Di antara semua pertanyaan terbuka dalam Japanese reading research, satu pertanyaan mekanistik paling langsung relevan untuk desain kartu Nugget Nihongo: **apakah pembaca Jepang selalu mengaktifkan fonologi ketika membaca kanji, ataukah mereka dapat mengakses makna secara langsung tanpa mediasi fonologis?**

Pertanyaan ini bukan hanya akademis. Jawabannya menentukan apakah menampilkan *yomi* (kana/furigana) pada kartu kanji adalah scaffolding pedagogis yang diperlukan atau justru properti yang dapat dilepas begitu makna karakter sudah dikuasai.

Dua posisi mendominasi literatur:

**Posisi 1 — Universal Phonological Pathway:** Bahkan dalam skrip logografis, kode fonologis diaktifkan secara otomatis dan awal selama proses pengenalan kata visual — sebelum makna diakses (Perfetti & Tan, 1998; Frost, 1998). Fonologi bukan opsional; ia adalah bagian integral dari arsitektur pengenalan kata lintas semua skrip, dengan modulasi orthografis yang menentukan *timing* dan *strength*-nya, bukan ada-tidaknya.

**Posisi 2 — Direct Lexical Route (Qualified):** Untuk karakter yang sangat terlatih, pembaca native Jepang dapat mengakses makna kanji tanpa mediasi fonologis penuh dalam kondisi tertentu — terutama ketika karakter memiliki keistimewaan ortografis yang memungkinkan *direct visual-semantic mapping* (Wydell, Patterson & Humphreys, 1993). Namun ini adalah *endpoint* dari ribuan jam eksposur, bukan kondisi awal.

Resolusi yang didukung oleh bukti: kedua posisi mendeskripsikan fenomena nyata, tetapi pada populasi dan kondisi yang berbeda. Pembaca native expert *dapat* mengakses kanji ke makna tanpa mediasi fonologis penuh untuk karakter yang sudah terotomatisasi — namun kode fonologis tetap diaktifkan secara paralel, bahkan jika tidak selalu kausal untuk comprehension (Perfetti & Tan, 1998; Tamaoka, 1991). Untuk **pelajar L2 yang berada di bawah sekitar 2.000 kanji**, asosiasi bentuk-makna-baca belum terotomatisasi — fonologi bukan bypass opsional tetapi jalur akses utama yang tersedia.

---

#### §9.10.7.1 — The Phonological Recoding Debate: Early Evidence

Debat tentang phonological recoding dalam membaca dimulai dari literatur multi-orthografi sebelum spesifik ke kanji. Feldman & Turvey (1983) mendemonstrasikan bahwa bahkan dalam skrip dengan kedalaman orthografis yang signifikan, proses fonologis tetap terlibat dalam pengenalan kata — membangun fondasi untuk klaim universalitas yang kemudian diuji lintas skrip. Kerja awal ini menetapkan bahwa *asumsi default* dalam teori pengenalan kata harus menyertakan keterlibatan fonologis, bukan mengecualikannya.

Wydell, Patterson & Humphreys (1993) kemudian menghadirkan data yang paling banyak dikutip dalam debat kanji — yang dikenal sebagai "case-stone" study. Mereka menguji apakah stimulus orthografis yang homofon (berbunyi sama) dengan kanji target dapat memicu priming efek pada pembaca Jepang native, sebagaimana yang ditemukan dalam skrip alfabetis. Temuannya bersifat nuanced: dalam beberapa kondisi eksperimental, priming fonologis untuk kanji *tidak* muncul dengan kekuatan yang sama seperti pada kata kana — mengindikasikan bahwa akses makna kanji *dapat* bypass mediasi fonologis penuh pada pembaca native yang sangat terlatih. Namun studi ini juga mengkonfirmasi bahwa ini adalah *fenomena native-reader spesifik* yang bergantung pada degree of orthographic familiarity yang sangat tinggi.

---

#### §9.10.7.2 — Native Reader Evidence: Fonologi Tetap Aktif

Temuan Wydell et al. (1993) sering disalahinterpretasi sebagai bukti bahwa kanji dibaca "tanpa fonologi." Tamaoka (1991) menyediakan koreksi empiris penting: menggunakan paradigma priming fonologis dengan stimulus Japanese native, ia menunjukkan bahwa aktivasi fonologis *memang terjadi* selama pembacaan kanji bahkan pada pembaca native — hanya timing dan magnitude-nya yang berbeda dari pembacaan kana atau teks alfabetis. Fonologi diaktifkan, namun pada beberapa pembaca expert, aktivasi tersebut tidak selalu *kausal* untuk akses makna (yakni makna dapat dicapai melalui visual-semantic route terlebih dahulu, dengan fonologi diaktifkan hampir bersamaan namun bukan prasyarat).

Implikasi untuk pelajar L2: pembaca native yang mampu mengakses kanji ke makna dengan mediasi fonologis minimal telah mencapai kondisi ini melalui *ribuan jam eksposur* yang membangun representasi visual-semantic yang kuat. Pelajar L2 tidak berada dalam kondisi ini — mereka tidak memiliki representasi kanji yang terotomatisasi. Untuk mereka, fonologi (kana, furigana) adalah *satu-satunya akses route yang fungsional* untuk sebagian besar kanji yang belum dikuasai.

---

#### §9.10.7.3 — Universal Phonological Pathway: Grounding the Claim

Perfetti & Tan (1998) memberikan grounding teoretis paling berpengaruh untuk klaim universalitas jalur fonologis dalam membaca. Menggunakan paradigma masked priming dengan stimulus Chinese (skrip morfosyllabic, analog dengan kanji dalam beberapa aspek), mereka mendemonstrasikan bahwa kode fonologis diaktifkan *sangat awal* dalam proses pengenalan karakter — sebelum pengakses makna selesai. Time course-nya menunjukkan: graphic activation → phonological activation → semantic activation, dengan fonologi mendahului semantik alih-alih semantik mendahului fonologi sebagaimana dipostulasikan oleh direct-access proponents. Orthografi me-modulasi *strength* dan *speed* aktivasi fonologis, bukan ada-tidaknya.

Frost (1998) memperluas posisi ini menjadi "strong phonological theory" of visual word identification yang secara langsung menjawab debat kanji exception. Argumennya: klaim bahwa kanji/hanzi dibaca "tanpa fonologi" adalah kesalahan interpretasi data — yang sesungguhnya ditemukan adalah perbedaan dalam *mekanisme* fonological mediation (direct whole-word phonology vs. assembled phonology via grapheme-phoneme rules), bukan ada-tidaknya fonologi. Semua skrip yang dipelajari dengan baik mengaktifkan representasi fonologis kata secara otomatis; yang berbeda adalah jalur yang digunakan untuk sampai ke sana.

Perfetti, Liu & Tan (2005) mengintegrasikan temuan Chinese reading research ke dalam Lexical Constituency Model (LCM) yang memiliki implikasi lintas skrip. LCM memprediksi bahwa kanji/hanzi dengan konstituen fonologis yang lebih kuat (on'yomi yang dapat diprediksi dari radikal fonetik) akan menunjukkan aktivasi fonologis yang lebih kuat dan lebih cepat daripada kanji dengan korespondensi fonetik yang irregular. Ini memiliki implikasi langsung untuk sequencing kanji introduction di platform: kanji dengan on'yomi yang konsisten dan dapat diprediksi adalah kandidat yang lebih baik untuk early introduction daripada kanji dengan pembacaan yang idiosinkratik.

#### §9.10.7.4 — Sintesis: Implikasi untuk Pelajar L2 di Bawah ~2.000 Kanji

Konvergensi evidence dari PR-01 hingga PR-06 menghasilkan satu kesimpulan yang dapat dioperasionalisasi:

**Akses makna kanji tanpa mediasi fonologis adalah endpoint native-reader yang membutuhkan ribuan jam eksposur untuk membangun representasi visual-semantic yang terotomatisasi — bukan strategi pedagogis yang dapat dipindahkan ke pelajar L2.**

Untuk pelajar L2 yang belum mencapai sekitar 2.000 kanji dengan representasi terotomatisasi, berikut adalah kondisi kognitif yang relevan:

1. **Tidak ada whole-word phonological representation yang tersedia** untuk sebagian besar kanji baru — tidak seperti pembaca native yang sudah memiliki lexical entries yang kaya untuk karakter familiar.

2. **Phonological recoding via kana/furigana adalah *satu-satunya route fungsional*** untuk mengakses makna kanji yang belum dikuasai — mirip dengan bagaimana pembaca awal bahasa alfabetis harus melakukan grapheme-phoneme conversion sebelum lexical lookup menjadi otomatis.

3. **Menghapus kana/furigana sebelum representasi fonologis-visual-semantik terbentuk secara stabil** tidak "memaksa" pelajar untuk membangun direct access route — ia hanya mengeliminasi akses route yang tersedia, sehingga menekan retensi (Mori & Shimizu, 2007 [ER-19]; Chikamatsu, 1996 [ER-20]).

4. **LCM (PR-06) menyarankan bahwa kanji dengan on'yomi reguler** (dapat diprediksi dari radikal fonetik) akan lebih mudah membangun representasi fonologis yang kuat daripada kanji dengan pembacaan idiosinkratik. Ini dapat digunakan untuk merancang sequencing yang memaksimalkan phonological transparency di early stages.

*Cross-reference: §4.5 (ER-19, ER-20) untuk dokumentasi lengkap furigana dilemma dalam konteks extensive reading; §9.10.1–§9.10.2 (OD-01, OD-06) untuk ODH dan DRC framework yang menjelaskan *mengapa* kanji memerlukan lexical route yang berbeda secara fundamental dari kana.*

---

#### Design Implication Block (§9.10.7)

> **Nugget Nihongo cards should always display the reading (kana/furigana) alongside the kanji form for all cards below the learner's mature mastery threshold.** Removing phonological scaffolding before a learner has established automatic form-meaning-reading integration suppresses retention rather than building logographic access.

| Prinsip | Evidence | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| Fonologi diaktifkan universal, bukan opsional | Strong phonological theory — semua skrip mengaktifkan kode fonologis | PR-05 (Frost 1998); PR-04 (Perfetti & Tan 1998) |
| Direct kanji access = native endpoint, bukan strategi awal | "Case-stone" study: direct access hanya untuk karakter yang sangat familiar pada native readers | PR-02 (Wydell et al. 1993) |
| L2 learner memerlukan fonologi sebagai access route utama | Pelajar L2 tidak punya lexical entries yang terotomatisasi untuk kanji baru | PR-03 (Tamaoka 1991) + PR-04 |
| Menghapus furigana terlalu awal menekan akuisisi kanji | Phonological bypass via furigana meningkatkan comprehension jangka pendek tapi menghambat kanji retention jangka panjang | ER-19 (cross-ref); ER-20 (cross-ref) |
| Kanji dengan on'yomi reguler lebih mudah membangun representasi | LCM: phonological constituent strength predicts activation ease | PR-06 (Perfetti et al. 2005) |

**Operasionalisasi yang direkomendasikan:**

1. **Display kana/furigana untuk semua kanji di bawah FSRS "Mature" threshold** — definisikan "Mature" sebagai minimum interval ≥21 hari dengan accuracy ≥90%.

2. **Graduated furigana reduction (bukan penghapusan mendadak):** Tahap 1 → full furigana; Tahap 2 → furigana on hover/tap only; Tahap 3 → kana tanpa furigana; Tahap 4 → kanji only. Setiap transisi dipicu oleh mastery threshold FSRS, bukan waktu studi yang berlalu.

3. **Prioritas early sequencing untuk kanji dengan on'yomi reguler:** Berdasarkan LCM (PR-06), kanji yang memiliki radikal fonetik dengan korespondensi on'yomi yang dapat diprediksi membangun representasi fonologis lebih cepat. Ini menyediakan satu dimensi tambahan untuk sequencing di luar frequency atau JLPT tier.

4. **Flag untuk Crispy — Feature Proposal:** Sistem secara otomatis mendeteksi kanji dengan phonological transparency tinggi (on'yomi dapat diprediksi dari radikal fonetik) vs. rendah, dan memprioritaskan yang transparent untuk early introduction. Memerlukan database radikal fonetik × on'yomi dari JMdict/KanjiDic2.
