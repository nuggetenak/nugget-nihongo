## §14.1 — Communicative Competence: The Model

The concept of communicative competence — the ability to use language appropriately within social context, not merely to produce grammatically well-formed sentences — was formalised by Hymes (1972) **[SC-01]** in direct response to Chomsky's narrower construct of linguistic competence. Hymes argued that a theory of language adequate for applied purposes must account for four dimensions of knowledge: whether (and to what degree) something is formally *possible* given the language's grammatical system, *feasible* for cognitive performance, *appropriate* to the social and situational context, and actually *done* in practice within the speech community. The distinction between what is grammatically possible and what is pragmatically appropriate is the foundational claim that drives the entire communicative competence research program, and it is precisely the gap between those two dimensions that characterises the keigo challenge for L2 Japanese learners.

Canale and Swain (1980) **[SC-02]** produced the first operationalisable framework for applied linguistics and language teaching, partitioning communicative competence into four explicit components:

1. **Grammatical competence** — mastery of the language code: phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and orthography. This is the domain addressed by standard structural instruction and by JLPT examination content up to and including N3.
2. **Sociolinguistic competence** — knowledge of the sociocultural rules governing language use: when, how, and with whom particular forms are appropriate; how to produce and interpret utterances that are contextually adequate, not merely syntactically correct. This is the domain of register, formality, social indexicality, and — in Japanese — the honorific system.
3. **Discourse competence** — knowledge of how to combine grammatical forms and meanings to achieve unified, coherent spoken or written text across extended sequences: cohesion, thematic progression, genre conventions.
4. **Strategic competence** — knowledge of verbal and nonverbal communication strategies deployed to compensate for breakdowns in communication or to enhance communicative effectiveness.

The critical theoretical contribution of this four-part model is its formal recognition that sociolinguistic competence is **irreducible to grammatical competence**. A learner who commands the morphosyntax of Japanese verb conjugation, particle use, and sentence-final patterns — who, in other words, has achieved high grammatical competence — may nonetheless be entirely deficient in the knowledge of when plain form versus polite form versus honorific form is socially required. That sociolinguistic gap produces communicative failure in precisely the contexts where authentic language performance is most consequential.

Bachman (1990) **[SC-03]** substantially revised and elaborated the Canale-Swain model, replacing its four-component structure with a hierarchical framework organised around two superordinate components: *organisational competence* (grammatical and textual) and *pragmatic competence* (illocutionary and sociolinguistic). The theoretical manoeuvre is consequential: in Bachman's model, sociolinguistic competence is repositioned as a subcategory of pragmatic knowledge, not an adjunct to grammar. Pragmatic knowledge — the ability to perform and interpret illocutionary acts appropriately, and to calibrate language use to social and situational context — is theoretically coordinate with organisational knowledge, not subordinate to it. A language ability assessment that samples only grammatical and textual competence is, by this framework, a partial assessment of language ability by design.

Bachman and Palmer (1996) **[SC-04]** further refined the framework for language assessment purposes, re-specifying the sociolinguistic component to include sensitivity to dialect and register variation, naturalness of expression, and cultural references and figurative language. The 1996 update is directly relevant to the Nugget Nihongo context because it makes **register variation** — the selection among formally equivalent utterances according to social context — an explicit target of language ability assessment, not a post-acquisition refinement. Under the Bachman-Palmer framework, keigo competence in Japanese is a central operationalisation of sociolinguistic competence: not a supplementary or culturally ornamental layer applied atop the real target of instruction, but a coordinate dimension of language ability whose absence constitutes a structural gap in communicative competence, not a surface imperfection.

The practical implication of this theoretical lineage is direct and consequential for platform design. Any Japanese L2 curriculum that treats keigo as an advanced topic, an optional cultural module, or a complexity reserved for post-N3 learners is miscalibrated against the communicative competence framework. Under Canale-Swain and Bachman-Palmer, sociolinguistic competence is a coordinate dimension of language ability from the earliest stages of instruction. A learner who exits a Japanese course — or completes a JLPT-focused deck — with high grammatical competence and low sociolinguistic competence is, by this framework, only partially competent, regardless of examination score.

## §14.2 — Keigo Acquisition Research

The acquisition of keigo (敬語) by L2 Japanese learners presents a distinctive and challenging profile within the broader interlanguage pragmatics research tradition. Cook (2008) **[SC-05]** provides the most comprehensive investigation of how learners of Japanese as a foreign language acquire and deploy speech style — a category that encompasses not only the morphosyntactic forms of 尊敬語 (*sonkeigo*), 謙譲語 (*kenjōgo*), and 丁寧語 (*teineigo*) but the continuous indexical system through which speakers locate themselves within social hierarchies in real-time interaction. Cook's central finding is that speech style in Japanese is not a discrete grammatical category acquired through rule exposure but a socially embedded practice acquired through participation in socially indexed relationships. This has a consequential corollary for L2 instruction: learners cannot achieve keigo competence by studying morphological rules in isolation, no matter how thoroughly those rules are drilled. They must acquire the social positioning logic — the understanding of which interactional relationships and institutional contexts require which register adjustments — that drives form selection.

The teachability dimension of keigo has been directly examined by Pizziconi (2011) **[SC-06]**, who interrogates the assumption — common in pedagogically oriented discussions of Japanese language teaching — that keigo is systematically teachable as a rule-governed system. Pizziconi's analysis distinguishes between the *teachable layer* of keigo (the morphosyntactic transformations that convert plain-form verbs to polite-form and honorific-form equivalents, and the basic distributional rules specifying when sonkeigo vs. kenjōgo applies) and the *unteachable layer* (the locally negotiated, contextually calibrated pragmatic judgments that native speakers make in real-time institutional interaction, drawing on tacit knowledge of the social field that cannot be fully codified into learnable rules). The pedagogical implication is not that keigo instruction is futile but that its scope must be precisely bounded: explicit instruction can and should target the teachable rule layer; it must simultaneously create structured exposure to authentic interactional exemplars to develop the non-rule-governed judgment layer that explicit instruction cannot reach.

A persistent finding across L2 Japanese politeness research is an **error asymmetry**: learners do not err randomly across the formality spectrum but systematically tend toward either hyperformality or hypoformality, rarely achieving the calibrated register appropriateness that native speakers produce as the social default. Siegal (1996) **[SC-07]**, in a study of Western women learning Japanese in naturalistic immersion conditions in Japan, documented this calibration failure as a structural feature of interlanguage pragmatics in Japanese — not as individual error but as a systematic learner pattern. Siegal found that learners who had acquired polite-form grammar (teineigo/desu-masu) tended to deploy it uniformly across interlocutors and contexts, producing persistent hyperformality with peers and workplace equals where plain form is expected, while simultaneously failing to calibrate upward to full sonkeigo with institutional superiors where the higher register is socially obligatory. The result is a characteristic learner register — identifiable to native-speaker interlocutors as non-native — that sits between the social positions the forms are designed to index. The learner is simultaneously too formal and not formal enough, in ways that native speakers perceive as a single, coherent signal of sociolinguistic incompetence.

This asymmetric error pattern is documented across multiple learner populations and learning contexts, and it persists even in advanced learners with high grammatical proficiency. The error endures because calibrated keigo selection requires simultaneous real-time processing of: (a) the interlocutor's social position relative to the speaker; (b) the institutional context of the interaction — workplace, classroom, service encounter; (c) the speech act being performed — directive, request, report, greeting; and (d) the sequential position within the ongoing exchange. Kasper and Rose (2002) **[SC-08]** situate this compound processing demand within the broader theoretical framework of interlanguage pragmatics development, demonstrating that pragmatic development in L2 consistently lags behind grammatical development because the knowledge required for pragmatic appropriateness is partly metalinguistic (rule-based, declarative, teachable) and partly non-declarative (intuitive, practice-based, and resistant to explicit instruction). Forms can be taught; the social judgment required to deploy those forms correctly under real-time cognitive load can only be partially taught and must be substantially practised.

Ohta (2001) **[CP-57]**, in a longitudinal classroom study of L2 Japanese acquisition, documents the development of polite speech registers through collaborative interaction in classroom settings, showing that even minimal explicit instruction on the distributional rules of desu/masu versus plain form is sufficient to establish the basic register distinction in a classroom population from early stages. However, Ohta also documents the persistence of **form-function mismatches** in advanced learners: learners produce morphologically correct teineigo in contexts where a native speaker would shift to plain form to signal in-group solidarity or relational closeness. The learner knows the form; they do not know that the socially appropriate move in this specific interactional moment is to *not* use the polite form. This form-function mismatch — present even in advanced learners with extensive classroom and exposure experience — is the defining characteristic of L2 keigo interlanguage.

The synthesis of this research base yields a clear instructional imperative: **explicit keigo instruction is necessary, not merely helpful — and it is not, by itself, sufficient.** Acquisition research consistently shows that keigo competence does not emerge naturally from high grammatical proficiency or extended input exposure in the absence of explicit instruction. Learners who achieve high grammatical proficiency without explicit keigo instruction develop stable interlanguage patterns — hyperformal or hypoformal — that resist correction because they are internally consistent and communicatively functional, if pragmatically marked. Explicit instruction must intervene at the level of the sociolinguistic rule before these stable patterns crystallise; subsequent structured practice in authentic interactional contexts must then develop the judgment layer that rule instruction alone cannot reach.

## §14.3 — Keigo in the SSW Context

The sociolinguistic competence challenge established in §14.1 and the acquisition challenges documented in §14.2 take on heightened institutional significance in the SSW (*Tokutei Ginou* / Specified Skilled Worker) workplace context. In the care, food service, construction, and agricultural workplaces that Indonesian SSW workers enter, keigo errors carry social costs that are disproportionate to their surface form and asymmetric to those experienced in general social interaction.

The asymmetry operates as follows. In a general social interaction between a native Japanese speaker and an evident non-native speaker, a register error — using plain form where polite form is socially expected, or failing to select sonkeigo when addressing a clear institutional superior — is noticed and evaluated internally by the native-speaker interlocutor but is typically managed through the face-saving attribution of linguistic limitation: the error is categorised as a *language problem*, not a *social intention problem*. The non-native speaker is granted a pragmatic exemption by virtue of their manifest L2 status. In a Japanese professional workplace, however, the categorisation mechanism shifts. Keigo encodes institutional position directly — each register selection is a real-time statement about the speaker's understanding of the social and hierarchical relationship with the interlocutor. A register error in this context does not merely sound impolite; it communicates that the speaker does not understand the institutional social order. The error is categorised as a *social competence problem*, with implications for trustworthiness, professional reliability, and suitability for the institutional role.

This social cost asymmetry positions keigo competence as a **professional credential** in Japanese workplace culture — a continuously performed signal of institutional understanding — not merely a politeness convention. In the care sector specifically, where the SSW worker is simultaneously subordinate to all supervisory nursing staff, responsible to residents and clients, and required to maintain professional dignity across every care interaction throughout the working day, this credentialling function of keigo is activated continuously. The compound register demand on the lowest-status entrant in any Japanese institutional hierarchy — maximum keigo upward to every interlocutor above, appropriate modulation in peer and resident interactions — is documented extensively in §15.5 [cross-reference: §15.5; VS-23 Niyekawa (1991)].

Blum-Kulka, House, and Kasper (1989) **[CP-07]**, in the foundational cross-cultural pragmatics study of how speakers across languages perform and interpret face-threatening acts — the Cross-Cultural Speech Act Realization Project (CCSARP) — demonstrate that **cross-cultural pragmatic transfer** produces systematic misreadings between L1 and L2 interactional norms that cannot be corrected by grammatical knowledge alone. Speakers apply their L1 pragmatic norms — the learned judgments about how much deference, indirectness, and face-work a given speech act requires — to L2 interactional contexts where those norms are institutionally inappropriate. For Indonesian SSW workers, whose L1 social register system has no structural equivalent to Japanese keigo (the Indonesian formal/informal distinction operates through lexical choice, discourse markers, and address-term selection, not through a morphologically encoded honorific grammar), pragmatic transfer from Indonesian produces exactly the calibration errors documented in §14.2: the Indonesian learner's L1-derived judgment about the appropriate level of formality for a workplace directive or care request is systematically miscalibrated for the Japanese institutional context, in both directions — insufficiently deferential upward, and over-formal in contexts where plain form would signal appropriate collegial solidarity.

*Cross-reference §8.11.3 (malu/face-work):* The Goffman-Brown-Levinson theoretical framework applied to malu in §8.11.3 is directly and substantially relevant to the keigo acquisition problem. In that framework [§8.11.3; Goffman (1967); Brown & Levinson (1987)], keigo is the Japanese institutional mechanism for face-preserving communication — the conventionalised set of linguistic forms through which speakers signal recognition of others' face needs, deference to institutional position, and membership in the social order. The face-work function of keigo, and its cross-cultural opacity to Indonesian learners who manage face through structurally different mechanisms (malu-driven self-effacement, indirect refusal, topic avoidance, and the highly contextualised use of address terms *Pak/Bu*), means that explicit keigo instruction must address not only the morphosyntactic forms but the sociopragmatic function those forms are performing. Indonesian learners' own culturally functional face-work strategies do not map onto keigo: a learner who manages face appropriately in Indonesian interactional contexts through *malu*-motivated deference is not thereby equipped to produce the specific linguistic forms through which that deference is institutionally recognised in Japanese. For the full theoretical framework, see §8.11.3.

*Cross-reference §15.5 (workplace communication):* Section 15.5 [SC-05, VS-23, VS-24] documents in detail the authentic register demands on SSW workers in Japanese care and food service settings, the asymmetrical register obligations imposed on the lowest-status entrant in Japanese institutional hierarchies, and the EPA monitoring research demonstrating the systematic mismatch between examination-level keigo and the register competence demanded in actual 介護 workplaces. The acquisition theory established in the present section provides the explanatory framework for the §15.5 empirical findings: EPA care workers documented in Ogawa (2012) [VS-24] fail to achieve calibrated keigo competence under workplace conditions not because they did not study the forms, but because examination preparation addressed only the teachable rule layer (§14.2; [SC-06]) without the structured authentic practice necessary to develop the non-rule-governed contextual judgment layer ([SC-05]; [SC-07]). The intervention implication — extended authentic practice in situated care-sector and food-service-sector keigo — is a design requirement for the Nugget Nihongo SSW deck that examination-prep materials cannot substitute for.

*Cross-reference §5.5.24 (pragmatic transfer):* The pragmatic transfer errors documented in §5.5.24 for Indonesian learners of Japanese — particularly in request, apology, and refusal speech acts — have direct keigo implications that should not be separately re-cited here but must be integrated into the instructional framework. Pragmatic transfer from Indonesian into Japanese in the honorific domain is not merely a case of an incomplete or inaccurate rule system; it is a case of a functionally adequate but structurally mismatched pragmatic system being applied to an L2 context where its assumptions are institutionally invalid.

Available research on keigo training for non-native workers in Japan is concentrated in the EPA care worker literature (§15.5 [SC-05, VS-23, VS-24]) and in the broader interlanguage pragmatics instruction research base. The consistent finding — both from EPA monitoring data and from pragmatics instruction research — is that passive acquisition of keigo through workplace exposure alone is insufficient to achieve the register calibration that Japanese professional contexts demand. Workers who arrive at Japanese care and food service workplaces with only examination-level keigo preparation consistently report persistent anxiety about register selection in high-stakes institutional interactions: shift reporting, resident care requests, communication with nursing supervisors and medical staff. This anxiety profile maps directly onto the *malu*-adjacent language anxiety mechanism documented in §8.11.3: register uncertainty in an institutionally visible context is a face-threatening condition that activates malu-type inhibition responses, and that inhibition exacerbates the performance gap. The anxious worker who doubts their register selection hesitates, uses a safe but socially marked form (invariant teineigo), and thereby produces exactly the hyperformal interlanguage pattern documented in §14.2 as the characteristic L2 keigo error.

Marriott (1995) **[SC-11]**, in a study of exchange students' acquisition of Japanese politeness patterns in naturalistic immersion conditions, found that extended authentic exposure — without explicit instruction — did not reliably produce native-like pragmatic calibration. Learners improved on specific high-frequency forms encountered repeatedly in highly scripted interactional contexts (shop service encounters, classroom greeting routines) but did not generalise to novel or less scripted institutional contexts requiring in-the-moment register judgment. For SSW workers, who must operate across both the fully scripted register demands of care-sector shift reporting and care request routines *and* the less scripted demands of collegial workplace interaction and unexpected communication events, this finding is particularly consequential. Explicit keigo instruction must cover both scripted and non-scripted institutional contexts, and both must be presented in their authentic sector-specific interactional forms.

**Design Implication for Nugget Nihongo:** The SSW deck's keigo module (flagged in §15.5 as requiring approximately 50–80 high-frequency register-marked patterns beyond standard JLPT N4 honorifics) must be grounded in the acquisition theory and institutional context established in §14.1–14.3. Specifically:

1. **Theoretical framing:** The module must present keigo as sociolinguistic competence [SC-02, SC-04] — a coordinate dimension of language ability — not as a grammar chapter or cultural supplement appended to the real instructional content.

2. **Instruction design:** Explicit rule instruction must target the teachable layer — morphosyntactic transformations, basic sonkeigo/kenjōgo distributional rules [SC-06] — while explicitly marking the non-rule-governed contextual judgment layer as a target of structured authentic practice [SC-05, SC-07].

3. **Card design:** Keigo items must be presented in their authentic interactional contexts — shift-report formulae, elder care request structures, supervisor address forms, client interaction routines for food service. Keterangan Tambahan notes must include contrastive information marking the social cost of register error in that specific context; these cards cannot present keigo as morphological variants of neutral vocabulary.

4. **UX framing (malu alignment):** Register error in a private SRS review session is not a face-threatening act — the error is witnessed only by the algorithm, in the manner documented in §8.11.3 [Brown & Levinson; Markus & Kitayama; Goffman]. The deck is structurally the correct environment in which to make — and explicitly understand — the keigo calibration errors that would be professionally costly in the authentic workplace. This affordance should be communicated to the learner as a feature of the learning design, not left implicit.

## §14.4 — INTERLANGUAGE PRAGMATICS: THE FIELD

### Narasi Teori

Pada akhir 1980-an, penelitian SLA (Second Language Acquisition) secara dominan mengkaji morfosintaks interlanguage: bagaimana fitur gramatikal berkembang, fossilisasi di mana, error apa yang muncul dalam urutan apa. Asumsi implisit yang sering tidak diartikulasikan adalah bahwa jika learner menguasai grammar, pragmatic competence akan menyusul secara natural. Kasper dan Blum-Kulka (1993) menantang asumsi ini secara sistematis: mereka memetakan **interlanguage pragmatics (ILP)** sebagai bidang penelitian tersendiri, dengan pertanyaan riset, metodologi, dan temuan yang tidak dapat direduksi ke studi morfosintaks.

ILP meneliti bagaimana learner L2 memahami dan memproduksi tindak bahasa secara kontekstual — bukan sekadar gramatikal. Pertanyaan sentralnya bukan "apakah kalimat ini benar secara gramatikal?" melainkan "apakah kalimat ini tepat untuk konteks sosial ini, dengan hubungan power dan distance ini, dengan goal komunikatif ini?" Untuk pelajar bahasa Jepang, ini berarti perbedaan antara *sumimasen* dan *moushiwake gozaimasen* bukan hanya perbedaan register (yang sudah dijelaskan dalam §14 Part 1) melainkan juga perbedaan pragmatik yang mendalam: kapan ungkapan yang secara gramatikal benar menjadi tidak tepat secara sosial dan sebaliknya.

Kasper dan Rose (2002) memberikan sintesis komprehensif tentang **bagaimana pragmatic competence berkembang** dalam akuisisi L2 — dan temuannya mengejutkan. Tiga generalisasi utama dari corpus Kasper dan Rose: Pertama, perkembangan pragmatik *lebih lambat* dari perkembangan gramatikal dan sering *tertinggal jauh di belakang* — learner yang sudah fasih secara gramatikal dapat masih secara konsisten melakukan pragmatic failure. Kedua, perkembangan pragmatik sangat *terpengaruh oleh instruction* — paparan natural saja (immersion atau input yang banyak tanpa perhatian ke pragmatik) tidak menghasilkan perkembangan pragmatik yang sebanding dengan instruction eksplisit. Ketiga, pragmatic transfer dari L1 adalah fenomena robust yang tidak hilang bahkan pada learner tingkat lanjut — ini bukan artefak proficiency rendah, melainkan fitur akuisisi yang stabil.

Bardovi-Harlig (2001) memberikan bukti empiris paling langsung bahwa instruction diperlukan untuk pragmatik. Dalam studinya tentang pelajar ESL di program intensif, ia menemukan bahwa meskipun learner menerima input yang melimpah dan berinteraksi secara regular dengan penutur asli, mereka gagal mengembangkan kompetensi pragmatik yang setara dengan penutur asli dalam domain request, apology, dan refusal. Learner ini tahu *bahwa* ekspresi tertentu ada (mereka telah mendengarnya), tetapi tidak tahu *kapan* menggunakannya — distinsi antara pengetahuan linguistik (form) dan pengetahuan pragmatik (kondisi kepatutan penggunaan).

Untuk pelajar bahasa Indonesia yang belajar bahasa Jepang, kombinasi temuan Kasper & Rose dan Bardovi-Harlig memiliki implikasi spesifik. Keigo bukan hanya sistem honorifik yang perlu dipelajari — itu adalah sistem pragmatik yang memerlukan penguasaan tidak hanya *form* tetapi juga *conditions of use*: siapa berbicara dengan siapa, dalam konteks institusional apa, dengan goal komunikatif apa, dalam situasi formal atau informal. Input yang banyak tanpa explicit instruction tentang kondisi penggunaan ini tidak akan menghasilkan keigo competence yang fungsional.

Cross-section ke §5.5.24 [CP cluster]: temuan ILP tentang transfer pragmatik yang persisten mengkonfirmasi prediksi kontrastif §5.5.24 — pragmatic transfer Indonesia ↔ Jepang bukan hanya soal perbedaan form tetapi perbedaan *assumption* tentang kapan dan bagaimana politeness diungkapkan.

### Design Implications untuk Nugget Nihongo

1. **Kartu pragmatik adalah kategori tersendiri, bukan subset kartu grammar.** Berdasarkan Kasper & Rose (SC-08), pragmatic competence berkembang dengan lintasan berbeda dari grammatical competence. Kartu yang mengajarkan *kapan* menggunakan sebuah ekspresi (conditions of use) — bukan hanya *apa* artinya — harus ada sebagai kategori sendiri dalam deck, bukan hanya sebagai catatan tambahan dalam kartu kosakata.

2. **Explicit instruction tentang pragmatics adalah non-optional.** Bardovi-Harlig (SC-16) membuktikan bahwa bahkan learner dengan input yang sangat banyak (program intensif, interaksi native-speaker regular) gagal mengembangkan pragmatik setara tanpa instruction eksplisit. Ini memperkuat justifikasi theoretical untuk *Keterangan Tambahan* field dalam kartu nihongo — bukan hiasan, melainkan pragmatic instruction yang tertanam.

3. **Contrastive pragmatic notes harus membahas conditions of use, bukan hanya form.** Kasper (SC-17) menunjukkan bahwa pragmatic transfer persists independent dari grammatical transfer — learner yang sudah benar secara gramatikal masih bisa melakukan pragmatic failure karena mereka menerapkan norma L1 (kapan, bagaimana, seberapa polite) ke ekspresi L2. Kartu keigo tidak cukup hanya mengajarkan morfologi horific — harus mengajarkan *kondisi sosial* yang mewajibkan form tersebut.

### Cross-Section Integration Notes

- **§14.4 → §5.5.24 [CP cluster]:** §5.5.24 mendokumentasikan transfer pragmatik spesifik Indonesia ↔ Jepang (request, apology, refusal strategies). §14.4 memberikan theoretical framework yang menjelaskan *mengapa* transfer ini terjadi dan persists: pragmatic transfer adalah fenomena yang berbeda dari grammatical transfer (SC-17) dan lebih resistant terhadap paparan natural (SC-08, SC-16).
- **§14.4 → §12 [KR-09, OT-02–OT-05]:** Swain (1985, §12.1) menunjukkan bahwa pushed output mendorong noticing gap antara interlanguage dan target. Untuk pragmatics, "gap" yang perlu di-notice bukan hanya gramatikal tetapi juga pragmatik — learner perlu aware bahwa ekspresi mereka, meskipun benar secara gramatikal, mungkin tidak tepat secara pragmatik.
- **§14.4 → §5.6 [EA cluster]:** Error Analysis (§5.6) mengklasifikasikan error termasuk pragmatic errors. §14.4 memperluas landasan teoritis untuk mengapa pragmatic errors memerlukan treatment yang berbeda dari grammatical errors — dan mengapa they are more persistent.

## §14.5 — PRAGMATIC FAILURE TYPOLOGY

### Narasi Teori

Tidak semua kegagalan pragmatik adalah sama, dan perbedaannya penting secara pedagogis. Thomas (1983) memperkenalkan distinsi yang menjadi salah satu yang paling berpengaruh dalam linguistik terapan: **sociopragmatic failure** versus **pragmalinguistic failure**. Distinsi ini langsung mengimplikasikan strategi intervensi pedagogis yang berbeda — dan untuk desain kartu SRS, ia menetapkan dua kategori kartu yang berbeda.

**Sociopragmatic failure** terjadi ketika learner membuat pilihan *pragmatik yang salah untuk konteks* — mereka menggunakan level politeness yang tidak tepat, menolak ketika seharusnya menerima (atau sebaliknya), menerapkan norma sosial L1 ke situasi L2 di mana norma yang berlaku berbeda. Kesalahan ini bukan tentang form: learner mungkin menggunakan ekspresi yang benar secara gramatikal dan bahkan terdengar polite dalam norma L1 mereka — tetapi salah dalam konteks L2 karena mereka tidak memiliki *pengetahuan budaya* yang diperlukan untuk menilai situasi dengan benar. Contoh dalam konteks Jepang: seorang pelajar Indonesia yang merespons pujian atasan dengan mengucapkan *arigatou gozaimasu* dan membenarkan pujian tersebut (*sou desu ne, hontou ni yoku dekimashita*) — terlihat sopan dalam norma Indonesia tetapi pragmatically inappropriate dalam konteks Jepang di mana ketidakrendahan hati terhadap atasan adalah pelanggaran sosial yang serius. Mereka harus menyangkal (*iie, mada mada desu*) — respons yang berlawanan dengan intuisi mereka.

**Pragmalinguistic failure** terjadi ketika learner memiliki *tujuan komunikatif yang benar* tetapi menggunakan *form yang salah* untuk mengekspresikannya. Mereka tahu bahwa situasi ini memerlukan ungkapan tertentu (misalnya, permintaan tidak langsung yang polite), tetapi mereka tidak menguasai form L2 yang konvensional untuk itu — dan menggunakan ekspresi yang, meskipun sintaktis benar, tidak diakui sebagai bentuk permintaan polite oleh penutur asli, atau bahkan terdengar kasar. Contoh: pelajar yang ingin meminta bantuan atasan dan menggunakan *tetsudatte kudasai* (sedikit lebih direct dari yang konteks ini izinkan) alih-alih *tetsudatte itadakemasu ka* atau *tetsudatte itadakeru to arigatai no desu ga* — form yang benar secara gramatikal tetapi tidak mencapai level of indirectness yang dipersyaratkan secara pragmatik.

Implikasi pedagogis dari distinsi Thomas adalah langsung. **Sociopragmatic failure memerlukan pengetahuan budaya** — learner perlu memahami *asumsi sosial* yang mendasari praktik bahasa Jepang: hierarki, konsep amae, ketidakrendahan hati yang konvensional (*kenjō*), dan norma-norma kontekstual yang tidak terlihat dari form bahasa itu sendiri. Intervensi pedagogis yang tepat adalah *kartu budaya* — kartu yang mengajarkan pengetahuan sosiokultural yang diperlukan untuk memilih respons yang tepat. **Pragmalinguistic failure memerlukan penguasaan form** — learner perlu drilling form-form konvensional untuk mengekspresikan speech act tertentu. Intervensi pedagogis yang tepat adalah *form-drilling* — kartu yang melatih ekspresi-ekspresi konvensional untuk request, apology, refusal, dan speech act lainnya secara berulang.

Leech (1983) memberikan landasan teoritis lebih luas untuk memetakan politeness principles yang beroperasi dalam interaksi — Maxim of Tact, Maxim of Generosity, dan seterusnya. Sementara Leech mengembangkan framework ini dalam konteks pragmatik Inggris, framework-nya telah diadaptasi untuk memahami bagaimana politeness diperhitungkan secara lintas bahasa, termasuk Jepang (Brown & Levinson, 1987 — pada dasarnya face-theory yang kompatibel dengan Leech). Yang penting untuk desain Nugget Nihongo: politeness principles tidak universal — Japanese politeness calculation melibatkan variabel yang berbeda dari Indonesian politeness calculation, dan learner yang tidak mengetahui perbedaan ini akan secara konsisten under- atau over-polite dalam konteks yang berbeda.

Bardovi-Harlig dan Dörnyei (1998) menyediakan data empiris tentang *kesadaran* pragmatic violation pada learner L2. Dalam studi mereka, learner EFL di lingkungan classroom memiliki kesadaran yang jauh lebih rendah tentang pragmatic violations dibandingkan dengan learner ESL di immersion environment — bahkan ketika proficiency gramatikal dikendalikan. Lebih mengejutkan: learner (terutama EFL) lebih aware terhadap grammatical errors daripada pragmatic violations, sementara penutur asli sebaliknya — mereka lebih toleran terhadap grammatical errors tetapi sangat aware terhadap pragmatic violations. Ini adalah asimetri yang fatal dalam konteks pembelajaran: learner memprioritaskan apa yang *tidak* diprioritaskan penutur asli, dan sebaliknya.

Untuk pelajar bahasa Indonesia yang menggunakan Nugget Nihongo dalam konteks self-study (bukan classroom EFL), awareness gap ini harus diatasi secara proaktif dalam desain kartu: kartu harus *membuat pragmatic violations visible* dengan cara yang tidak dilakukan oleh kartu kosakata konvensional.

### Design Implications untuk Nugget Nihongo

1. **Dua tipe failure Thomas (SC-19) menghasilkan dua tipe kartu yang berbeda.** Sociopragmatic failure → kartu budaya (*Keterangan Tambahan* berisi penjelasan norma sosial dan konteks penggunaan). Dichotomy ini bukan sekadar kategorisasi akademis — ia langsung menetapkan apa yang harus ada di *front card* versus *back card* sebuah kartu pragmatik.

2. **Front card harus membuat pragmatic context visible.** Berdasarkan Bardovi-Harlig & Dörnyei (SC-21), learner secara natural tidak aware terhadap pragmatic violations sampai mereka dibuat aware. Kartu yang hanya menampilkan "bagaimana mengatakan X" tanpa menampilkan "dalam konteks sosial apa X tepat" melatih pragmalinguistic competence saja dan tidak mengatasi sociopragmatic failure. Front card yang ideal untuk kartu pragmatik harus mengandung *context cue* — situasi, relasi power, tingkat formalitas — bukan hanya vocabulary item.

3. **Keigo cards harus mengatasi kedua tipe failure.** Kesalahan pelajar Indonesia dalam keigo bukan hanya tentang salah menggunakan morphology horific (pragmalinguistic) — tetapi juga tentang tidak tahu kapan konteks sosial mewajibkan keigo sama sekali (sociopragmatic). Kartu yang hanya mengajarkan sonkeigo/kenjōgo paradigma morfologis (pragmalinguistic drilling) tidak cukup; kartu yang mengajarkan konteks sosial (rapat, percakapan dengan atasan, permintaan ke pelanggan) tanpa form adalah incomplete dari arah lain.

4. **Cross-reference §12 [OT-06–OT-08]:** Schmidt's Noticing Hypothesis (§12.2) menjelaskan *mengapa* pragmatic violations perlu di-make-visible: tanpa conscious attention ke pragmatic norms, learner tidak akan mengakuisisi norma tersebut dari input natural. Kartu pragmatik yang mengeksplisitkan *norma* (bukan hanya form) adalah noticing trigger untuk sociopragmatic competence.

## §14.6 — SPEECH ACT ACQUISITION AND SRS DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

### Narasi Teori

Speech acts — tindak bahasa yang melakukan sesuatu (meminta, memohon maaf, menolak, mengucapkan selamat, mengancam) — adalah unit pragmatik yang paling langsung terkena dampak dari gap antara L1 dan L2 pragmatic competence. Bagaimana speech acts diakuisisi dalam L2 Jepang, dan apa yang dapat SRS lakukan untuk mendukung akuisisi tersebut?

Blum-Kulka, House, dan Kasper (1989) memberikan metodologi dasar untuk penelitian speech act lintas bahasa melalui **Cross-Language Speech Act Research Project (CCSARP)**. CCSARP mengumpulkan data dari delapan bahasa dan dialek menggunakan *Discourse Completion Tasks (DCTs)* — kuesioner yang memberikan situasi tertulis dan meminta responden menuliskan apa yang akan mereka katakan. Metodologi ini, meskipun kemudian dikritik karena gap antara DCT data dan actual speech production, memberikan peta pertama yang sistematis tentang bagaimana request dan apology strategies bervariasi lintas bahasa. Untuk penelitian L2 Jepang, CCSARP menetapkan benchmark untuk apa yang dianggap "native-like" pragmatic behavior dan bagaimana learner menyimpang darinya.

Temuan kunci CCSARP yang relevan untuk konteks Indonesia-Jepang: **indirectness dalam request** bervariasi secara dramatis lintas bahasa. Bahasa-bahasa yang diteliti berbeda bukan hanya dalam *form* request tetapi dalam *default level of directness* yang dianggap sopan. Bahasa Jepang berada pada ujung high-indirectness spectrum — bahkan permintaan yang sederhana menggunakan conventionalized indirect forms (*~te itadakemasu ka*, *~te moratte mo yoroshi deshou ka*) yang tidak memiliki padanan satu-ke-satu dalam bahasa Indonesia. Bahasa Indonesia, dalam konteks CCSARP, jatuh pada middle range — lebih langsung dari Jepang tetapi lebih tidak langsung dari beberapa bahasa Eropa. Ini memprediksi bahwa Indonesian learners of Japanese akan secara sistematis *under-indirect* dalam request — pragmatic transfer yang tidak dapat dikoreksi hanya dengan menambah paparan input.

Rose (2000) memberikan studi cross-sectional yang melacak perkembangan speech act acquisition dalam L2 Jepang secara spesifik. Dalam studinya dengan pelajar bahasa Jepang di Hong Kong, Rose menemukan bahwa perkembangan request dan apology strategies mengikuti *pola staged* yang dapat diprediksi: learner awal menggunakan strategi yang sangat langsung (direct requests, bare apologies), learner tingkat menengah mengembangkan repertoire yang lebih luas tetapi sering *over-applies* strategi tertentu, dan learner lanjutan mendekati native-like variability — kemampuan untuk memilih strategi yang tepat berdasarkan konteks sosial. Temuan Rose menegaskan bahwa perkembangan speech act dalam L2 bukan gradual improvement dalam satu dimensi, tetapi *staged restructuring* dari sistem pragmatik learner — analogous dengan staged acquisition dalam morfosintaks (Processability Theory dalam §5.5.28) tetapi dalam domain yang berbeda.

Taguchi (2011) menawarkan sintesis tentang *teaching pragmatics* yang relevan untuk mengidentifikasi apa yang SRS dapat dan tidak dapat lakukan. Berdasarkan review Taguchi dari literatur instruction-in-pragmatics:

**SRS dapat mendrill:** form-form konvensional untuk speech acts tertentu (*sumimasen, moushiwake gozaimasen, ~te itadakemasu ka*, dll.), recognition distinsi antara context types yang memerlukan strategi berbeda, dan explicit knowledge tentang pragmatic rules. Ini adalah domain yang tepat untuk Anki cards — *pragmalinguistic competence* dalam terminologi Thomas (§14.5).

**SRS tidak dapat sepenuhnya menyediakan:** output practice yang diperlukan untuk mentransfer explicit knowledge ke spontaneous production, negotiation-of-meaning experience yang terjadi dalam real interaksi, dan feedback dari penutur asli yang mengkonfirmasi atau menolak pragmatic choices. Ini adalah domain yang harus dikompensasi oleh mode pembelajaran lain — interaksi, output practice, shadowing, dan penggunaan nyata.

Cohen (1996) menunjukkan bahwa *ability to perform speech acts* dalam L2 adalah kemampuan yang dapat dilatih secara explicit, tetapi pembedaan antara *knowledge* dan *performance* harus dipertahankan. Learner dapat mengetahui bahwa dalam konteks X, ekspresi Y lebih tepat dari Z — ini adalah explicit knowledge yang dapat dibangun oleh SRS. Tetapi kemampuan untuk mendeploykan Y secara spontan dalam real-time conversation, tanpa pause untuk retrieval, memerlukan proceduralization (dalam terminologi DeKeyser, §12.4 [OT-20–OT-21]) yang hanya datang dari practice produksi yang berulang.

Roever (2011) menyediakan perspektif dari testing pragmatics yang relevan untuk desain kartu SRS. Apa yang dapat diukur secara reliable sebagai pragmatic competence? Roever mengidentifikasi tiga komponen testable: *implicature comprehension* (memahami apa yang dimaksud bukan hanya apa yang dikatakan), *routine formulae knowledge* (mengetahui ekspresi konvensional untuk konteks tertentu), dan *speech act production* (kemampuan menghasilkan speech act yang tepat). Untuk SRS, ini mengimplikasikan tiga tipe kartu yang berbeda, masing-masing dengan stimulus dan expected response yang berbeda: kartu implicature, kartu routine formulae recognition, dan kartu production prompts.

### Design Implications untuk Nugget Nihongo

1. **Tiga tipe kartu pragmatik, berdasarkan Roever (SC-28):**
   - *Implicature cards*: Front card = situasi + ujaran (sering tidak langsung); Back card = apa yang dimaksud + penjelasan indirectness convention Jepang. Melatih comprehension pragmatik.
   - *Routine formulae cards*: Front card = konteks sosial (misalnya, "baru saja menerima sesuatu dari atasan"); Back card = ekspresi konvensional yang tepat (*itadakimasu, osoreirimasu*, dll.) + kapan masing-masing digunakan. Melatih recognition dan explicit knowledge.
   - *Speech act production prompts*: Front card = situasi + role (misalnya, "Kamu bawahan, atasan baru saja membantu kamu dengan pekerjaan — ucapkan terima kasih yang tepat"); Back card = contoh ekspresi native-like + spectrum dari terlalu formal hingga terlalu kasual. Melatih production awareness.

2. **SRS covers explicit knowledge; output practice covers proceduralization — dan Nugget harus transparan tentang ini.** Berdasarkan Cohen (SC-27) dan Taguchi (SC-26), ada batas yang tidak dapat dilewati SRS dalam speech act acquisition: explicit knowledge tentang pragmatic appropriateness dapat dibangun melalui card-based learning, tetapi kemampuan mendeploykan speech acts secara spontan dalam real-time interaction memerlukan output practice yang melampaui Anki. Methodology page Nugget harus secara jujur mengkomunikasikan ini — SRS adalah fondasi, bukan ceiling.

3. **Cross-reference §12 [KR-09, OT-02–OT-05]:** Swain's Output Hypothesis langsung relevan di sini. "Failed speech act attempt" — ketika learner mencoba mengekspresikan sesuatu dalam bahasa Jepang dan menerima reaksi yang tidak diharapkan (kebingungan, rasa tidak nyaman dari lawan bicara) — adalah pushed output event yang menciptakan noticing gap. SRS tidak dapat mensimulasikan ini secara autentik, tetapi dapat menyiapkan learner untuk mengenali ketika gap terjadi.

4. **Staged progression Rose (SC-24) mengimplikasikan card sequencing.** Speech act cards untuk Indonesian learners of Japanese tidak harus diperkenalkan semuanya sekaligus. Berdasarkan pola staged progression Rose, card sequencing yang optimal adalah: (a) recognize direct vs. indirect request spectrum → (b) drill conventional indirect forms → (c) practice selecting form by context (power, distance, imposition level). Ini adalah tiga level card yang berbeda, bukan satu set kartu yang homogen.

5. **CCSARP (CP-07) baseline mengkonfirmasi gap directness Indonesia ↔ Jepang.** Data lintas bahasa Blum-Kulka et al. mengkonfirmasi prediksi kontrastif §5.5.24: Indonesian learners of Japanese are systematically under-indirect dalam request. Ini menetapkan prioritas konten: kartu indirect request strategies harus ada dalam deck sebagai kategori bervolume tinggi, bukan hanya sebagai scattered additions.

Cohen (SC-27) mengkonfirmasi ini spesifik untuk speech acts. Cross-reference memperkuat argumen bahwa SRS adalah necessary but not sufficient untuk speech act mastery.

- **§14.6 → §1 [SRS, AR cluster]:** FSRS's retrieval scheduling dapat secara optimal menjadwalkan pragmatic routine formulae cards dengan spacing interval yang sesuai — berdasarkan Kim & Webb (2022, §1) tentang spacing effect untuk L2 vocabulary, prinsip yang sama berlaku untuk pragmatic formulae sebagai "chunks" yang perlu diakuisisi.
- **§14.6 → §15 [VS cluster]:** Untuk konteks SSW (Specified Skilled Worker), speech act competence dalam workplace Japanese adalah kebutuhan komunikatif langsung — *request ke atasan, apology setelah kesalahan kerja, refusal yang sopan*. §14.6 memberikan theoretical backing untuk pentingnya konten speech act dalam deck vokasional.
