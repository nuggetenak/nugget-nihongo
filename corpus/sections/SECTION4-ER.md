## §4.1 — Extensive Reading: Foundational Framework

### Core Theoretical Background

Extensive reading (ER) is a language learning activity defined not by what is read but by *how* and *why* it is read. The foundational distinction — established through thirty years of empirical work — is between reading for *meaning and experience* (ER) and reading for *analysis and form* (intensive reading, IR). A learner reading a graded manga story to find out what happens is doing ER. A learner annotating the same page to analyze transitive verb forms is doing IR. Both have value; they serve different acquisition functions; and conflating them undermines both.

The field's most influential framework for ER pedagogy emerged from Day and Bamford's foundational work, which synthesized available research into ten operational principles. The principles collectively describe a learning environment, not a single technique: ER works through *volume*, *ease*, *autonomy*, and *continuity* — features that are systematically absent from most classroom-based L2 instruction.

The theoretical underpinning for *why* ER produces acquisition gains connects directly to the Comprehensible Input framework (§1.6, CI-01–CI-05). However, Krashen's later work — particularly *The Power of Reading* — extends the CI hypothesis into a specific claim about *free voluntary reading*: that self-selected, pleasurable reading is the single most powerful input activity available to L2 learners, and that the pleasure dimension (affective engagement, reader autonomy, intrinsic motivation) is not a luxury but a mechanism. Forced or assessed reading suppresses the affective filter and undermines the acquisition that should accompany comprehensible input. §4.1 does not re-cite the Input Hypothesis papers (CI-01–CI-05); it extends Krashen's framework to its reading-specific application.

**[ER-01]** **Day, R. R., & Bamford, J. (1998).** *Extensive reading in the second language classroom.* Cambridge University Press.

- **Core contribution:** The foundational monograph for ER pedagogy. Day and Bamford synthesize empirical research on L2 reading to articulate ER's theoretical basis and practical requirements. The book's most-cited contribution is the formulation of the features that define ER as a distinct practice: learners read large quantities of material, they choose their own material, material is at or below their current level, reading is its own reward (not a vehicle for post-reading tasks), teachers model and facilitate rather than test, and individual differences in pace and preference are accommodated. The book establishes ER as a strand of language education that addresses what traditional instruction systematically neglects: fluency, motivation, incidental vocabulary growth, and reading stamina. The 1998 monograph is the source of the "ten principles" framework that was later condensed in the 2002 article (ER-02).
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Positions Nugget's SRS-based vocabulary study as serving ER's prerequisite (building the lexical threshold), not as competing with ER. The methodology page should explicitly recommend ER as the natural complement to Nugget's study activities, citing Day & Bamford's framework for what to read and why.

**[ER-02]** **Day, R. R., & Bamford, J. (2002).** Top ten principles for teaching extensive reading. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 14*(2), 136–141. Retrieved from https://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

- **Core contribution:** The compact, widely cited distillation of the ten principles from ER-01. The principles: (1) reading material is easy; (2) a variety of reading material is available; (3) learners choose what they read; (4) learners read as much as possible; (5) the purpose is pleasure, information, and general understanding; (6) reading is its own reward; (7) reading speed is fast; (8) reading is individual and silent; (9) teachers orient and guide learners; (10) the teacher is a reading role model. The paper provides the operational checklist against which any ER implementation can be evaluated. Crucially, graded readers — the primary vehicle for ER in a Japanese context — satisfy all ten principles only if they are at genuinely comfortable comprehension levels (≥98% text coverage; see §4.2).
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The ten principles serve as the design specification for any ER track integrated into the platform. A learner who has reached the 3,000-word-family threshold (§4.3, cross-reference §CA.2) is ready for NHK Web Easy and lower-level graded readers; this paper provides the criteria for what makes those materials ER rather than intensive study.

**[ER-03]** **Krashen, S. D. (2004).** *The power of reading: Insights from the research* (2nd ed.). Libraries Unlimited.

- **Core contribution:** *The Power of Reading* is Krashen's most direct and sustained argument for free voluntary reading (FVR) as the primary driver of L2 literacy development. It is a distinct work from the Input Hypothesis papers (CI-01–CI-05) and extends Krashen's framework in two important directions: first, by marshaling evidence specifically from reading contexts (not listening, speaking, or classroom instruction); second, by foregrounding the *affective* dimension — arguing that reading done under compulsion or with attached assessment produces measurably worse acquisition outcomes than equivalent time spent in genuinely voluntary reading. The most practically significant claim is the convergent evidence across studies that FVR outperforms traditional grammar instruction and skill-building approaches for developing reading fluency, vocabulary breadth, and writing quality simultaneously. The FVR hypothesis is both a claim about input modality and a claim about motivational structure.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The FVR hypothesis provides the theoretical grounding for Nugget's positioning: deliberate SRS-based vocabulary study (Nugget's core activity) builds the lexical foundation that makes FVR possible and productive. Without sufficient vocabulary, reading is not voluntary and not pleasurable — it is laborious decoding. With sufficient vocabulary (§4.2), FVR becomes the most efficient vocabulary growth and literacy development activity available. Nugget accelerates the path to FVR; FVR is the destination. The methodology page should communicate this arc explicitly.

**[ER-04]** **Elley, W. B. (1991).** Acquiring literacy in a second language: The effect of book-based programs. *Language Learning, 41*(3), 375–411. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-1770.1991.tb00611.x

- **Core contribution:** One of the earliest large-scale empirical studies of ER outcomes in naturalistic classroom conditions. Elley examined "book flood" programs in Fiji and Singapore — contexts where L2 learners received sustained access to high-interest reading materials without formal instruction attached. Learners in book-flood conditions showed significantly greater gains in reading comprehension, listening comprehension, oral proficiency, and writing quality compared to matched classes receiving traditional instruction. Crucially, the book-flood gains emerged *without explicit vocabulary instruction* — the vocabulary growth was incidental, acquired through reading volume rather than formal study. This finding — that vocabulary can be acquired incidentally at scale through extensive reading — is the empirical basis for ER's role in the lexical development arc described in §4.3.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Elley (1991) provides the first large-scale demonstration that reading volume, not instruction intensity, is the primary driver of L2 literacy gains. For Nugget's methodology page, this supports the claim that beyond a vocabulary threshold (~3,000 word families; §4.3), the most efficient study activity shifts from deliberate SRS review to engaged reading — not because SRS stops working, but because reading volume begins to deliver qualitatively broader acquisition outcomes per unit of time.

**Design Implication (§4.1):** The ER framework is not an alternative to Nugget's SRS-based approach — it is ER's prerequisite. Day & Bamford's principle that reading material must be easy (≥98% text coverage; §4.2) means that learners cannot do genuine ER until they have sufficient vocabulary. Krashen's FVR hypothesis adds the critical affective dimension: the moment reading becomes laborious, acquisition gains drop sharply. Nugget's role in the learner arc is to accelerate vocabulary development to the threshold at which ER becomes possible, pleasurable, and productive. The platform's onboarding and milestone communications should frame vocabulary acquisition as building toward reading freedom — not as an end in itself.

## §4.2 — The Lexical Threshold for Reading Comprehension

### Core Theoretical Background

Perhaps the most practically actionable finding in the L2 reading literature is the identification of specific vocabulary thresholds that determine whether a reader can comprehend text independently. This is not a gradient relationship — comprehension does not scale linearly with vocabulary. At 90% text coverage, approximately 1 in every 10 words is unknown; meaning collapses. At 95% coverage, approximately 1 in 20 words is unknown; reading is labored but partially possible. At 98% coverage, approximately 1 in 50 words is unknown; reading is genuinely independent. Below 98%, the cognitive load of unknown word processing interferes with global comprehension; above 98%, readers can tolerate the remaining unknown items through contextual inference without losing the thread.

This threshold finding has direct implications for two of Nugget Nihongo's core design decisions: (1) the size of the vocabulary deck necessary before learners are recommended to begin ER with particular materials; and (2) the grading criteria for recommending NHK Web Easy, manga, graded readers, and novels at each proficiency level. Vocabulary size targets described in §CA.2 (cross-reference) are grounded empirically in this coverage literature — not in JLPT categories, which are pedagogical constructs, not comprehension-calibrated thresholds.

**[ER-05]** ⚠️ **FLAG: POTENTIAL DUPLICATE OF BC-08** **Hu, M., & Nation, I. S. P. (2000).** Unknown vocabulary density and reading comprehension. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 13*(1), 403–430. Retrieved from https://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

- **⚠️ Anti-duplication note:** BC-08 (§1.12) covers this paper. If BC-08 is the same Hu & Nation (2000), Agent 38 should consolidate: use BC-08 for §1 context and ER-05 for §4 context, or collapse to BC-08 with cross-reference. Citing here because the §4.2 treatment requires its methods and findings directly, not merely a passing reference.
- **Core contribution:** The most-cited empirical study of text coverage thresholds. Hu and Nation measured reading comprehension at four vocabulary coverage levels — 80%, 90%, 95%, and 100% — using specially constructed texts with controlled unknown word proportions. Participants (university EFL learners) demonstrated that 80% and 90% coverage produced near-chance comprehension; 95% coverage yielded partial but unstable comprehension; only at 98% coverage (extrapolated from their findings) did genuine independent comprehension become reliably possible. The study formalized what Nation had theorized: the 95% figure widely cited in the pre-2000 literature is *insufficient* for independent reading. The true threshold is higher — approximately 98%, meaning no more than 1 unknown word per 50 running tokens.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Defines the coverage standard against which all vocabulary level recommendations must be calibrated. A learner with 3,000 word families achieves approximately 95% coverage of general Japanese text — still below the 98% threshold. A learner with 6,000–8,000 word families approaches 98% coverage for newspapers and novels. These thresholds, combined with the vocabulary size estimates in §CA.2 (cross-reference Nation, 2006; Schmitt & Schmitt, 2014), provide the empirical basis for Nugget's per-level reading recommendations.

**[ER-06]** **Laufer, B. (1989).** What percentage of text-lexis is essential for comprehension? In C. Lauren & M. Nordman (Eds.), *Special language: From humans thinking to thinking machines* (pp. 316–323). Multilingual Matters.

- **Core contribution:** The first systematic attempt to quantify vocabulary coverage requirements for reading comprehension. Laufer proposed the "95% coverage threshold" from corpus-based analysis of EFL reading data — arguing that readers needed to know at least 95% of the words in a text to achieve adequate comprehension. This 1989 paper established the research tradition that Hu & Nation (2000) would later revise upward. Laufer's 95% figure influenced vocabulary pedagogy through the 1990s; its revision to 98% (Hu & Nation, 2000, ER-05) is one of the most practically consequential findings in the field. Laufer (1989) is distinct from Laufer (1992), which is already cited in §CA.1 (vocabulary threshold for comfortable reading, 5,000 word families). The 1989 paper addresses the *coverage* question; the 1992 paper addresses the *vocabulary size* question.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Provides the historical baseline from which current 98% consensus evolved. Citing both Laufer (1989) and Hu & Nation (2000, ER-05) demonstrates methodological progression: the field moved from "approximately 95%" to "empirically, 98%" through controlled experimentation. The methodology page can use this progression to show that vocabulary size recommendations are evidence-based, not arbitrary.

**[ER-07]** **Liu, N., & Nation, I. S. P. (1985).** Factors affecting guessing vocabulary in context. *RELC Journal, 16*(1), 33–42. https://doi.org/10.1177/003368828501600103

- **Core contribution:** Nation's empirical examination of when readers can successfully infer unknown vocabulary from context. Liu and Nation found that successful contextual guessing requires not only high text coverage but also specific discourse-level clues — the unknown word must be supported by accessible surrounding context. Critically, guessing accuracy collapsed when the surrounding text itself contained multiple unknown items — even at 95% coverage, the unknown words are not randomly distributed, meaning dense clusters of unknowns produce local comprehension failures disproportionate to the overall coverage statistic. The paper provides a *mechanism* for why the 98% threshold (ER-05) is not merely about raw density but about the cognitive conditions needed for successful inference.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Explains *why* the lexical threshold matters and cannot be circumvented by telling learners to "guess from context." Contextual guessing is a legitimate reading strategy (§4.4), but it requires contextual support — which requires high coverage. Nugget's onboarding communication should set honest expectations: "The vocabulary gap you're closing with Nugget is the prerequisite for reading strategies to work. Strategies don't substitute for vocabulary; they work *because* you have vocabulary."

**[ER-08]** **Carver, R. P. (1994).** Percentage of unknown vocabulary words in text as a function of the relative difficulty of the text: Implications for instruction. *Journal of Reading Behavior, 26*(4), 413–437. https://doi.org/10.1080/10862969409547861

- **Core contribution:** Carver's contribution to the coverage threshold literature comes from a different methodological tradition — psychometric analysis of reading difficulty in native readers rather than L2 learners. Carver found that text difficulty, as experienced by native readers, is overwhelmingly predicted by the percentage of low-frequency (unknown) vocabulary in the text. Even for native readers, texts with more than 2% unknown words (i.e., below 98% coverage) are experienced as difficult. This convergence between L1 and L2 findings — both pointing to 98% as the critical coverage level — strengthens the generalizability of Hu & Nation's (2000) L2-based finding. It also establishes that the 98% threshold is not an artifact of L2 learner limitations but a fundamental property of human lexical processing during reading.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Carver's L1 data allows Nugget's methodology page to present the 98% coverage threshold as a universal feature of reading comprehension, not a handicap specific to learners. This framing is motivationally important: "Needing 98% coverage isn't a sign of weakness — it's how reading works for everyone. Our job is to build you to that threshold for Japanese."

**Design Implication (§4.2):** The lexical threshold literature converges on a clear operational standard: 98% text coverage is the minimum for genuinely independent reading. This translates to specific vocabulary size targets by text type — for Japanese specifically, the vocabulary size research in §CA.2 (cross-reference Nation, 2006; Schmitt & Schmitt, 2014) provides the word-family estimates for NHK Web Easy, graded readers at each level, manga, and novels. Nugget's level-up milestones and reading recommendations should be calibrated to these thresholds, not to JLPT level completion. A learner who passes N4 has not necessarily reached 98% coverage of graded reader Level 2 materials — the JLPT and the coverage thresholds are different instruments measuring different things.

## §4.3 — Reading Frequency and Incidental Vocabulary Acquisition

### Core Theoretical Background

Incidental vocabulary acquisition — vocabulary learned as a by-product of reading for meaning, not as a primary study target — is one of the most consequential phenomena in the L2 vocabulary literature. It is also one of the most misunderstood. The popular claim that "reading is the best way to learn vocabulary" is partially true, but it masks a crucial qualification: incidental learning from reading is *highly efficient at scale and above the lexical threshold* but *highly inefficient below the threshold and for initial form-meaning mapping*.

The strategic question for Nugget Nihongo is not "SRS or reading?" but "SRS and reading, in what proportion, at what proficiency level?" The research reviewed in §4.3 answers this question empirically: deliberate SRS study dominates below approximately 3,000 word families; ER-based incidental acquisition dominates above that threshold. This boundary is not sharp — it reflects the probability that a learner at a given vocabulary size will encounter unknown words often enough, and in sufficiently rich contexts, for incidental acquisition to occur efficiently.

Cross-reference §1.7 (BC-02, Webb & Nation, 2017) — the SRS-vs-ER threshold analysis is introduced in §1.12 and extended here with the Nation & Wang (1999) graded reader encounter data and the Webb (2007) repetition research.

**[ER-09]** **Nation, I. S. P., & Wang, K. (1999).** Graded readers and vocabulary. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 12*(2), 355–380. Retrieved from https://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

- **Core contribution:** The empirical examination of vocabulary encounter rates in graded readers across levels. Nation and Wang calculated how many times a target word appears across a graded reader series at each level — and how many books at that level a learner would need to read to encounter each word the 10–12 times considered necessary for incidental acquisition (following the encounter-to-acquisition benchmarks in Nation's vocabulary knowledge research). The critical finding: at lower proficiency levels, the encounter rates in graded readers are too low to produce incidental acquisition efficiently. A learner would need to read an impractical volume of graded Level 1 material to acquire lower-frequency vocabulary incidentally. At higher levels (approximately Level 4 and above in standard graded reader series), encounter rates become sufficient to support incidental acquisition for mid-frequency vocabulary.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Nation & Wang (1999) provides the empirical basis for recommending different learning strategies at different proficiency levels. For learners below the 3,000-word-family threshold, Nugget's SRS approach is more efficient than graded reader ER for vocabulary growth — the encounter rates simply aren't there. For learners above 3,000 families, graded readers and authentic materials begin to deliver the encounter density needed for incidental acquisition. Nugget's methodology page should present this as a transition arc: "SRS-first → threshold → ER-dominated."

**[ER-10]** **Webb, S. (2007).** The effects of repetition on vocabulary knowledge. *Applied Linguistics, 28*(1), 46–65. https://doi.org/10.1093/applin/aml048

- **⚠️ Anti-duplication note:** Webb (2007) may overlap with OT-25–OT-30 (§12.5, Barcroft/Webb vocabulary consolidation cluster). Check §12 file before finalizing. If already cited in §12 as OT-2X, cross-reference rather than create ER-10.
- **Core contribution:** Webb's study manipulated the number of times learners encountered target words in a reading passage (1, 3, 7, or 10 encounters) and measured subsequent vocabulary knowledge across multiple dimensions (form recognition, meaning recall, use in context). The key findings: (1) a single encounter produces negligible acquisition; (2) meaningful acquisition of form-meaning connections requires a minimum of 7–10 encounters; (3) even 10 encounters in a single reading context produces shallower vocabulary knowledge than the same number of encounters distributed across multiple reading sessions (a retrieval practice and spacing effect consistent with §1.2 and §1.3). Webb's findings establish both a *floor* (1–3 encounters is insufficient) and a *ceiling* (10 encounters in a single context leaves considerable knowledge depth ungained) for ER-based incidental vocabulary acquisition.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Webb (2007) explains precisely when SRS is more efficient than ER and why: for words encountered fewer than 7 times in reading contexts, SRS-mediated spaced retrieval produces richer and more durable knowledge representations than incidental reading encounter. For words encountered frequently in authentic texts (above ~3,000 word families), the reading encounter rate eventually exceeds what SRS can provide per unit time. The strategy handoff from SRS to ER is not about abandoning deliberate study — it is about recognizing when the encounter ecology has shifted sufficiently to make ER the dominant acquisition mechanism.

**[ER-11]** **Pigada, M., & Schmitt, N. (2006).** Vocabulary acquisition from extensive reading: A case study. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 18*(1), 1–28. Retrieved from https://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

- **Core contribution:** A fine-grained case study of what vocabulary knowledge actually develops through ER. Pigada and Schmitt tracked a French learner of Greek over four weeks of extensive reading, testing vocabulary knowledge dimensions (spelling, meaning, grammatical behavior) before and after reading. Their finding complicates simple "ER builds vocabulary" claims: ER was most effective at consolidating *partial* knowledge of words the learner had already partially acquired, and least effective at producing *initial* form-meaning acquisition of completely unknown words. Spelling knowledge improved most readily; productive use and grammatical knowledge improved least. This demonstrates that ER and deliberate study operate on *different* vocabulary knowledge dimensions — ER builds breadth and consolidates partial knowledge; SRS establishes initial form-meaning mappings.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Pigada & Schmitt (2006) resolves an apparent contradiction in the literature: if ER is such an efficient vocabulary acquisition mechanism, why does it underperform SRS at the initial learning stage? Because they are addressing different learning challenges. Nugget's SRS cards establish initial form-meaning links (the dimension SRS optimizes); subsequent ER encounters deepen, broaden, and consolidate that knowledge into multi-dimensional lexical representations. The two activities are not competing — they are addressing sequential stages in the vocabulary acquisition arc. The methodology page should present this explicitly.

**Design Implication (§4.3):** The incidental acquisition literature generates a strategic recommendation with direct implications for Nugget's platform design: the transition from SRS-dominated to ER-dominated vocabulary learning occurs around the 3,000-word-family threshold. Below this level, SRS delivers encounter density and form-meaning mapping that ER cannot match. Above this level, ER delivers encounter breadth and contextual depth that SRS cannot scale to efficiently. Nugget's milestone system should make this transition visible to learners — flagging the point at which graded reader and NHK Web Easy integration becomes the highest-yield vocabulary activity, and shifting SRS deck maintenance into a supporting rather than primary role.

## §4.4 — L1 Reading Strategy Transfer

### Core Theoretical Background

A sophisticated reader's L1 literacy includes far more than the ability to decode print. It includes metacognitive strategies: predicting what comes next, monitoring one's own comprehension, recognizing when understanding has failed, identifying the main idea from supporting details, summarizing, making inferences. These strategies are not language-specific — they operate on *meaning*, and they transfer to L2 reading contexts. Or more precisely: they transfer *conditionally*, above a linguistic threshold.

The central theoretical contribution of §4.4 is the identification of this threshold condition. Alderson's (1984) foundational analysis asked whether L2 reading difficulty is primarily a reading problem (failure to apply L1 strategies) or a language problem (insufficient L2 vocabulary and grammar to support the strategies). His answer: predominantly language. Below a critical vocabulary and grammar threshold, even skilled L1 readers cannot deploy their metacognitive strategies in the L2. The strategies are present; the linguistic material to apply them to is not.

This has specific implications for Indonesian learners of Japanese that extend the analysis in §9.10 (ODH — cross-reference). Indonesian learners bring a shallow orthography background: Bahasa Indonesia is nearly transparent phonemically, so Indonesian readers have well-developed phonological decoding strategies that are *not transferable* to kanji. They must build entirely new orthographic processing routes for Japanese (§9.10.1, OD-01 cross-reference) while simultaneously their comprehension-level metacognitive strategies remain latent, waiting for sufficient L2 vocabulary to activate them. This creates a double development burden unique to learners from shallow L1 orthographies approaching Japanese.

**[ER-12]** **Grabe, W. (2009).** *Reading in a second language: Moving from theory to practice.* Cambridge University Press.

- **Core contribution:** The most comprehensive synthesis of L2 reading research available. Grabe integrates cognitive, psycholinguistic, and educational perspectives to produce a theory of L2 reading that is both explanatorily powerful and pedagogically actionable. The book's treatment of L1-to-L2 strategy transfer is the most nuanced in the literature: good L1 readers do transfer metacognitive strategies (comprehension monitoring, predicting, summarizing, identifying main ideas), but the transfer is *conditional* on sufficient L2 linguistic knowledge. Transfer of *phonological decoding* strategies is more problematic — L1 phonological awareness transfers only when the L2 orthography uses similar principles (which Japanese does not, for Indonesian learners; cross-reference §9.10 ODH). Grabe's framework synthesizes the Alderson (1984, ER-13) language threshold with the Cummins (1979) interdependence hypothesis and produces a nuanced claim: L1 reading skill and L2 linguistic knowledge are both necessary but neither is sufficient for skilled L2 reading.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Grabe (2009) provides the theoretical architecture for understanding why Indonesian learners of Japanese face a compounded difficulty: not only the vocabulary gap (§4.2–§4.3) and the orthographic gap (§9.10) but also the strategic transfer gap — their well-developed L1 reading strategies are waiting, unable to deploy, until L2 vocabulary and grammar develop sufficiently. Nugget's intermediate-level content (N2–N1) should explicitly teach comprehension strategy application, framing it as "unlocking what you already know" rather than teaching entirely new skills.

**[ER-13]** **Alderson, J. C. (1984).** Reading in a foreign language: A reading problem or a language problem? In J. C. Alderson & A. H. Urquhart (Eds.), *Reading in a foreign language* (pp. 1–24). Longman.

- **Core contribution:** The chapter that framed the central theoretical debate in L2 reading research for two decades. Alderson examined whether poor L2 reading performance is caused by (a) failure to transfer L1 reading strategies or (b) insufficient L2 linguistic proficiency to support those strategies. His analysis of the available evidence concluded that L2 linguistic competence is the primary bottleneck below a threshold level. He proposed the "language threshold hypothesis": learners below a critical level of L2 vocabulary and grammar are essentially *language-limited*, regardless of their L1 reading sophistication. Above this threshold, L1 reading skill becomes a significant predictor of L2 reading performance. This was the first systematic empirical argument that the ER-versus-grammar-instruction debate was partially misconceived: building L2 linguistic competence (vocabulary + grammar) is the prerequisite for ER strategy deployment, not its alternative.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Alderson (1984) provides the theoretical legitimacy for Nugget's SRS-first design. The language threshold argument means that vocabulary and grammar instruction (Nugget's core activity) is not the conservative, traditional option compared to immersive ER — it is the prerequisite for ER to work. Learners who attempt early immersion without meeting the language threshold do not develop L1 strategy transfer; they develop learned helplessness and avoidance. Nugget's role is to build them to the threshold at which their existing L1 reading sophistication can activate.

**[ER-14]** **Bernhardt, E. B., & Kamil, M. L. (1995).** Interpreting relationships between L1 and L2 reading: Consolidating the linguistic threshold and the linguistic interdependence hypotheses. *Applied Linguistics, 16*(1), 15–34. https://doi.org/10.1093/applin/16.1.15

- **Core contribution:** Bernhardt and Kamil's structural equation modelling analysis of L1 and L2 reading found that L2 linguistic competence (grammar + vocabulary) accounts for approximately 30% of variance in L2 reading comprehension, while L1 reading ability accounts for approximately 20%, and approximately 50% of the variance remains unaccounted for — attributed to "L2 reading-specific" factors. This finding has several important implications: (1) L2 reading is not simply L1 reading applied to a new language (otherwise L1 reading skill would predict more of the variance); (2) L2 linguistic competence is a necessary but not sufficient condition for L2 reading competence; (3) there are L2 reading-specific skills — including script familiarity, orthographic processing speed, and text-type awareness — that must develop separately. For Japanese learners, the orthographic processing skills (§9.10 cross-reference) constitute a significant component of this "L2 reading-specific" variance.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Bernhardt & Kamil (1995) establishes that even after reaching the language threshold, L2 reading requires additional development of reading-specific skills. This grounds the argument for dedicated reading practice (ER) as complementary to vocabulary and grammar study — not merely a downstream application of it. For Indonesian learners specifically, the kanji script acquisition track (§9.3, KS-01–KS-09 cross-reference) addresses the orthographic component of this L2 reading-specific variance.

**[ER-15]** **Koda, K. (2005).** *Insights into second language reading: A cross-linguistic approach.* Cambridge University Press.

- **Core contribution:** Koda's monograph is the most comprehensive cross-linguistic treatment of L2 reading acquisition, specifically addressing how L1 orthographic background shapes L2 reading development. The book synthesizes research on alphabetic-to-logographic transfer (directly relevant for Indonesian learners of Japanese) and characterizes the reading challenge across typologically distinct writing systems. For Indonesian learners of Japanese, Koda identifies three independent challenges that most L2 reading research — conducted on alphabetic-to-alphabetic transfer — fails to address: (1) grapheme-phoneme recorrespondence (the mora-based phonological coding of kana replaces grapheme-phoneme correspondence entirely; sublexical route strategies must be rebuilt from scratch); (2) logographic processing (kanji requires whole-word lexical lookup rather than any phonological mediation; this is an entirely different processing architecture); and (3) morphological transparency (Japanese word segmentation in running text is ambiguous — there are no spaces, and script switching between hiragana, katakana, and kanji carries morphological information). These three challenges stack, creating a reading acquisition timeline that is qualitatively longer than alphabetic-to-alphabetic L2 learning.
- **Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Koda (2005) provides the cross-linguistic grounding for why Indonesian learners face a longer and more complex Japanese literacy acquisition path than most ER research — conducted primarily on English and European L2 learners — would suggest. The methodology page should acknowledge this explicitly: Japanese reading fluency takes longer for Indonesian learners not because the learners are less able, but because the orthographic distance is greater. This is simultaneously a message about realistic expectations and a motivation for Nugget's sustained vocabulary-building program: the path is longer, but it is mapped.

**Design Implication (§4.4):** The L1 strategy transfer literature converges on a sequence for Indonesian learners: (1) *before the language threshold*, direct vocabulary and grammar instruction (Nugget's SRS core) is the highest-yield activity; (2) *at and beyond the threshold*, L1 metacognitive strategies activate and ER becomes genuinely productive; (3) *throughout*, orthographic processing skills (§9.10 ODH cross-reference) develop on a separate timeline and require dedicated kana and kanji practice. The Indonesian learner's shallow-orthography L1 background means their phonological decoding strategies do not transfer — but their comprehension-level metacognitive strategies (monitoring, predicting, summarizing) fully transfer once the language threshold is met. Platform design should make this transfer visible: advanced levels should include explicit prompts for learners to apply comprehension strategies they already possess, framed as activation of existing skills rather than instruction of new ones.

### §4.5 — Extensive Reading Research for Japanese as L2

#### The Evidence Base: Thin but Growing

Extensive reading research occupies a paradoxical position in Japanese as a second/foreign language (JSL/JFL) pedagogy. The theoretical case for ER in any L2 context is strong — Agent 22's §4.1–§4.4 coverage documents this firmly — but empirical studies specifically examining ER with learners *of Japanese* remain sparse to a degree that warrants explicit acknowledgement rather than papering over. The bulk of ER research, including much of the meta-analytic work Agent 22 cites, involves Japanese *native speakers* developing L2 English reading skills, not the reverse. This is not merely an academic footnote: the orthographic complexity of Japanese, its multi-script system, and the particular scaffolding demands of graded reader design create conditions that cannot be assumed to mirror findings from alphabetic L2 reading contexts. Practitioners and platform designers operating in this space must navigate that transfer uncertainty honestly.

---

**[ER-16]** Hitosugi, C. I., & Day, R. R. (2004). Extensive reading in Japanese. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 16*(1), 20–39. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Hitosugi and Day conducted one of the very few empirical investigations of ER with learners studying Japanese as a foreign language, following university-level JFL students engaged in a semester-long ER program using self-selected Japanese-language texts. Their findings replicated several hallmarks of ER effects documented in other L2 contexts: gains in reading rate, improved attitudes toward Japanese reading, and increased reading confidence — but effect sizes were modest and the study's small sample (N = 22) limits generalisability. Crucially, the authors reported significant individual variation in willingness to engage with extended reading when the orthographic demands of Japanese were perceived as excessive, suggesting that motivation and orthographic anxiety interact in ways not captured by alphabetic-context ER models. The paper is among the foundational texts establishing that ER is *feasible* in Japanese L2 pedagogy, while simultaneously signalling that its operationalisation requires adaptations specific to Japanese's script complexity.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Hitosugi & Day establish the empirical floor for Japanese L2 ER advocacy. Their finding that orthographic anxiety mediates ER engagement directly motivates Nugget Nihongo's vocabulary-first sequencing: learners who arrive at ER with a consolidated kanji and vocabulary foundation are less likely to hit the anxiety-driven disengagement Hitosugi and Day observed.

**[ER-17]** Taguchi, E., Takayasu-Maass, M., & Gorsuch, G. J. (2004). Developing reading fluency in EFL: How assisted repeated reading and extensive reading affect fluency development. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 16*(2), 70–96. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** This study compared assisted repeated reading (ARR) and extensive reading as fluency-building interventions with Japanese EFL university learners — that is, Japanese *native speakers* developing reading fluency in English. The distinction matters for citation framing: this is not a study of learners of Japanese, but of Japanese learners in their L2 English classroom. Its relevance to Japanese L2 pedagogy is methodological rather than direct: Taguchi et al. demonstrate that both ARR and ER produce measurable reading rate gains over a semester, with ARR producing faster initial fluency gains and ER producing more durable attitude improvements. The repeated-reading component involved reading the same text multiple times at pace, with timed performance tracking — a procedure directly transferable to Japanese L2 reading instruction regardless of the target language direction. The study provides the strongest available evidence base for incorporating timed repeated reading elements into a Japanese L2 fluency curriculum.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The fluency-building logic in Taguchi et al. supports the design rationale for any timed reading feature within Nugget's ER recommendation panel. Repeated reading of known-vocabulary texts (graded readers at the learner's current JLPT level) is the most empirically defensible route to automatic word recognition, which in turn reduces the cognitive load available for higher-order comprehension.

**[ER-18]** Robb, T., & Kano, M. (2008). Effective extensive reading outside the classroom: A large-scale experiment. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 20*(2), 234–247. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Robb and Kano report on a longitudinal study at a Japanese university (Kyoto Sangyo University) in which students were assigned extensive reading targets as out-of-class homework, tracked via reading logs, without in-class scaffolding beyond the initial orientation. The study is notable for its ecological validity (real classroom conditions, no laboratory controls) and its finding that self-directed ER programmes produce gains in reading motivation and rate comparable to in-class facilitated ER, *provided* learners have adequate initial vocabulary to access the texts. Students who began the programme without sufficient vocabulary to process the graded texts at reasonable speed showed negligible gains and discontinued voluntary reading earlier in the semester. This finding empirically grounds the vocabulary-first sequencing argument: ER assigned before lexical readiness is not merely ineffective but may be actively demotivating.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Robb and Kano's self-directed ER model is the most directly applicable to Nugget Nihongo's context: learners using a mobile SRS platform are, by definition, self-directed. The Kyoto study's failure condition — beginning ER without vocabulary readiness — maps precisely onto the risk of recommending ER to Nugget users before they have consolidated sufficient vocabulary.

**[ER-19]** Mori, Y., & Shimizu, H. (2007). Japanese language students' attitudes toward kanji and their learning strategies. *Foreign Language Annals, 40*(3), 455–471. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1944-9720.2007.tb02867.x *(verify DOI)*

**Core contribution:** Mori and Shimizu surveyed JFL students at US universities on their attitudes toward kanji learning and the strategies they deployed, finding a clear bifurcation: students with positive attitudes toward kanji as a meaningful orthographic system used deeper processing strategies (semantic radical analysis, compound decomposition, mnemonic linking to meaning), while students with negative or avoidant attitudes defaulted to phonological bypass — relying on furigana or romaji transliteration rather than engaging with the kanji form. Critically, the furigana-reliant group showed significantly lower retention of kanji forms over a delayed post-test, even when immediate comprehension scores were comparable. This is the empirical grounding for the *furigana dilemma* in Japanese L2 graded readers: phonological scaffolding via furigana enables comprehension access in the short term but may systematically suppress kanji acquisition by providing a bypass route that skilled readers would not use.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The furigana dilemma has direct implications for how Nugget Nihongo should position its kanji display settings and how it should advise learners approaching graded readers. A learner who reads exclusively with full furigana support will build reading comprehension habits that do not transfer to furigana-free authentic texts. A graduated furigana-reduction strategy — tied to SRS mastery milestones — is the design implication.

**[ER-20]** Chikamatsu, N. (1996). The effects of L1 orthography on L2 word recognition: A study of American and Chinese learners of Japanese. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition, 18*(4), 403–432. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0272263196004032 *(verify DOI)*

**Core contribution:** Chikamatsu compared how American English L1 speakers and Chinese L1 speakers processed Japanese written words, exploiting the critical difference that Chinese readers arrive with prior logographic experience while English readers do not. Her findings are foundational for understanding the orthographic scaffolding dilemma in Japanese L2 reading: English-background learners, lacking logographic prior knowledge, showed a strong tendency to phonologically recode Japanese words (using hiragana or furigana as the primary access route) rather than processing kanji as whole semantic units. Chinese-background learners, by contrast, showed patterns closer to native Japanese readers in kanji-as-unit processing. This L1 transfer effect in orthographic processing has a direct pedagogical implication: furigana scaffolding in graded readers, while necessary for English-background learners, actively reinforces a processing route (phonological recoding) that is ultimately less efficient for kanji-dense authentic Japanese text.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Most Nugget Nihongo users from Indonesian L1 backgrounds share the alphabetic orthographic profile of Chikamatsu's English-background learners. This makes the furigana bypass risk particularly salient: the platform's kanji introduction sequencing and graded reader recommendations should account for learners who will default to phonological processing unless the visual logographic system is explicitly and early prioritised.

**Design Implication:** The Japanese L2 ER evidence base is thin — Hitosugi & Day (2004) [ER-16] remains the only controlled study specifically targeting learners of Japanese — and the field's honest position is that most ER findings must be extrapolated from alphabetic L2 or Japanese EFL contexts. Nugget Nihongo's methodology page should acknowledge this gap directly rather than overclaiming. The orthographic dimension of Japanese ER — the furigana scaffolding dilemma, the phonological bypass risk for alphabetic-background learners, and the need for graduated kanji exposure — represents the most important Japanese-specific adaptation of general ER theory that the platform must address. The design recommendation is: do not position ER as simply "reading practice" but as a kanji-acquisition vehicle that requires deliberate orthographic strategy guidance alongside vocabulary readiness gating.

### §4.6 — Graded Reader Framework for Japanese

#### The Graded Reader Ecosystem

Japanese enjoys an unusually well-developed graded reader ecosystem relative to most non-English L2 contexts. The principal series available as of 2026 include: *Ask Graded Readers* (ASK 出版), which spans five levels aligned to JLPT N5–N2 vocabulary ranges; *White Rabbit Press Graded Readers*, which uses an independent six-level scale with explicit word-count ceilings per level; and the *Tadoku* (多読) series, which emphasises very low-level entry texts with high visual support for absolute beginners. The Extensive Reading Foundation (ERF) lists and endorses these series through its *Language Learner Literature* award programme and its *ER Central* database, providing the field with a curated quality benchmark. What the ecosystem currently lacks is a consistent vocabulary-level tagging system across series: Ask uses JLPT alignment, White Rabbit uses its own scale, and Tadoku avoids explicit level labels in favour of word-count thresholds. This inconsistency creates a mapping problem for learners trying to transition between series, and for platforms like Nugget Nihongo trying to recommend appropriate entry points.

**[ER-21]** Nation, I. S. P., & Wang, K. (1999). Graded readers and vocabulary. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 12*(2), 355–380. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Nation and Wang examined the vocabulary demands of graded readers across multiple series and levels, applying the lexical coverage framework — the proportion of running words a reader must know to access a text — to graded reader design. Their key finding is that graded readers, when well-constructed, allow learners to read with approximately 95–98% word coverage, meaning one unknown word per 20–50 running words, a density low enough for comprehension to proceed without dictionary consultation. Nation and Wang also identify a systematic problem: poorly designed graded readers introduce vocabulary above the stated level, creating unexpected coverage gaps that undermine the reading experience. Their framework provides the most widely cited standard for graded reader quality assessment, and the 95–98% coverage target has become the operational definition of a "readable" text for L2 learners at any level.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Nation and Wang's 95–98% coverage threshold defines the vocabulary floor for productive ER. For Japanese graded readers, this translates to a JLPT-level vocabulary sufficient to cover the target series' word frequency range. The platform should communicate this threshold to users: "You know enough words to begin reading Level X graded readers."

**[ER-22]** Waring, R., & Takaki, M. (2003). At what rate do learners learn and retain new vocabulary from reading a graded reader? *Reading in a Foreign Language, 15*(2), 130–163. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Waring and Takaki tracked vocabulary acquisition in a Japanese EFL context through a single graded reader reading, using three post-reading tests at immediate, one-week, and three-month intervals. Their findings are foundational and, for ER advocates, sobering: of 25 target words encountered in the text, learners acquired approximately 1 new word (at the form-meaning level) from reading alone, and retention at three months was lower still. However, the study also showed that words already partially known — at Paribakht and Wesche's (1997) VKS Stage 2–3 range (see §CA.10 VD cluster, cross-reference only) — showed substantially higher consolidation from the reading encounter than words encountered for the first time. This finding reframes what ER is *for*: not primary acquisition of new vocabulary, but consolidation and deepening of partially known vocabulary. The SRS-first, ER-second sequencing is empirically grounded precisely in this finding.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Waring and Takaki are the strongest empirical argument against "ER instead of SRS" and for "SRS then ER." Their study implies that a learner who encounters a word in a graded reader after having already built a partial representation through SRS review will benefit substantially more from that encounter than a learner meeting the word cold. This is the core of the complementarity thesis developed in §4.7.

**[ER-23]** Takase, A. (2007). Japanese high school students' motivation for extensive reading. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 19*(1), 1–18. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Takase surveyed and interviewed Japanese high school students engaged in a sustained ER programme, identifying motivation profiles associated with successful long-term ER engagement versus early dropout. The primary differentiator was not reading proficiency but *perceived agency*: students who reported choosing their own texts sustained ER engagement significantly longer than those assigned texts, even when the assigned texts were well-matched to their level. A secondary finding of direct relevance to platform design was that students who perceived ER as an isolated activity — disconnected from their vocabulary study — reported lower motivation than those who understood the relationship between vocabulary building and reading access. Takase's framework suggests that metacognitive framing ("your SRS practice is building toward reading this") is as important a motivational lever as text difficulty calibration.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The self-selection principle Takase documents supports allowing learners to choose among recommended graded reader titles rather than prescribing a single pathway. The metacognitive framing finding suggests that Nugget should explicitly surface the connection between SRS mastery milestones and ER readiness — making visible what vocabulary work *enables* in terms of reading access.

**[ER-24]** Bamford, J., & Day, R. R. (Eds.). (2004). *Extensive reading activities for teaching language.* Cambridge University Press.

**Core contribution:** This edited volume, the implementation companion to Day and Bamford's (1998) foundational theoretical text, assembles practitioner accounts and activity frameworks for integrating ER into diverse language teaching contexts. Most directly relevant to the Japanese L2 graded reader framework are the chapters addressing multi-script contexts, the role of reading logs and self-reporting in sustaining ER motivation, and the design of "bridge" activities that connect graded reader vocabulary to authentic text encounters. The volume's central pedagogical argument — that ER without explicit bridging activities fails to transfer reading gains to unremediated texts — aligns with the design implication of the SRS → graded reader → authentic text progression. The editors also outline the ten methodological principles of ER implementation that have become the field's de facto standard checklist, of which the most frequently violated in Japanese L2 contexts are: "the reading material is easy" (not always true of Japanese graded readers above N3) and "reading is its own reward" (often undermined by grading or vocabulary quiz requirements attached to ER programmes).

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Bamford and Day's ten principles provide a design checklist for how Nugget should position ER recommendations: easy, self-selected, uninterrupted, and intrinsically motivated. Any in-app quiz or comprehension check attached to ER reading violates the spirit of the approach; if Nugget incorporates a reading feature, the vocabulary flagging mechanism should be lightweight and optional.

**Design Implication:** The graded reader ecosystem for Japanese is pedagogically mature but structurally fragmented. Nugget Nihongo's ER recommendations panel can add significant value by translating between the JLPT vocabulary knowledge a learner has built through SRS and the specific graded reader series and levels accessible at that threshold — a mapping that currently does not exist in standardised form. Nation and Wang's [ER-21] coverage threshold (95–98%) is the operative benchmark: a learner who has consolidated N5 vocabulary (~800 words) can access Tadoku Level 0–1 and Ask Level 1; N4 vocabulary (~1,500 words) opens Ask Levels 2–3 and White Rabbit Level 2. The bridge strategy — SRS builds core vocabulary → Level 1–2 graded readers consolidate and expand → NHK Web Easy as the authentic-text gateway → manga and novels as the fluency ceiling — should be the platform's explicit, visualised learning arc.

### §4.7 — Design Implications: ER Integration with SRS

#### The Complementarity Thesis

The relationship between spaced repetition systems and extensive reading is often framed as a trade-off — time spent on Anki decks is time not spent reading, and vice versa. This framing is empirically incorrect and pedagogically damaging. The complementarity thesis, grounded in Waring and Takaki's [ER-22] consolidation findings and the broader vocabulary depth literature (see §CA.10 VD-01–VD-21, cross-reference only), holds that SRS and ER are sequential rather than competing: SRS establishes the form-meaning mappings that make words *accessible* in text; ER then provides the varied contextual encounters that transform accessible words into deeply known words — the collocational, register, and grammatical dimension knowledge that SRS alone cannot efficiently build.

Nation's Four Strands framework (§1.7, cross-reference only) provides the theoretical housing for this thesis: meaning-focused input (what ER supplies) and form-focused instruction (what SRS supplies) are not alternatives but necessary complements within a balanced language learning programme. What the complementarity thesis adds is a *sequencing claim*: SRS must precede ER at the early stages, because ER without sufficient lexical coverage produces frustration rather than acquisition (Robb & Kano, 2008 [ER-18]; Hitosugi & Day, 2004 [ER-16]). The transition from SRS-dominant to ER-complemented learning is triggered by a vocabulary threshold, not by a fixed time investment or a calendar milestone.

**[ER-25]** Pigada, M., & Schmitt, N. (2006). Vocabulary acquisition from extensive reading: A case study. *Reading in a Foreign Language, 18*(1), 1–28. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Pigada and Schmitt tracked one advanced adult French L2 learner across four weeks of extensive reading in French, examining gains across three dimensions of vocabulary knowledge: spelling, meaning, and grammar. Their fine-grained analysis revealed that ER produces highly uneven vocabulary gains across knowledge types: meaning knowledge showed the most consistent improvement, spelling knowledge improved for frequently encountered words, and grammatical knowledge improved least of all. Crucially, the study confirmed Waring and Takaki's [ER-22] implication: the words that showed the greatest gains from ER were those with some prior representation — words the learner had encountered before and partially knew. Words appearing for the first time in the ER text showed minimal acquisition even after multiple text encounters. The practical implication is that ER is most productive when it operates on a vocabulary already partially established through other means — namely, SRS.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Pigada and Schmitt provide the micro-level evidence for the complementarity thesis. A Nugget user reading a Japanese graded reader will consolidate vocabulary they have already studied through SRS at a dramatically higher rate than vocabulary they encounter for the first time in text. This supports the "Add to Nugget" feature concept: learners should be able to flag unknown words encountered in ER and queue them for SRS review, closing the loop between reading exposure and deliberate retention.

**[ER-26]** Hirsh, D., & Nation, I. S. P. (1992). What vocabulary size is needed to read unsimplified texts for pleasure? *Reading in a Foreign Language, 8*(2), 689–696. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Hirsh and Nation applied lexical coverage analysis to a corpus of English novels, finding that approximately 2,000 high-frequency word families are required to achieve 95% coverage of unsimplified English fiction — the threshold Nation & Wang [ER-21] identify as minimally necessary for comprehension without constant dictionary consultation. This 2,000-word figure has become a benchmark in L2 English vocabulary pedagogy and is the foundation for the claim that basic vocabulary consolidation must precede authentic text reading. For Japanese, the equivalent threshold is complicated by the orthographic layer: a learner who knows 2,000 Japanese word meanings but cannot recognise their kanji forms is effectively illiterate in authentic texts. The Hirsh-Nation coverage threshold, applied to Japanese, must therefore be operationalised as *word families with kanji recognition*, not simply form-meaning pairs.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** The JLPT N4 core vocabulary (~1,500 word families) represents the recommended milestone at which Nugget users should begin structured ER with lower-level graded readers. This falls slightly below the Hirsh-Nation 2,000-word unsimplified-text threshold — which is appropriate, since graded readers are *simplified* texts designed to operate below that ceiling. The N4 milestone is defensible as the *graded reader entry point*, with N3–N2 (~3,000–6,000 words) as the threshold for authentic-text ER with NHK Web Easy and similar intermediate-complexity sources.

**[ER-27]** Pellicer-Sánchez, A., & Schmitt, N. (2010). Incidental vocabulary acquisition from an authentic novel: Do things fall apart? *Reading in a Foreign Language, 22*(1), 31–55. Available at http://nflrc.hawaii.edu/rfl/

**Core contribution:** Pellicer-Sánchez and Schmitt studied vocabulary acquisition by advanced English L2 learners reading an authentic novel (*Things Fall Apart* by Chinua Achebe) over several weeks, tracking gains on target words appearing at various frequencies in the text. Their findings quantified the incidental acquisition rate from authentic reading: words appearing approximately 10 or more times in the text showed measurable acquisition; words appearing fewer than 5 times showed minimal gains. Critically, even at high encounter frequencies (15+ occurrences), learners typically moved only one stage along a vocabulary knowledge scale — from zero knowledge toward partial recognition, not from partial recognition to productive mastery. The study establishes that authentic-text ER is productive for consolidating partially known vocabulary and for moving words from zero to partial knowledge at high encounter frequencies, but is not a reliable route to full productive mastery for most words.

**Relevance to Nugget Nihongo:** Pellicer-Sánchez and Schmitt set realistic expectations for what authentic-text ER can accomplish in the Nugget Nihongo learning arc. The transition from graded readers to NHK Web Easy and eventually to manga and novels will deepen vocabulary knowledge but not complete it — continued SRS engagement through the intermediate and upper-intermediate stages remains necessary for productive vocabulary development. The platform should communicate this to users: moving to authentic texts is a milestone, not a graduation from deliberate vocabulary study.

**Design Implication:** The SRS → ER integration should not be positioned as a handoff but as a modulation. At beginner stages (below N4), SRS is the primary learning activity and ER is absent or incidental. At early intermediate (N4), structured ER with graded readers is introduced alongside continued SRS, targeting the consolidation of already-SRS'd vocabulary. At intermediate and above (N3–N2), ER with authentic-complexity texts (NHK Web Easy, manga) becomes an increasingly large share of learning time, while SRS deck expansion continues at a moderated pace. SRS never ceases; its function shifts from primary acquisition to maintenance and targeted depth-building (collocations, register) for vocabulary the learner has encountered in ER contexts.

#### Summary: The §4 Design Position for Nugget Nihongo

Extensive reading is not an optional enrichment activity for Nugget Nihongo users — it is the necessary second stage of a two-stage vocabulary acquisition architecture. SRS (Nugget's core function) builds the lexical foundation that makes ER possible; ER then provides the contextual depth that SRS cannot efficiently supply. The research base for this complementarity thesis is strongest for the general ER-vocabulary consolidation relationship (Waring & Takaki [ER-22]; Pigada & Schmitt [ER-25]) and somewhat thinner for the Japanese-specific context (Hitosugi & Day [ER-16] remains the anchor study). The platform's intellectual integrity depends on being transparent about this evidential asymmetry while designing confidently within it.

The recommended vocabulary milestone — JLPT N4 core (~1,500 word families with kanji recognition) — is the operationally defensible entry point for structured graded reader ER, supported by Hirsh and Nation's [ER-26] coverage threshold logic applied to simplified-text conditions. Below this threshold, ER should be available but not recommended as a primary learning mode. Above it, the platform should actively scaffold the transition into reading through level-matched recommendations, a visualised reading progression ladder, and lightweight in-reading vocabulary flagging that closes the loop back into the SRS system.
