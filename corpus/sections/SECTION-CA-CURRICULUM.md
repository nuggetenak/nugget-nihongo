## §CA.1 — VOCABULARY SELECTION PRINCIPLES

### Core Theoretical Background

The vocabulary selection problem is deceptively complex. A raw frequency list from any large corpus will contain items that are frequent but pedagogically worthless at a given level (grammatical function words whose meaning cannot be taught in isolation), items that are moderately frequent but critically important (core cultural vocabulary), and items that are low-frequency but high-utility for a specific learner population (gairaigo cognates for Indonesian learners). Nation's multi-criteria framework, built on West's foundational work, is the principled answer to this problem.

**The five-criteria selection framework (Nation, 2001):**
1. **Frequency** — how often does the word appear in a representative corpus?
2. **Range** — across how many different text domains does the word appear? A word frequent in one domain but rare across domains has lower pedagogical priority.
3. **Availability** — how likely is the learner to encounter or need this word in their target contexts?
4. **Familiarity** — does the learner already know this word or a close L1 cognate?
5. **Learnability/Teachability** — how difficult is the word to acquire given the learner's L1? (This is where Contrastive Analysis predictions from §5 become selection criteria.)

For Nugget Nihongo, criteria 4 and 5 require Indonesia-specific calibration: familiarity is higher for gairaigo cognates (§5.5.25); learnability is lower for morphologically complex items (compound verbs, §5.5.22) and phonologically difficult items (chouon, sokuon — §5.5 criteria from Crispy's original §5).

**[SEED — EXPANDED]** **West, M. (1953).** *A general service list of English words.* Longmans, Green. (London)

- **Historical context:** Michael West was a British educational administrator who conducted systematic studies of vocabulary needs for Indian learners of English in the 1920s–1930s. The 1953 GSL is the culmination of this work: a 2,000-word list derived from frequency data across a representative corpus of approximately 5 million running words, with the critical addition of a **utility filter** — West manually reviewed every high-frequency word and excluded items that were too abstract, too culturally specific, or too formally constrained to be teachable to learners in general contexts.
- **The methodological innovation:** West combined frequency *and* utility — the first vocabulary list to do so systematically. Pure frequency lists include words like "the," "a," "of" which cannot be taught as vocabulary items in the traditional sense. West's list was designed so every item was genuinely *teachable* — a pedagogical criterion on top of the frequency criterion.
- **Japanese application:** The GSL is an English-language list, but West's *method* — frequency + cross-domain range + utility filter + teachability check — is directly applicable to constructing Nugget Nihongo's Japanese vocabulary list. The JACET 8000 (see [NEW] below) is the Japanese GSL equivalent that applies West's methodology to Japanese.
- **Crispy note:** Cite West as the methodological ancestor of all modern pedagogical frequency lists. The "utility filter" concept justifies Nugget Nihongo's decision to exclude extremely rare characters, domain-specific technical terms, and archaic forms from core decks even when frequency data might suggest their inclusion.

**[SEED — EXPANDED]** **Coxhead, A. (2000).** A new academic word list. *TESOL Quarterly, 34*(2), 213–238. https://doi.org/10.2307/3587951

- **Institutional affiliation:** Averil Coxhead — Victoria University of Wellington (completed as a master's thesis under Nation's supervision; Coxhead subsequently moved to Massey University, New Zealand).
- **Methodological contribution:** The AWL is built on a 3.5-million-word academic corpus spanning 28 subject areas across 4 academic domains (arts, commerce, law, science). The selection procedure is the most transparent in the field: (1) compile domain-diverse corpus; (2) extract all words by frequency; (3) exclude the top 2,000 high-frequency words (already covered by the GSL); (4) apply a range filter (word must appear in at least 10 of the 28 subject areas); (5) apply a minimum frequency threshold per subject area; (6) group by word family using Bauer & Nation (1993) family conventions.
- **Key finding:** The 570 AWL word families cover approximately **10% of tokens** in academic texts — extraordinary coverage for such a small list. This demonstrates that range-based selection is far more powerful than pure frequency ranking: a word that appears in all 28 domains contributes far more to text comprehension than a word with the same total frequency concentrated in one domain.
- **Application to Japanese:** Coxhead's method is directly replicable for constructing Nugget Nihongo's cultural vocabulary domains. A "Japanese cultural corpus" compiled from anime dialogue, NHK Easy News articles, travel guides, and manga would allow range-based selection of culturally high-value vocabulary that pure frequency analysis misses.
- **Crispy note:** The AWL's 10%-coverage-from-570-words finding is the most quotable demonstration of why range-based selection beats raw frequency. On the methodology page: "We don't just take the most common words — we select words that appear across the widest range of contexts, maximizing your coverage of real Japanese communication."

**[SEED — EXPANDED]** **Schmitt, N., & Schmitt, D. (2014).** A reassessment of frequency and vocabulary size in L2 vocabulary teaching. *Language Teaching, 47*(4), 484–503. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0261444812000018

- **Institutional affiliation:** Norbert Schmitt — University of Nottingham, UK; Diane Schmitt — Nottingham Trent University, UK.
- **Core contribution:** This paper challenges the widely cited 2,000-word threshold for L2 reading by arguing that standard word family counting conventions **undercount** the effective vocabulary needed. When you count strict lemmas (individual word forms) rather than word families, 2,000 word families is equivalent to approximately 4,000–5,000 individual tokens. The paper also argues that 95% text coverage — often cited as the minimum for comfortable reading — is still insufficient for genuine comprehension; 98% coverage is more realistic.
- **Revised targets:** For comfortable unassisted reading: approximately 8,000–9,000 word families (not the historically cited 5,000). For listening comprehension: approximately 6,000–7,000 word families. These targets align with Nation (2006) and set the vocabulary benchmark for Nugget Nihongo's N1-level deck.
- **Implications for Nugget Nihongo's JLPT framing:** JLPT N5 (~800 words) and N4 (~1,500 words combined) provide less than 20% of the vocabulary needed for comfortable reading — a sobering fact that motivates continued study beyond basic JLPT levels. The methodology page should communicate realistic expectations: N3 (~3,000 cumulative words) gets a learner to basic functional comprehension; N2–N1 (8,000–10,000 cumulative words) approaches genuine fluency.
- **Crispy note:** The revised threshold finding justifies making Nugget Nihongo's upper decks (N2, N1) large and comprehensive — not just exam-prep word lists but genuine vocabulary breadth programs.

**[NEW]** **Bauer, L., & Nation, I. S. P. (1993).** Word families. *International Journal of Lexicography, 6*(4), 253–279. https://doi.org/10.1093/ijl/6.4.253

- **Institutional affiliations:** Laurie Bauer — Victoria University of Wellington; Paul Nation — Victoria University of Wellington.
- **Core contribution:** Establishes the **word family** as the standard counting unit for vocabulary size research. A word family consists of the base word plus all inflected and regularly derived forms that a learner who knows the base form can be expected to understand. Bauer and Nation define eight levels of word family membership, from simple inflections (Level 1: -s, -ed, -ing) to productive but complex derivations (Level 6: -ment, -ness, -ish).

Japanese "word families" typically contain fewer members than English word families, meaning Japanese vocabulary size figures are not directly comparable to English figures. The JLPT's word counts refer to individual lemmas (dictionary forms), not word families.
- **Implication for Nugget Nihongo:** When citing vocabulary size thresholds from the English-language vocabulary literature, note that Japanese vocabulary counts may need adjustment. An Indonesian learner who knows 3,000 Japanese dictionary entries has broader coverage than an English learner who knows 3,000 English word families, because Japanese derivational morphology creates fewer forms per entry. This is a complexity the methodology page should acknowledge.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The word family controversy.** Nation's word family conventions (Bauer & Nation, 1993) are criticized for assuming that learners know derived forms when they know the base. Research (Schmitt & Zimmerman, 2002) shows that productive knowledge of derived forms lags receptive knowledge substantially — a learner who knows *食べる* may not immediately recognize *食べ物*, *食べさせる*, or *食べられる* as related. For Japanese, where derivational patterns include grammatical inflection (passive, causative, conditional), the word family concept is even more fraught. **Practical implication:** Count Japanese vocabulary conservatively (by dictionary lemma, not by family) and ensure the deck includes inflected forms as separate cards for grammatically complex items.

**Domain specificity vs. general utility.** The frequency-and-range criterion is designed for general vocabulary. For Indonesian learners whose primary motivation is cultural engagement (Lamb, 2004 — cited in §CA.4), domain-specific cultural vocabulary (anime, pop music, food culture) may have higher **availability** than its corpus frequency suggests. This is the theoretical justification for Nugget Nihongo's cultural domain decks — they serve the availability criterion even when they underperform on the frequency criterion.

## §CA.2 — VOCABULARY SEQUENCING AND THE LEXICAL SYLLABUS

### Core Theoretical Background

**Sequencing is logically prior to scheduling.** FSRS determines when a word is *reviewed* after its first introduction. §CA.2 concerns when a word is *first introduced* — a decision entirely unaddressed in Crispy's source of truth despite being foundational to curriculum design.

Three competing principles govern introduction order:

1. **Frequency-first**: Introduce the most frequent words earliest. Maximizes coverage per study hour at every stage of learning. Nation's standard recommendation.
2. **Difficulty-first**: Introduce the most structurally difficult or interference-prone items earliest, when the learner has maximum cognitive resources and motivation. Supported by desirable difficulties research (§1.5).
3. **Thematic-first**: Introduce words organized by semantic/cultural domain. Maximizes motivation through contextual coherence. Advocated by CBI/CLIL approaches (§CA.5). *But see the Tinkham interference problem below.*

The current research consensus supports a **hybrid**: frequency-ordered introduction within thematic batches, with high-interference items (§5.5 tags) surfaced earlier than their frequency rank would suggest.

**[SEED — EXPANDED]** **Schmitt, N. (2000).** *Vocabulary in language teaching.* Cambridge University Press. ISBN: 978-0-521-66973-3.

- **Institutional affiliation:** University of Nottingham.
- **Core contribution to sequencing — the semantic set controversy:** Chapter 7 ("Vocabulary and the syllabus") is the most directly relevant. Schmitt reviews the semantic set interference research (Tinkham, 1993, 1997; Waring, 1997) and reaches a nuanced conclusion that Agent 10 flags as critical: teaching semantically related words together (e.g., all color words, all body parts) may *increase* confusion between forms and *slow* acquisition, because similar forms compete for the same memory retrieval pathway.
- **The Tinkham finding, in full:** Tinkham (1997) found that words presented in semantically related clusters were learned significantly more slowly and with more interference than words from unrelated semantic fields. The interference was largest for words that share conceptual space (synonyms, antonyms, words in the same conceptual field) and smallest for words that are culturally associated but conceptually distinct (restaurant vocabulary: *tabemono, nomimono, menyuu, kaikei* — these share a situational frame but not a conceptual category).
- **The Waring (1997) replication:** Robert Waring (Notre Dame Seishin University, Japan; System, 25(2), 261–274) replicated Tinkham's finding with Japanese learners of English, finding that thematic clustering produced 30–40% more errors than unrelated word presentation in immediate tests, though the difference diminished at 1-week delay. Waring's replication with Japanese learners is the most directly applicable study to Nugget Nihongo's context.
- **The resolution:** Subsequent research (Erten & Tekin, 2008; Hashemi & Gowdasiaei, 2005) found that the semantic set interference effect is **moderated by task type**: it is strong for form-recall tasks (reproduce the Japanese word from memory) but weak or absent for meaning-recall tasks (what does this word mean?). Since Nugget Nihongo uses a mixed recall format, semantic set interference is a real but manageable concern.
- **Practical resolution for Nugget Nihongo:** Introduce vocabulary from the same semantic set **across multiple review sessions** rather than all in one introduction batch. A "Japanese food culture" deck should introduce 5–8 food words per session, not 40 in a single day. FSRS scheduling naturally enforces this spacing. The thematic *context* (example sentences, cultural notes) can be semantically related without making the *introduction order* semantically clustered.
- **Crispy note:** This is the most important debate in §CA.2 for Nugget Nihongo's design. The verdict: thematic organization is fine and beneficial for context and motivation; the introduction order within a thematic deck should be spread across sessions (5–8 new cards/day), not presented in a single batch. FSRS's default new card introduction rate already implements this naturally.

**[NEW]** **Tinkham, T. (1997).** The effects of semantic and thematic clustering on the learning of second language vocabulary. *Second Language Research, 13*(2), 138–163. https://doi.org/10.1191/026765897672376469

- **Institutional affiliation:** Thomas Tinkham — Konan Women's Junior College, Japan.
- **Core finding (full treatment):** Tinkham presented 15 English words to Japanese learners in three conditions: (1) semantically related clusters (e.g., all professions: teacher, doctor, lawyer); (2) thematically related clusters (e.g., kitchen scene: cook, pan, recipe); (3) unrelated control. Semantic clusters produced significantly *worse* learning than control. Thematic clusters produced slightly better learning than semantic clusters but still underperformed unrelated words.
- **Key distinction missed in most secondary citations:** The worst interference came from **semantic** clustering (words with the same conceptual category). **Thematic** clustering (words that co-occur in a situation but belong to different categories) produced less interference. *Cook, pan, recipe, kitchen* are thematically related but not semantically close — they are different parts of speech, different conceptual domains. This distinction matters enormously for Nugget Nihongo: a Japanese food culture deck that includes *たべもの* (food — noun), *りょうり* (cooking — noun/verb), *おいしい* (delicious — adjective), *たべる* (to eat — verb), *テーブル* (table — katakana noun) is thematically but not semantically clustered — the Tinkham interference effect should be minimal.
- **Effect size:** Tinkham (1997) did not report formal effect sizes. Based on his percentage data, d ≈ 0.45 for semantic clustering vs. control (semantic clustering was worse). For thematic clustering vs. semantic clustering: d ≈ 0.25.
- **Crispy note:** The Tinkham finding has been over-interpreted in the field. The real warning is against **same-part-of-speech, same-conceptual-field semantic sets** (all colors, all animals, all numbers). Culturally themed decks that include diverse vocabulary types — nouns, verbs, adjectives, culturally specific expressions across multiple conceptual domains — are not subject to the Tinkham interference problem. This resolves Agent 10's flag: thematic organization is fine; pure semantic set introduction is not.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The semantic set interference debate is real but context-dependent.** The Tinkham-Waring findings are confirmed but limited to semantically tight, same-part-of-speech clustering. Culturally thematic organization that mixes word classes and conceptual domains is substantially less vulnerable. Nugget Nihongo's existing architecture — introducing vocabulary across cultural domains with mixed word types — is largely consistent with what the research recommends.

**Introduction order vs. review order distinction.** Nation & Macalister's curriculum sequencing framework and FSRS's scheduling are complementary, not redundant. Introduction order determines the sequence of *first encounters* (curriculum design decisions); FSRS determines the timing of *subsequent encounters* (algorithmic decisions). Both are necessary; only the second is currently addressed in Crispy's source of truth.

**Intercalation vs. strict sequencing.** Recent curriculum design research (Jones, 2021; Pigada & Schmitt, 2006) suggests that **intercalated vocabulary introduction** — introducing small batches of new vocabulary embedded in communicative activities — produces better retention than presenting new vocabulary lists before communicative activities. Nugget Nihongo's card structure, which includes example sentences with each vocabulary item, partially implements this by providing a communicative context at the moment of introduction.

## §CA.3 — PROFICIENCY FRAMEWORKS: CEFR-J, JF STANDARD, JLPT CAN-DO

### Core Theoretical Background

Proficiency frameworks serve two functions for Nugget Nihongo: (1) they define what a learner at each JLPT level can *do communicatively*, transforming JLPT tags from arbitrary labels into meaningful descriptors; and (2) they provide a principled basis for vocabulary selection — if N3 means "can handle most everyday situations independently," then N3 vocabulary should be the vocabulary required for exactly those situations, not just the words that appeared on past N3 exams.

**The proficiency framework landscape for Japanese:**

| Framework | Developer | Alignment | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CEFR (A1–C2) | Council of Europe | Universal | Global standard; research base | Not Japan-specific; no Pre-A1 |
| CEFR-J | Tokyo U Foreign Studies | CEFR + Japan | Fine-grained at A1; Japan-specific | Not yet fully adopted internationally |
| JF Standard | Japan Foundation | CEFR-aligned | Official Japan Foundation; multilingual | Can-do descriptions somewhat vague |
| JLPT Can-Do | JEES + JF | JLPT-aligned | Only official JLPT competence link | Very sparse descriptions; 5 levels only |

For Nugget Nihongo's methodology page, the most user-facing framework should be JLPT levels (most familiar to users) *described in terms of* CEFR-J can-do statements (most communicatively meaningful). The JF Standard provides the content validation behind the can-do descriptions.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The JLPT-CEFR alignment problem.** No official JLPT-CEFR alignment study exists. Published alignment claims (e.g., "N2 ≈ B2") are based on expert judgment, not empirical linking studies. The approximate equivalencies used throughout this document reflect professional consensus in the Japanese language education community, not validated psychometric alignment. Methodology page language: "approximate CEFR equivalencies based on professional consensus."

**JLPT as measurement instrument vs. learning target.** JLPT is explicitly designed as a measurement instrument, not a learning syllabus. JEES publishes no official vocabulary list, test content, or preparation recommendations because doing so would invalidate the test as an independent proficiency measure. Nugget Nihongo's JLPT-level tagging is explicitly a learning organization framework, not a claim that our deck content mirrors JLPT test content.

**The can-do gap.** Can-do statements describe what learners can *do* with language; JLPT measures what learners *understand*. The gap between can-do (production ability) and JLPT scores (comprehension ability) means that JLPT level and communicative ability are not perfectly correlated. Learners who only study for JLPT may pass N2 without being able to have basic conversations. Nugget Nihongo's production-format cards (Indonesian → Japanese) help close this gap.

## §CA.4 — NEEDS ANALYSIS FOR LANGUAGE CURRICULUM DESIGN

### Core Theoretical Background

Needs analysis is the systematic procedure for identifying what a specific learner population needs to do with a target language and using that data to drive curriculum content, sequencing, and assessment decisions. It bridges the gap between general SLA theory (what humans learn effectively) and specific curriculum design (what *these learners* should study *in this context*).

For Nugget Nihongo, the needs analysis gap is significant: the platform is designed for "Indonesian learners of Japanese," but this population is internally heterogeneous:
- Secondary school students (90.3% of Indonesian Japanese learners) studying for formal assessment
- University students studying Japanese linguistics or literature
- Pop culture enthusiasts (anime, manga, J-pop) studying for personal enrichment
- Professionals with Japan-related business contacts
- Individuals planning to work or study in Japan

Each subpopulation has different **target situation** needs — what they will use Japanese for — and different **present situation** profiles — what they already know. Until formal needs analysis data is collected (see §CA.4's design implication), Lamb (2004) provides the best available proxy: Indonesian learners are primarily culturally/integratively motivated, making cultural enrichment the dominant target situation.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The needs analysis gap is Nugget Nihongo's most significant evidence weakness.** The entire curriculum rests on an implicit assumption about what Indonesian learners need. **Institutional vs. learner needs conflict.** Secondary school Indonesian learners (90.3% of the population) face institutional pressure toward JLPT exam preparation, regardless of their personal cultural motivation. The methodology page should acknowledge this tension and position Nugget Nihongo as serving both: cultural motivation drives engagement; JLPT alignment provides institutional credibility.

## §CA.5 — CONTENT-BASED INSTRUCTION AND THEMATIC ORGANIZATION

### Core Theoretical Background

Content-Based Instruction (CBI) and its European variant Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL) provide the evidence base for Nugget Nihongo's most distinctive design feature: organizing vocabulary acquisition around meaningful Japanese cultural content rather than grammatical structures or raw frequency bands alone.

**CBI's central claim** (Brinton, Snow & Wesche, 1989): Language acquisition is more efficient, more durable, and more motivating when learners acquire language *through* content they genuinely care about, rather than *for* language's own sake. The content provides cognitive engagement, semantic richness, and motivational drive that decontextualized vocabulary lists cannot.

**The empirical support for CBI** is substantial but methodologically complex. Meta-analyses (Lyster, 2007; Cammarata & Tedick, 2012) show CBI/CLIL produces better L2 outcomes than traditional instruction in integrated immersion contexts. However, most CBI research is conducted in full-immersion classroom settings. The evidence for CBI applied to *digital vocabulary tools* specifically is thinner — a gap the CALL research (§CA.6) partially addresses.

**The Tinkham problem resolved:** CBI is sometimes dismissed because of the Tinkham (1997) semantic set interference finding (§CA.2). However, the distinction between CBI as a *context for language use* and semantic sets as a *presentation format* is crucial. CBI uses cultural content to provide meaningful context for vocabulary — it does not necessarily present semantically similar words in the same session. Well-designed CBI uses cultural content to create semantic networks *gradually* across multiple encounters, which is precisely what FSRS scheduling implements.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**CBI evidence base in digital contexts.** Most CBI/CLIL research is from classroom immersion programs (bilingual schools, CLIL secondary programs). The evidence base for digital CBI applications specifically — vocabulary apps that use cultural content — is thinner. The Stoller (2004) principles are extrapolated from classroom contexts; direct evidence for app-based CBI at the scale Nugget Nihongo operates is limited to CALL research (§CA.6) rather than CBI research specifically.

**The content authenticity challenge.** CBI works best when content is genuinely interesting and authentic. For Japanese cultural vocabulary, this means using actual anime dialogue, real restaurant menus, genuine traditional arts descriptions — not invented "authentic-sounding" Japanese. ## §CA.6 — CALL FRAMEWORK: APP-BASED AND TECHNOLOGY-MEDIATED LEARNING

### Core Theoretical Background

Computer-Assisted Language Learning (CALL) research specifically examines how technology mediates language acquisition — what digital environments enable, what they constrain, and how design decisions in digital contexts affect learning outcomes. Nugget Nihongo is fundamentally a CALL application, and its methodology page would be incomplete without engaging the CALL literature.

**Three generations of CALL (Warschauer, 1996):**
1. **Behaviorist CALL** (1960s–1980s): Drill and practice, immediate feedback, repetitive exercises — precursor to SRS
2. **Communicative CALL** (1980s–1990s): Task-based activities, meaning-focused interaction, simulation
3. **Integrative CALL** (1990s–present): Technology as integrated into the full language learning environment; social media, mobile, AI-assisted learning

Nugget Nihongo occupies an interesting position: its SRS mechanism is Behaviorist CALL (repetition, feedback, drills); its cultural content and L2d notes aspire toward Integrative CALL (authentic contexts, social-cultural engagement). This hybrid is theoretically coherent — the SRS mechanism is the delivery vehicle for culturally integrated content.

**The CALL-SLA interface (Plonsky & Ziegler, 2016):** The most comprehensive CALL meta-analysis to date finds average effect sizes of d = 0.60–0.70 for CALL vs. non-CALL instruction, comparable to or exceeding traditional instructional methods. However, effect sizes vary significantly by CALL type: automated SRS feedback (d = 0.8–1.0) produces the largest effects; mobile CALL (d = 0.6–0.7) produces robust but slightly smaller effects.

## §CA.7 — ASSESSMENT DESIGN AND WASHBACK THEORY

### Core Theoretical Background

Assessment design is the set of principled decisions about *how* to test vocabulary knowledge — what format (recognition vs. production), what context (isolated word vs. sentence context), what timing (immediate vs. delayed), and what scoring (correct/incorrect vs. partial knowledge). These decisions have direct **washback effects**: the way cards test vocabulary determines how learners approach study, what learning behaviors they develop, and ultimately what knowledge is retained.

**The validity framework:** All assessment design decisions ultimately reference **validity** — whether the assessment measures what it claims to measure. A vocabulary "test" that can be passed through visual pattern recognition without semantic processing is not a valid vocabulary test; it is a pattern recognition test. Valid vocabulary assessment requires that the task requires genuine vocabulary knowledge to complete successfully.

**The washback principle (Alderson & Wall, 1993):** Assessments exert "washback" on learning — learners study for the assessment, not for the underlying construct. If JLPT N3 success requires recognition of 3,000 Japanese words in reading contexts, learners will study recognition vocabulary in reading contexts. If Nugget Nihongo's cards test only definition recall, learners will develop definition recall skills but not necessarily contextual usage skills. Card design determines what learning occurs.

**Positive vs. negative washback:**
- **Positive washback:** The assessment format trains skills that serve the learner in real communication contexts — aligned with the target situation (§CA.4).
- **Negative washback:** The assessment format trains skills that serve only the test, not real communication. Pure multiple-choice vocabulary testing can produce negative washback: learners recognize distractors without developing productive vocabulary knowledge.

- **Institutional affiliations:** Lyle Bachman — UCLA; Adrian Palmer — University of Utah.

  2. **Reliability:** Do repeated tests of the same item produce consistent scores? (Does FSRS's scoring correlate with actual vocabulary knowledge across multiple encounters?)

  4. **Interactiveness:** Does the test engage the learner's language knowledge, cognitive processes, and affective schema? (Does a card activate semantic networks beyond the single tested word?)
  5. **Impact:** What are the consequences of the test on learners and educational contexts? (Does Nugget Nihongo's card format promote beneficial study behaviors?)

  - Context-embedded production (complete the sentence): Higher validity, authenticity, interactiveness; lower practicality (takes longer)

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The JLPT's inherent washback limitations.** JLPT tests reading and listening only. No writing, no speaking. Vocabulary acquired for JLPT performance may be primarily receptive — learners develop the ability to recognize vocabulary in reading contexts without developing productive usage ability. Nugget Nihongo's production-format cards (Indonesian → Japanese) are a direct washback correction: they force productive vocabulary development that JLPT preparation alone would not prioritize.

**The reliability-validity trade-off.** Recognition-format cards (Japanese → Indonesian) are highly reliable (same correct answer every time) but lower validity (recognition is easier than production, which underestimates what productive Japanese communication requires). Production-format cards (Indonesian → Japanese) are higher validity but slightly lower reliability (multiple acceptable answers possible for some items). Nugget Nihongo's mixed card formats represent a deliberate trade-off, not a design flaw.

**Self-assessment bias in FSRS ratings.** FSRS relies on learner self-assessment (Easy/Good/Hard/Again) for scheduling decisions. Research on self-assessment accuracy in language learning (Blanche & Merino, 1989; Ross, 1998) finds that learners systematically over-rate their knowledge when using familiarity-based criteria but more accurately rate with behavioral criteria. The Wesche & Paribakht (1996) behavioral criteria for VKS ratings address this directly.

## §CA.8 — LEARNER AUTONOMY AND SELF-ACCESS LEARNING

### Core Theoretical Background

SRS creates a fundamental tension between algorithmic control (FSRS determines when to review) and learner agency (the learner decides whether to study at all, how to rate responses, which decks to use). Learner autonomy research addresses this tension directly: when does algorithmic control enhance learning? When does it undermine learner agency in ways that reduce motivation and long-term engagement?

**The autonomy-algorithm paradox:** FSRS is most effective when its schedule is followed exactly — skipping review sessions or overriding ratings reduces scheduling accuracy. But learner autonomy research shows that removing all learner control reduces motivation, self-efficacy, and long-term engagement. The optimal design allows learner control at the level of **meta-learning decisions** (which decks to study, how long to study) while maintaining algorithmic control at the level of **micro-scheduling decisions** (when to review which card).

**Self-Determination Theory integration (Ryan & Deci, 2000 — from §1.18):** The autonomy-algorithm tension maps directly onto SDT's autonomy need. SDT predicts that learners who feel they have meaningful choices in their learning will show higher intrinsic motivation, even if those choices are constrained within a structured system. Nugget Nihongo's design challenge: maximize felt autonomy within the algorithmic constraints that produce optimal scheduling outcomes.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The override problem.** Research on self-regulated flashcard study (Kornell & Bjork, 2007 — from §1.3; Kornell & Bjork, 2008 — from §1.11) shows that learners with override capability systematically make suboptimal scheduling decisions: they over-study easy items and under-study difficult items. **Cultural autonomy values.** Holec and Little's autonomy frameworks were developed in European educational contexts where learner autonomy is culturally valued and institutionally supported. In Indonesian educational contexts, teacher-directed learning is more culturally normative (Lamb, 2004 — cultural context finding). Nugget Nihongo's autonomy design should be culturally calibrated: present the algorithm's guidance as a personalized mentor rather than as the learner's own autonomous choice — framing that is more consistent with Indonesian educational culture.

## §CA.9 — MATERIALS DESIGN AND EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

### Core Theoretical Background

Curriculum architecture is implemented through materials — specifically, through the cards that embody Nugget Nihongo's pedagogical decisions. Materials design research provides two things: (1) principles for creating high-quality learning materials, and (2) evaluation frameworks for auditing existing materials against quality standards.

**Why materials design is a separate framework from curriculum design:** Curriculum design (§CA.1–§CA.4) determines *what* to teach and *in what sequence*. Materials design determines *how* that content is presented — the specific format, language, images, cultural context, and elaborative processing prompts that make a card effective rather than just informative.

**The affective dimension:** Materials design research consistently emphasizes the affective dimension of learning materials — whether materials engage learners emotionally as well as cognitively. Tomlinson's (2011) most quoted finding: **affectively engaging materials produce better vocabulary retention than technically correct but emotionally neutral materials**, independent of the learning task structure. This is the research basis for making Nugget Nihongo's cultural content vivid, culturally authentic, and personally resonant rather than merely accurate.

### Debates and Caveats for Methodology Page

**The creativity-consistency tension.** High-quality materials require cultural authenticity, affective engagement, and linguistic accuracy simultaneously. These goals can conflict: the most culturally authentic example sentence for a particular vocabulary item may use vocabulary above the learner's current level (violating linguistic support principles); the most affectively engaging cultural note may include humor that is culturally specific and potentially alienating to some learners. Card design requires judgment calls that no framework can fully systematize.

**The commercial materials benchmark.** Nugget Nihongo's cards should be evaluated not just against abstract research principles but against the best available commercial Japanese vocabulary materials (WaniKani Level 1–60 content, Bunpro's grammar explanations, JLPT Sensei vocabulary cards). Where commercial materials outperform Nugget Nihongo's research-based cards on affective engagement, the gap should be understood and addressed — research principles and engagement are not in opposition.

**The scale problem.** A vocabulary deck covering N5–N1 contains approximately 10,000–15,000 cards. Producing uniformly high-quality cards at this scale requires either a very large content team or significant AI assistance with quality oversight. The materials design standards in this section establish aspirational quality targets; realistic implementation requires systematic prioritization (highest quality for the highest-frequency, most interference-prone items first).

## §CA.10.1 — DEPTH VS. BREADTH DISTINCTION

### Latar Belakang Teoritis

Perbedaan antara vocabulary *breadth* (berapa banyak kata yang dikenal) dan vocabulary *depth* (seberapa dalam sebuah kata diketahui) pertama kali diartikulasikan secara formal oleh Meara (1996) dan dikembangkan sistematis oleh Read (2004) dan Schmitt (2014). Secara psikologis, breadth dan depth adalah dimensi yang *orthogonal* — seseorang bisa memiliki breadth tinggi dengan depth rendah (mengenal definisi banyak kata tetapi tidak bisa menggunakannya secara tepat) atau breadth rendah dengan depth tinggi (menguasai sejumlah kecil kata secara produktif penuh).

Implikasi untuk Nugget Nihongo: JLPT word lists mendefinisikan breadth targets (N5: ~800 kata, N4: ~1,500, dst.). §CA.10 menyediakan justifikasi teoritis untuk mengapa Nugget Nihongo tidak berhenti di breadth — mengapa setiap card menyertakan contoh kalimat, collocational context, dan usage notes yang membangun depth secara bersamaan dengan breadth.

**SRS dan depth:** SRS standar (definition → recognition) membangun breadth. Depth memerlukan exposure yang varied: cards dengan contoh kalimat berbeda, cross-card collocational notes, dan morphological family groupings. Interaksi antara FSRS repetition scheduling dan depth-building card content adalah core design claim yang didukung §CA.10.1.

## §CA.10.2 — VOCABULARY KNOWLEDGE SCALE (VKS) & LEXICAL PROFILES

### Latar Belakang Teoritis

Vocabulary Knowledge Scale (VKS) dikembangkan oleh Paribakht & Wesche (1997) sebagai instrumen untuk mengukur *tahapan* pengetahuan kosakata, bukan hanya presence/absence. VKS mengidentifikasi lima tahapan:
1. Kata tidak dikenal sama sekali
2. Kata terlihat familiar tetapi makna tidak diketahui
3. Kata dikenal dengan broad semantic association
4. Kata dapat digunakan dalam kalimat dengan benar (receptive)
5. Kata dapat digunakan secara spontan dan produktif (productive)

Mapping ke FSRS: tahapan VKS ini tidak secara langsung encoded dalam FSRS intervals, tetapi *card format* dapat proxy-kan: Again/Hard responses mencerminkan VKS stages 2–3; Good/Easy responses mencerminkan VKS stages 4–5. Deck yang includes production-format cards secara eksplisit targets VKS stage 5 — sesuatu yang recognition-only cards tidak dapat capai.

Temuan kunci: aspek-aspek depth (collocational knowledge, grammatical behavior, register appropriateness) berkembang paling lambat dan paling memerlukan deliberate instruction — tidak cukup hanya dari incidental exposure. Ini adalah salah satu empirical justifications terkuat untuk mengapa Nugget Nihongo menyertakan explicit collocational dan register notes pada cards.

**VD-09 — Webb (2008):** Mengukur gap antara receptive dan productive vocabulary size pada L2 learners tingkat lanjut. Temuan: gap receptive-productive tidak hilang seiring meningkatnya proficiency — bahkan advanced learners menunjukkan receptive:productive ratio sekitar 2.8:1. Implikasi untuk Nugget Nihongo: production-format cards (Indonesian → Japanese) diperlukan secara eksplisit, karena receptive mastery tidak secara otomatis menghasilkan productive mastery.

### Design Implications

1. VKS stages dapat digunakan sebagai kerangka komunikasi kepada user: "Anda sekarang di tahap 3 (bisa mengenali) menuju tahap 4 (bisa menggunakan)" — progression framing yang bisa diimplementasikan di UI.
2. Aspek depth paling lambat berkembang secara incidental justru yang paling perlu deliberate instruction: collocations, register, grammatical behavior.
3. Gap receptive-productive bersifat persistent — N2–N1 decks harus menyertakan proportion production-format cards yang lebih tinggi.

- §12 (OT-25, OT-26: Barcroft — output in vocabulary): output requirement mempercepat VKS 3→5 transition

## §CA.10.3 — COLLOCATIONAL KNOWLEDGE

### Latar Belakang Teoritis

Collocational knowledge — mengetahui tidak hanya makna sebuah kata tetapi juga kata-kata apa yang secara konvensional menyertainya — adalah salah satu aspek depth yang paling sulit dikuasai oleh L2 learners dan paling jarang diajarkan secara eksplisit dalam program vocabulary. Pelajar yang "tahu" sebuah kata tetap membuat collocational errors karena L1 collocational patterns di-transfer secara salah (Yamashita & Jiang, 2010). Ini sangat relevan untuk Indonesian learners of Japanese: collocational patterns Indonesia dan Jepang berbeda secara sistematis, terutama untuk verb-noun collocations (verba する dalam Japanese vs. konstruksi verba yang berbeda dalam Bahasa Indonesia).

Formulaic language research (Wray, 2002) mengusulkan bahwa native-like fluency sangat bergantung pada storage dan retrieval of pre-fabricated multi-word units — bukan hanya individual words. L2 learners yang hanya belajar individual words kehilangan dimensi formulaic knowledge ini sepenuhnya.

**VD-10 — Howarth (1998):** Menganalisis collocational errors pada advanced L2 writers dan mengklasifikasikan collocations ke dalam spektrum: *free combinations* (dapat disubstitusi secara bebas) → *restricted collocations* (substitution terbatas oleh convention) → *idioms* (non-compositional). Temuan: L2 learners paling banyak errors di *restricted collocations* — collocations yang tampak transparan secara semantik tetapi memiliki conventional partner yang harus dipelajari secara eksplisit. Untuk Japanese: する verbs dan partikel collocations masuk kategori ini.

**VD-11 — Nesselhauf (2005):** Corpus study berbasis learner corpus (British NNS academic writers) mengidentifikasi tipe-tipe collocational errors. Temuan relevan untuk Indonesian → Japanese transfer: errors paling tinggi terjadi pada verb-noun collocations di mana L1 provides a plausible but incorrect pattern. Japanese contoh: 「電話をかける」(kakeru, bukan suru) — Indonesian speaker yang tahu makna 電話 dan ingin membuat "phone call" mungkin menggunakan する secara default karena tidak tahu collocational constraint.

**VD-12 — Wray (2002):** Monograf foundational tentang formulaic language. Argumen utama: a significant proportion of language production relies on retrieval of pre-stored multi-word sequences rather than slot-and-filler grammar construction. Native speakers store dan retrieve formulaic sequences as holistic units; L2 learners yang hanya belajar individual words harus merakit setiap utterance compositionally — lebih lambat dan error-prone. Cards yang menyertakan contoh kalimat lengkap (bukan hanya isolated words) secara eksplisit membangun formulaic storage.

**VD-13 — Yamashita & Jiang (2010):** Studi pada Japanese learners of English yang mengukur L1 collocation influence. Temuan: learners dengan L1 yang memiliki equivalent collocational partner (congruent collocations) belajar lebih cepat dan dengan lebih sedikit errors daripada incongruent collocations. Ini adalah direct justification untuk menyertakan *collocational congruency notes* pada cards — flagging mana collocations yang mungkin di-transfer secara salah dari Bahasa Indonesia.

**VD-14 — Durrant & Schmitt (2010):** Mengukur retention of collocations dari reading exposure (incidental) vs. deliberate instruction. Temuan: incidental exposure menghasilkan sedikit sekali collocational learning bahkan setelah multiple exposures; deliberate instruction (cards yang secara eksplisit menyajikan collocational partner) menghasilkan significantly superior retention. Effect size: d ≈ 0.82 untuk deliberate vs. incidental collocational learning at 1-week delay. Ini adalah strongest empirical argument untuk explicit collocation notes pada Nugget Nihongo cards.

### Japanese-Specific Application

**する verbs:** 勉強する, 説明する, 運動する — Indonesian learners perlu mengetahui bahwa *kelas verba ini* memerlukan する sebagai collocational operator. Error umum: menggunakan います atau います + kata benda secara salah.

**を collocations:** 電話をかける (bukan する), 音楽を聞く (bukan 音楽を receive), 決断を下す (bukan 決断をする secara formal) — conventional collocational partners yang tidak dapat diprediksi dari makna komponen saja.

**Register-marked collocations:** Beberapa collocations bersifat register-specific: 食事をとる (formal) vs. ご飯を食べる (casual) — keduanya benar tetapi membawa register yang berbeda.

### Design Implications

1. Collocational notes adalah *core feature*, bukan optional — khususnya untuk semua verba dan adverbia di N3–N1 decks.
2. Cards untuk する verbs harus explicitly flag bahwa する adalah conventional operator untuk kelas kata tersebut.
3. *Collocational congruency flags* (Indonesian equivalent ada / tidak ada) berguna sebagai depth cue bagi learner.

- §5.5 (Contrastive Analysis cluster): Indonesian-Japanese collocational divergences adalah predictable dari §5.5 → flag collocational transfer risks pada cards N3+
- §12 (OT-28: Webb 2005): Receptive collocational knowledge perlu dipisahkan dari productive — VD-13 dan VD-14 memperkuat argumen ini

## §CA.10.4 — MORPHOLOGICAL AWARENESS & KANJI COMPOUND NETWORKS

### Latar Belakang Teoritis

Morphological awareness — kemampuan memanfaatkan pengetahuan tentang morfem pembentuk kata untuk inferensi makna — adalah salah satu perbedaan terbesar antara strong dan weak vocabulary learners di semua L2. Untuk Japanese, ini berimplikasi langsung pada kanji compound network: pelajar yang memahami 水 (water) + 泳 (swim) = 水泳 (swimming) memiliki *leverage* morfologis yang pelajar yang memperlakukan setiap kanji compound sebagai entri terpisah tidak miliki.

Indonesian learners memulai dari zero kanji knowledge — tidak ada cognate advantage. Ini membuat morphological awareness training menjadi khususnya high-yield: setiap morfem kanji yang dikuasai membuka inferential access ke puluhan compound baru. Dengan ~2,000 kanji standar (Jōyō Kanji), terdapat sekitar 50,000+ compound dalam penggunaan sehari-hari — menghafalnya satu per satu tidak feasible; membangun morphological awareness adalah satu-satunya scalable strategy.

## §CA.10.5 — WORD ASSOCIATION NETWORKS & SEMANTIC DEPTH

### Latar Belakang Teoritis

Dalam mental lexicon, kata tidak disimpan sebagai isolated entries tetapi sebagai *nodes dalam jaringan asosiatif* — terhubung ke kata-kata lain melalui semantic (sinonim, antonim, hiponim), collocational (kata-kata yang sering muncul bersama), dan morphological (anggota word family) links. L2 mental lexicon yang berkembang menunjukkan densifikasi jaringan ini dari waktu ke waktu: early learner memiliki sparse networks dengan banyak isolated nodes; advanced learner memiliki rich, dense networks yang menyerupai L1 native speaker organization.

Wolter (2001) menunjukkan bahwa L2 mental lexicon berorganisasi awalnya berdasarkan *formal* similarity (phonological, orthographic), bukan semantic similarity seperti pada L1 — suatu pola yang reverses seiring meningkatnya proficiency. Ini memiliki implikasi signifikan untuk deck organization: pada tahap awal, formal organization (hiragana/katakana groupings, pitch accent patterns) mungkin more cognitively natural; semantic and collocational network organization menjadi lebih cognitively accessible di tahap menengah-lanjut.

Kebanyakan adult L2 learners *arrested at stage 2* — ini adalah theoretical explanation untuk mengapa intermediate-level learners banyak errors meskipun vocabulary size-nya besar.

### Design Implications

1. **Deck organization by semantic network** (bukan hanya JLPT level) justified untuk N3+ learners yang telah melewati formal-similarity phase (Wolter, 2001).
2. **Three Aitchison organizing principles** dapat diimplementasikan sebagai deck architecture: topic-based main decks (semantic fields) + collocational notes dalam cards + hierarchical vocabulary groupings di UI.
3. **Jiang's stage arrest** problem adalah justifikasi untuk mengapa Nugget Nihongo menyertakan collocational, grammatical, dan pragmatic information — addressing the specific gap dimana kebanyakan adult L2 learners berhenti berkembang.

## §CA.10.6 — DESIGN IMPLICATIONS: DEPTH-BUILDING CARD FEATURES

### Sintesis §CA.10.1–§CA.10.5 → Spesifikasi Actionable

§CA.10.1–§CA.10.5 secara bersama-sama menghasilkan spesifikasi yang konkret untuk apa yang harus ada pada Nugget Nihongo cards untuk membangun vocabulary depth, bukan hanya breadth:

**1. Contoh Kalimat (Mandatory, bukan Optional)**
- Justifikasi: Webb (2007) VD-05 — varied-context repetition builds depth (d ≈ 0.61–0.78); Durrant & Schmitt (2010) VD-14 — explicit collocational presentation >> incidental exposure (d ≈ 0.82); Wray (2002) VD-12 — formulaic storage requires exposure to multi-word units.
- Implementasi: minimum 1 contoh kalimat per card untuk semua kata kerja dan kata keterangan; 1 kalimat untuk kata benda dengan collocational constraints yang tinggi. Catatan: multiple example sentences (2–3) untuk N2–N1 vocabulary, targeting varied collocational contexts.

**2. Collocational Notes untuk Verba dan Adverbia**
- Justifikasi: Howarth (1998) VD-10 — restricted collocations require explicit instruction; Nesselhauf (2005) VD-11 — verb-noun collocational errors most common; Yamashita & Jiang (2010) VD-13 — L1 transfer errors highest for incongruent collocations.
- Implementasi: Flag collocational partners yang conventional untuk する verbs, を verbs, dan adverbs dengan restricted distribution. Flag "⚠ Bukan [Indonesian equivalent]" untuk high-transfer-error collocations.

**3. Morphological Family Notes untuk Kanji Compounds (N3+)**
- Justifikasi: Kieffer & Lesaux (2008) VD-15 — morphological awareness independently predicts 12% reading comprehension variance; Mori (1998) VD-16 — explicit radical instruction improves compound inference accuracy.
- Implementasi: Cards untuk kanji compounds menyertakan radical/component breakdown (e.g., 水泳: 水 [air] + 泳 [berenang] = berenang/renang). Grouping view opsional yang menampilkan semua cards dalam satu morphological family.

**4. Register/Usage Notes**
- Justifikasi: Schmitt (1998) VD-08 — register appropriateness adalah salah satu aspek depth yang paling lambat berkembang secara incidental; Jiang (2000) VD-21 — most adult L2 learners arrested at stage 2 (tidak mencapai pragmatic integration).
- Implementasi: Flag register untuk kata-kata dengan register-distinct alternatives: めし vs. ご飯 vs. 食事; ありがとう vs. ありがとうございます vs. 感謝いたします. Dapat diimplementasikan sebagai optional "usage context" field pada cards.

**5. Cross-Card Semantic Network Links (N3+, Progressive Feature)**
- Justifikasi: Wolter (2001) VD-18 — depth adalah associative richness; Aitchison (2003) VD-19 — semantic fields, collocational links, dan hierarchical taxonomies sebagai mental lexicon organizing principles; Meara & Wolter (2004) VD-20 — V_Links model.
- Implementasi: "Related cards" feature yang menampilkan 3–5 semantically related cards. Prioritas: (a) sinonim/near-synonym dengan register distinction; (b) antonym; (c) morphological family members; (d) frequent collocational partners sebagai separate cards.

### Interaction dengan FSRS Architecture

Depth-building card features berinteraksi dengan FSRS scheduling:
- *Varied-context repetition* (Webb, VD-05): Implemented naturally jika deck includes multiple example sentences yang rotates dengan setiap review
- *Again/Hard/Good/Easy sebagai depth proxies*: Aligns dengan Henriksen (VD-04) three dimensions dan VKS stages (VD-06)
- *Production-format cards untuk depth*: Webb (VD-09) receptive-productive gap → production-format cards necessary for VKS stage 5; FSRS schedules these at appropriate intervals
