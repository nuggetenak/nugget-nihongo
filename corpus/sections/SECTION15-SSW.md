### §15.1 — Language for Specific Purposes: Theoretical Foundation

#### The Needs-Driven Turn in Language Pedagogy

The field of Language for Specific Purposes (LSP) emerged as a principled rejection of general-language teaching approaches that treat all learners as equivalent in motivation, context, and target use. The canonical theoretical statement of this shift is Hutchinson and Waters (1987), who argued that language learning must be understood as a purposive activity: learners are not engaged in language learning as an end in itself but as a means to accomplish specific vocational, academic, or professional goals. Their *English for Specific Purposes: A Learning-Centred Approach* established the foundational three-component needs analysis framework that continues to structure LSP course design: **target needs** (what the learner must be able to do in the target situation), **learning needs** (what the learner needs in order to learn), and the gap between them **[VS-01]**. This framework is not an English-language construct — it is a principled approach to purposive language teaching applicable to any L2 whose primary motivation is occupational or functional.

Dudley-Evans and St John (1998) extended Hutchinson and Waters' framework by distinguishing five roles for the LSP practitioner — teacher, course designer, materials provider, collaborator, and researcher — and by articulating a more granular needs analysis process that separates **target situation analysis** (TSA: what authentic language tasks must the learner perform?) from **present situation analysis** (PSA: what is the learner's current proficiency?) and from **learning situation analysis** (LSA: what conditions govern the learning environment?) **[VS-02]**. The distinction between TSA and PSA is particularly consequential for Nugget Nihongo: the platform's Indonesian SSW learner arrives with a defined target situation (a specific sector in a Japanese workplace), a specifiable present situation (typically A1–A2 Japanese proficiency at entry), and a well-documented learning situation (mobile-first, self-directed, time-constrained, often combined with full-time work or pre-departure study). All three analysis components are tractable — which is not always the case in general L2 contexts.

Basturkmen (2006) offers the most comprehensive treatment of LSP syllabus design for occupational contexts, distinguishing between **product-oriented syllabuses** (which specify what language items or skills the course will teach) and **process-oriented syllabuses** (which specify what tasks or activities the learner will engage in) **[VS-03]**. For a mobile flashcard platform, the product orientation is unavoidable — cards are discrete vocabulary and phrase items — but Basturkmen's argument that product selection must be justified against authentic task demands is the critical bridge: a card is not justified by its abstract frequency in a corpus but by its recurrence in target-situation tasks. A nursing care worker's card for *おむつを交換する* (to change a diaper) is justified by task demand, not by general-language frequency.

#### Needs Analysis as Methodology

Long (2005) provides the most methodologically rigorous treatment of needs analysis as a research method for language course design, distinguishing between **perceived needs** (what learners or employers believe is needed), **felt needs** (what learners themselves experience as gaps), and **objective needs** (what corpus, discourse, or workplace analysis reveals as actual language demands) **[VS-04]**. Long argues — against stakeholder-survey-only approaches — that objective needs analysis through authentic target discourse corpora must underpin course design, because perceived and felt needs systematically underestimate the linguistic complexity of authentic workplace communication. For the Indonesian SSW context, this distinction has direct policy implications: the formal Japanese proficiency requirements (JFT-Basic or JLPT N4) represent a regulatory minimum derived from perceived needs, while the authentic communicative demands of care work or food service production represent the objective needs that determine learner success post-arrival.

#### Applying LSP to Japanese for Indonesian SSW Workers

The application of LSP principles to Japanese for Indonesian SSW learners is theoretically straightforward but practically underresourced. Japanese LSP research exists for academic contexts (Japanese for Academic Purposes), medical contexts (医学日本語), and business contexts (ビジネス日本語), but the SSW occupational domain — care, food service, construction, agriculture — remains comparatively understudied in published LSP literature. The present section establishes the theoretical grounding for treating Nugget Nihongo's SSW deck not as a general Japanese vocabulary product with cultural content, but as a genuine LSP product: its content is defensible against occupational needs analysis, its sequencing reflects target-situation task demands, and its vocabulary selection criteria are grounded in the frequency, range, and availability framework established by Nation (§CA.1 — cross-reference) calibrated to SSW sector corpora.

**Design Implication:** Nugget Nihongo's SSW deck is not general Japanese vocabulary plus cultural content — it is a Language for Specific Purposes product. Every card in the SSW sub-decks should be traceable to an authentic target-situation task drawn from the relevant SSW sector. Cards that cannot be justified against a TSA for at least one major SSW sector (care, food service, construction, agriculture) belong in the general Japanese deck, not the SSW deck. This distinction operationalizes Hutchinson and Waters' **[VS-01]** core claim at the card level: content is justified by learner purpose, not by cultural interest or corpus frequency alone.

### §15.2 — Tokutei Ginou (特定技能): Language Requirements by Sector

#### The SSW Visa System: Structure and Scale

The Specified Skilled Worker (特定技能 — *Tokutei Ginou*) visa category was introduced by Japan's Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act revision of 2018 and implemented from April 2019. The system was designed to address critical labour shortages in sectors where Japan's aging population and declining birth rate have produced structural workforce deficits that cannot be addressed by domestic labour supply alone. The Immigration Services Agency of Japan administers the system and publishes quarterly statistical reports on SSW holders by nationality and designated industry sector **[VS-05]**. As of the system's most recent major expansion, sixteen industry sectors are designated under Tokutei Ginou Tier 1 (特定技能1号), including: nursing care (介護), building cleaning (ビルクリーニング), food and beverage manufacturing (飲食料品製造業), food service (外食業), construction (建設), agriculture (農業), fishery (漁業), accommodation (宿泊), automobile repair (自動車整備), aviation (航空), shipbuilding (造船・舶用工業), and several manufacturing sub-sectors.

Indonesian nationals consistently rank among the top three source countries for Tokutei Ginou applications, with the care sector (介護) and food manufacturing/service sectors (飲食料品製造業・外食業) representing the dominant pathways. This demographic reality is the commercial premise of Nugget Nihongo's SSW deck: the platform's primary user is an Indonesian worker preparing for, or recently placed within, one of these high-volume SSW sectors. This population does not need general Japanese — they need occupationally targeted Japanese with a defined proficiency threshold, a specific vocabulary load, and a time-bound preparation context.

#### Language Qualification Requirements: JFT-Basic and JLPT N4

Japanese language proficiency is a formal entry requirement for the Tokutei Ginou system. Two qualification pathways are accepted: passing the Japan Foundation Test for Basic Japanese (JFT-Basic / 日本語基礎テスト) or passing the Japanese Language Proficiency Test at N4 or above. The Japan Foundation designed the JFT-Basic specifically for SSW applicants, positioning it as a computer-adaptive test targeting A2-level Japanese as defined by the CEFR (§CA.3 — cross-reference for CEFR/JLPT alignment) **[VS-06]**. The JFT-Basic tests four modalities — vocabulary, grammar, listening, and reading — and is delivered at designated testing centers in source countries including Indonesia. Its vocabulary scope is approximately 1,500–2,000 Japanese items at A2 level, with an emphasis on functional communication in everyday and proto-occupational contexts rather than academic or literary language.

The alternative pathway, JLPT N4, tests reading and listening comprehension at approximately A2 level (§CA.3 — cross-reference for CEFR-J/JLPT equivalences) **[VS-07]**. JLPT N4 targets approximately 1,500 cumulative vocabulary items and covers basic grammar patterns sufficient for simple communication in familiar contexts. Both qualifications — JFT-Basic and JLPT N4 — represent the *minimum* threshold for SSW visa eligibility, not the language level at which effective workplace functioning is achieved. The research gap identified in §15.3 below concerns precisely this discrepancy: what authentic language level is required for genuine occupational effectiveness in each SSW sector, and how far above the regulatory minimum does the authentic target sit?

The nursing care sector (介護) operates under a separate and higher language requirement: care workers must demonstrate JLPT N4 as an entry condition *and* JLPT N3 within a specified period of employment, reflecting the sector's judgment that N4-level Japanese is insufficient for safe, effective communication in care settings where misunderstanding has direct consequences for client welfare. This is the only SSW sector with a mandatory post-entry language progression requirement, and it is a direct indicator that the regulatory minimum substantially underestimates authentic occupational language demand.

#### Sector-Specific Language Profiles

The SSW sector distribution produces differentiated authentic language demands. Nursing care (介護) requires a high proportion of interpersonal communication — directives, status checks, comfort and reassurance utterances, instructions, and formal documentation — with a significant proportion of age-appropriate informal register since clients are predominantly elderly. Food service and manufacturing (外食業・飲食料品製造業) require procedural vocabulary (recipe terms, equipment names, safety protocols, ordering and billing interactions), together with team communication in production or kitchen contexts where brevity, precision, and speed are operationally important. Construction (建設) requires technical nomenclature (materials, tools, structural elements), safety communication (emergency directives, hazard warnings), and team coordination under physically demanding conditions. Agriculture (農業) requires seasonal vocabulary, crop and equipment terminology, and basic business Japanese for market and supply chain interactions. Each of these sector profiles constitutes a distinct LSP sublanguage — a point developed further in §15.3 through the vocabulary needs analysis framework.

Research on the EPA (Economic Partnership Agreement) care worker program — a predecessor system that brought Indonesian and Filipino care workers to Japan under bilateral agreements from 2008 — provides the best available empirical evidence on authentic language demands in Japanese care work contexts. Noyama (2012) documented the Japanese language learning challenges faced by EPA candidate care workers in institutional settings, finding that the formal Japanese tested in JLPT examinations differed substantially from the interactional Japanese required in daily care work, and that candidates who passed JLPT N3 still frequently reported communication difficulties in authentic care tasks, particularly in understanding elderly speech, handling unexpected requests, and producing appropriate register for formal care documentation **[VS-08]**. This EPA-program evidence is the closest available empirical proxy for the SSW experience and represents the most direct published basis for arguing that the regulatory language threshold (N4/JFT-Basic) is insufficient for authentic occupational effectiveness.

**Design Implication:** Nugget Nihongo's SSW deck architecture should be organized by sector, with content prioritized by the two largest Indonesian SSW pathways — nursing care (介護) and food/food service (飲食料品製造業・外食業). The language threshold for deck completion should be communicated as exceeding the minimum regulatory requirement: JFT-Basic/N4 is the entry bar, but the SSW deck targets functional workplace communication at N3 in care and food service contexts. Card tags should include sector labels (e.g., `#kaigo`, `#shokuhin`, `#kensetsu`, `#nogyo`) to support filtered study aligned to the user's designated SSW sector. The Noyama (2012) **[VS-08]** finding on the gap between examination Japanese and authentic care-work Japanese should inform card design — care-sector cards should include authentic interactional examples, not only formal register.

### §15.3 — Vocabulary Needs Analysis for Japanese Occupational Domains

#### The General Service / Specific Purpose Distinction

The foundational methodological problem for SSW vocabulary curriculum design is the partition between general-service vocabulary and domain-specific vocabulary. Nation and Hwang (1995) addressed this problem directly, examining where general-service vocabulary — the high-frequency, high-range words that cover the bulk of text tokens across diverse domains — ends and where specialized vocabulary specific to particular fields begins **[VS-09]**. Their finding established a principled lexical stratification model: the general service core (approximately the most frequent 2,000 word families in a balanced corpus) covers approximately 87% of tokens in general text; specialist vocabulary covers a further 3–5% that is domain-specific but internally dense; technical vocabulary proper covers a final 1–3% that is highly specialized and narrow. For SSW learners, this stratification implies a three-tier learning priority: (1) master the JFT-Basic/N4 general service core (~1,500 Japanese lemmas) as an urgent priority; (2) acquire domain-specific SSW vocabulary (~300–500 sector-specific lemmas) as the primary added-value layer; (3) acquire technical sub-vocabulary as an ongoing workplace acquisition task. The first tier is addressed by Nugget Nihongo's general Japanese decks; the second tier is the primary target of the SSW deck; the third tier is partly scaffolded by the SSW deck and partly acquired through workplace immersion.

Chung and Nation (2003) refined this model specifically for technical vocabulary in specialised texts, proposing that technical vocabulary is characterised not only by domain-restricted distribution but by semantic precision — technical terms resist paraphrase in ways that general vocabulary does not, because their meaning is standardised within a professional community **[VS-10]**. In Japanese care work, this precision principle applies to terms like *バイタルサイン* (vital signs), *移乗* (patient transfer/repositioning), *誤嚥* (aspiration — the swallowing of food into the airway), and *看取り* (end-of-life care attendance). These terms are not merely infrequent in general corpora; they are semantically precise in ways that make them high-stakes items for care workers, where imprecision or misrecognition could compromise client safety. The SSW deck's care-sector vocabulary selection should be driven by this high-stakes precision criterion alongside frequency.

Coxhead (2018) provided the most comprehensive recent synthesis of vocabulary selection methodology for ESP contexts, arguing that ESP vocabulary research has moved beyond simple frequency-list derivation toward multi-dimensional needs analysis that incorporates learner consultation, corpus analysis of target-situation texts, and expert-informant validation by practitioners in the target field **[VS-11]**. For Nugget Nihongo's SSW deck, full multi-dimensional needs analysis is an aspirational long-term research program rather than a prerequisite for initial deck release — but Coxhead's framework establishes the intellectual standard against which the deck's vocabulary selection can be evaluated and progressively refined.

#### The Layered Architecture for SSW Vocabulary

The vocabulary literature supports a three-layer architecture for SSW learner decks:

**Layer 1 — JFT-Basic/N4 General Core (~1,500 lemmas):** This layer targets the vocabulary assessed by the JFT-Basic and JLPT N4, drawn from high-frequency general Japanese. Vocabulary size targets for this layer are established in §CA.1 and §CA.3 (cross-reference — not re-cited here). This layer should be sequenced frequency-first in line with Nation's recommendations (§CA.1 — cross-reference) and is the primary content of Nugget Nihongo's general Japanese decks. SSW learners who have not yet achieved JFT-Basic/N4 level should prioritize this layer before progressing to Layer 2.

**Layer 2 — SSW Sector Core (~300–500 lemmas per sector):** This layer is the primary target of the SSW deck. It contains domain-specific vocabulary identified through available sector-specific corpus evidence, practitioner consultation, and available research on workplace Japanese communication. Research on Japanese in care work contexts (介護日本語) — a body of literature that expanded substantially following the EPA care worker program from 2008 and the SSW system from 2019 — documents the core lexical items required for competent communication in care settings **[VS-12]**. For food service and food manufacturing, vocabulary identified from procedural task analysis of restaurant and production kitchen contexts constitutes the primary source. Nation and Hwang's **[VS-09]** general service boundary (~2,000 lemmas) provides the lower bound for what Layer 2 content must supplement: only items that fall outside the N4/JFT-Basic general core and recur in authentic SSW-sector texts belong in this layer.

**Layer 3 — Technical Sub-Vocabulary (acquired in situ):** This layer comprises highly technical terms — medical device names in care, specific equipment nomenclature in construction or manufacturing — that are best acquired through workplace exposure and are too narrow to justify the opportunity cost of dedicated deck cards. The SSW deck may scaffold Layer 3 acquisition by including representative items in Keterangan Tambahan notes, but should not invest card slots in items that learners will encounter and acquire naturally through workplace immersion.

#### The West–Nation Methodological Inheritance

The vocabulary selection methodology for LSP contexts inherits directly from West's (1953) foundational work on the General Service List (§CA.1 — cross-reference for full treatment of West). West's methodological innovation — combining frequency data with a utility filter that excluded pedagogically inert items — is precisely the approach required for SSW vocabulary: raw corpus frequency of occupational Japanese texts identifies candidate items, but a utility filter is then applied to retain only items that are authentically teachable as discrete vocabulary units (not grammatical functions), that appear across multiple sub-contexts within the target sector (range), and that are not already covered by the JFT-Basic/N4 general core (additionality). The West methodology, as formalised by Nation's multi-criteria framework (§CA.1 — cross-reference), provides the principled procedure for constructing Nugget Nihongo's SSW vocabulary lists from sector-specific Japanese corpora.

#### The Research Gap and Its Implications

A candid assessment of the available published research must acknowledge that Japanese LSP for SSW occupational contexts is an emerging rather than a mature research area. The English-language LSP literature reviewed in §15.1 provides a robust theoretical and methodological framework **[VS-01, VS-02, VS-03, VS-04]**. The Japanese care-work language research — stimulated by the EPA program — provides the best available empirical evidence on authentic language demands, though much of this research is in Japanese-language journals with limited international circulation and unverified DOIs **[VS-08, VS-12]**. Vocabulary needs analysis studies specifically targeting SSW-sector Japanese for Indonesian learners essentially do not yet exist as a published research base; the SSW system itself is less than a decade old. This gap is simultaneously a limitation and an opportunity: Nugget Nihongo is operating in a space where the academic literature has not yet caught up with the policy and commercial reality, and the platform's eventual card usage data — which SSW-sector cards are reviewed, which are failed, which are retired — represents a potential empirical contribution to this emerging research area if appropriately documented and analysed.

**Design Implication:** The SSW deck should be architecturally structured as two distinct layers: Layer 1 (JFT-Basic/N4 general core, housed in the general Japanese deck) and Layer 2 (SSW sector-specific vocabulary, approximately 300–500 items per major sector). Layer 2 cards should carry sector tags (`#kaigo`, `#shokuhin`, `#kensetsu`, `#nogyo`) enabling filtered study. Vocabulary selection for Layer 2 should follow West's utility filter **[VS-09]** — frequency within sector-specific contexts, combined with additionality over the N4 general core. In the absence of a published Japanese-language equivalent of the General Service List calibrated to SSW contexts, Nugget Nihongo's initial Layer 2 content should be drawn from expert practitioner consultation, available care-work Japanese textbook vocabulary lists, and sector-specific workplace document analysis, with the explicit intention of refining content against learner performance data as the platform matures.

### §15.4 — Identity and Investment Theory in Migrant Language Learning

#### Theoretical Foundation

The dominant framework for understanding adult migrant language learning motivation is not cognitive but social. Norton's investment construct, introduced in her foundational monograph *Identity and Language Learning* **[VS-16]**, proposes that language learning is not merely a cognitive act of internalising a new code — it is a social practice through which learners invest in, contest, and reconstruct their identities. Norton argues that when adult learners invest in a target language, they do so with the understanding that they will thereby acquire a wider range of symbolic and material resources, and that the return on this investment must be commensurate with the cost — the time, effort, vulnerability, and identity risk it entails.

The precursor to Norton's monograph is Peirce's (1995) *TESOL Quarterly* article **[VS-17]**, which first introduced the term "investment" as a theoretically motivated alternative to "motivation" in SLA research. Peirce argued that the concept of motivation — as typically operationalised in Gardner and Lambert's integrative/instrumental dichotomy — treated the learner as a unified individual with a stable psychological orientation toward the target language community. Investment theory, by contrast, acknowledges that the learner is a *historically situated*, *multiply positioned* subject whose relationship to the target language is dynamic, contradictory, and linked to real material stakes. A Filipino domestic worker in Hong Kong and an Indonesian SSW applicant preparing for Japan are not "unmotivated" or "motivated" in the abstract — they are investing in a specific imagined future with calculable expected returns.

Darvin and Norton (2015) **[VS-18]** updated the investment model to incorporate Bourdieu's capital framework **[VS-21]** explicitly. In Darvin and Norton's revised model, language learning operates simultaneously across three dimensions of capital: *economic capital* (direct income from employment in Japan), *social capital* (network access to employers, colleagues, and institutional gatekeepers), and *symbolic capital* (status, legitimacy, and the right to be heard in a Japanese workplace). The model situates investment within a field of ideological positions — beliefs about which languages have value, which accents signal competence, and which learners deserve recognition — and recognises that learners with less social power must invest more while obtaining less symbolic return. For Indonesian SSW applicants, the asymmetry is stark: Japanese workplace norms require linguistic deference from low-status newcomers precisely from the category of speakers who arrive with the smallest investment capital and the highest stakes.

#### Imagined Communities and the SSW Applicant

The concept of "imagined communities," imported from Anderson's (1983) work on nationalism **[VS-20]** and applied to language education by Norton (2013) **[VS-19]**, provides a critical supplementary lens. SSW learners do not merely learn Japanese as an abstract system — they are investing toward membership in an *imagined* professional community: the Japanese care facility, the Japanese kitchen brigade, the Japanese construction crew. The imagined community is experienced as real and motivationally potent even before the learner has set foot in Japan, because it supplies the social identity position that gives the investment its meaning. A learner who can picture themselves as a competent 介護職員 (care worker) giving respectful instructions to an elderly patient has a concretised investment target that sustains effort through the months of pre-departure study.

Norton's updated edition (2013) **[VS-19]** develops the pedagogical implications of imagined communities: instructional design should connect target-language content to the specific imagined community the learner is investing toward. For Nugget Nihongo, this means that SSW-track vocabulary should not be presented in generic contexts ("a person walks to the station") but in sector-specific situated contexts that activate the imagined community — care-sector dialogues, food-production instructions, construction-site safety notices — so that each review session is simultaneously a cognitive retrieval act and an imagined community rehearsal.

#### Fluctuating Investment and Pedagogical Design

A key empirical observation that investment theory explains, but motivational psychology alone does not, is the *pattern of motivational fluctuation* that characterises SSW applicants in the pre-departure phase. Investment theory predicts that motivation will be highest when the expected return is most salient (visa application imminent, exam booking confirmed) and lowest when the investment timeline is uncertain or the pathway bureaucratically stalled. This is not "low motivation" in a trait sense — it is a rational response to uncertainty about returns. Pedagogical design cannot resolve bureaucratic uncertainty, but it can sustain investment by keeping the imagined community vivid and by providing concrete evidence that investment is accumulating. Progress metrics function as investment ledgers: JLPT N4 card completion percentages, SSW-sector coverage indicators, and milestone notifications are not gamification decorations but investment-evidence signals that sustain engagement through periods when the return timeline is unclear.

This framework complements, but is distinct from, L2MSS (§8 [MV-03+]) — L2MSS models the "ideal L2 self" as the motivational engine; investment theory examines the *economic and social mechanics* that make that imagined self a real stake in a real field. Neither framework subsumes the other. For SSW learners in particular, investment theory is the more powerful descriptor because the returns are concrete, calculable, and institutionally defined (visa eligibility, employment contract, income).

*Cross-references: §8 [MV-03+] L2MSS — cross-reference only, do not re-cite. §13.5 [ID-29–ID-35] growth mindset — the investment construct parallels growth mindset research in emphasising the role of framing effort as accumulating capital rather than exposing fixed ability.*

**Design Implication:** Nugget Nihongo's onboarding and progress architecture should make the investment trajectory explicitly visible. JLPT N4 countdown indicators, SSW-sector vocabulary completion percentages, and milestone notifications are not gamification features — they are investment-evidence mechanisms that sustain learner motivation when bureaucratic timelines are uncertain. The platform should frame each review session as a concrete increment of vocational capital, not merely a memory exercise.

### §15.5 — Japanese Workplace Communication: Keigo Register in Professional Context

#### The Register Gap: What JLPT Does Not Prepare Learners For

Standard Japanese language instruction — and JLPT preparation materials up to and including N3 — treats keigo (敬語) as a grammar topic: the rules for transforming plain-form verbs into respectful or humble equivalents, the distinction between 尊敬語 (sonkeigo), 謙譲語 (kenjōgo), and 丁寧語 (teineigo), and the social conditions under which each register applies. This grammatical treatment is necessary but fundamentally insufficient for authentic workplace communication.

Cook's (2008) work on the role of speech style in professional and institutional settings **[SC-05]** demonstrates that keigo in authentic workplace contexts functions not as a set of discrete grammatical rules but as a continuous indexical system through which speakers locate themselves in institutional hierarchies, signal solidarity or distance, and manage face in real time. The opacity of this system to non-native speakers is not merely a vocabulary or morphology problem — it is a pragmatic participation problem. A speaker who produces grammatically correct 謙譲語 forms in inappropriate interactional contexts (using them toward peers rather than superiors, omitting them in contexts where they are obligatory) signals social incompetence even if every individual morpheme is accurate. The social consequences of register errors in Japanese professional settings are disproportionate to their surface form, because keigo encodes institutional position directly. A register error does not merely sound "impolite" — it communicates that the speaker does not understand the social order.

Niyekawa's (1991) guide to the honorific system **[VS-23]** documents the layered complexity of keigo as actually practised in Japanese institutional contexts, distinguishing between the classroom-teachable rules and the contextually negotiated actual usage patterns that native speakers navigate intuitively. Niyekawa's analysis is particularly relevant for SSW workers because it highlights the asymmetrical register demands on junior newcomers: as the lowest-status person in any room, the SSW worker is obligated to use the highest keigo levels toward virtually every interlocutor — supervisors, senior colleagues, and patients or clients — while receiving plain or minimally formal speech in return. This asymmetry is extreme relative to the social register demands placed on any other category of foreign resident in Japan.

#### The EPA Research Baseline

The most directly relevant empirical research base for SSW workplace communication is the literature on the Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA) programs that have sent Indonesian and Filipino care workers to Japan since 2008. EPA candidates must pass Japanese language certification requirements (originally JLPT N5 at entry, with N3 or N2 required for full certification) while working in Japanese care facilities — producing a natural experiment in workplace language acquisition under institutional pressure. Ogawa (2012) and subsequent EPA monitoring research **[VS-24]** has documented the systematic mismatch between the register competence tested in EPA examinations and the register competence demanded in actual 介護 (nursing care) workplaces. EPA care workers report that examination-level keigo prepares them for test items but not for the real interactional challenge: producing appropriate register continuously across an entire workday, in fast-paced contexts, without the processing time available during written examination. The gap is not content knowledge — EPA workers know the forms — it is automaticity and contextual deployment under cognitive load.

This finding from EPA research has direct implications for SSW preparation, as SSW workers in 介護 (Care) and 外食業 (Food Service) sectors face comparable register demands without the institutional support infrastructure of EPA programs.

#### The Keigo-Vocabulary Intersection

An additional complication for the SSW sector specifically is that keigo competence is not separable from domain vocabulary. Expressing deference while discussing care procedures, food preparation sequences, or safety protocols requires not only the appropriate register morphology but the appropriate technical lexicon — and the two must be produced simultaneously, under real-time conditions. The compound demand (domain vocabulary + register performance) means that SSW sector keigo cannot be trained as a grammar module appended to otherwise generic Japanese vocabulary study. It must be trained as a *situated speech practice* in which domain vocabulary and register are co-acquired.

For the 介護 sector in particular, high-frequency register-marked patterns include: formulaic expressions for addressing 利用者 (service users / residents) by title, polite request forms for physical assistance, and the set phrases used in shift handover reports (申し送り, mōshiokuri). These patterns are highly routinised in Japanese care facilities but are not covered in any standard JLPT preparation material.

*Cross-reference forward: For keigo acquisition theory and interlanguage pragmatics — the process by which learners internalise pragmatic competence — see §14 [SC cluster — forthcoming]. §15.5 covers keigo as a workplace requirement; §14 covers keigo as an acquisition target.*

**Design Implication:** The SSW deck must include a dedicated Keigo in Context sub-deck that goes substantially beyond JLPT N4 honorifics. Authentic workplace keigo for the 介護 and 外食業 sectors requires an estimated 50–80 additional high-frequency register-marked patterns, including formulaic shift-report language, polite request structures for physical assistance contexts, and title-based address forms for 利用者 — none of which appear in standard JLPT preparation materials. These patterns should be presented in sector-specific situated contexts, not as isolated morphological rules.

### §15.6 — Heritage Speaker vs. Migrant Worker Learner Profiles: A Synthesis

#### Defining the Population: Who is the Nugget Nihongo User?

Second language Japanese research operates across at least three distinct learner populations with non-overlapping profiles: (1) heritage speakers — individuals with Japanese family background for whom the language is partially or fully domestic; (2) academic learners — students enrolled in Japanese language programs at university, typically in Western anglophone contexts; and (3) migrant worker learners — adults acquiring Japanese instrumentally for employment purposes, typically with no heritage connection and a full adult L1 phonological system in place. Kondo-Brown's (2006) work on heritage language development in East Asian immigrant contexts **[VS-25]** documents the sharp divergence between heritage and academic learner profiles across vocabulary knowledge, phonological accuracy, and affective relationship to the target language. Heritage speakers often present with native-like phonology but systematic lexical gaps in formal registers; academic learners present with the inverse profile — extensive grammatical knowledge but non-native phonology and limited pragmatic range.

The Indonesian SSW applicant belongs unambiguously to the third category. There is no heritage Japanese community in Indonesia; Bahasa Indonesia and Japanese are typologically distant in both phonology and morphosyntax; and the learner relationship to Japanese is instrumental and economic rather than identity-constitutive in the diaspora sense. This means that research findings on heritage Japanese learner populations — which dominate the heritage language literature — are not transferable to the SSW context. The vocabulary gaps, identity stakes, and pedagogical entry points differ categorically.

#### The Modal User Profile: Re-Entry Learners from SMA

Empirically, the modal Nugget Nihongo user is not a complete beginner. Indonesian secondary education (SMA — Sekolah Menengah Atas) includes Japanese as an elective foreign language at approximately 30–40% of schools in urban and semi-urban areas, particularly in Java, Bali, and Sulawesi. The typical SSW applicant has therefore had 2–3 years of classroom Japanese in SMA, followed by a gap period of 3–8 years during which active Japanese use was minimal or absent. This produces a re-entry learner profile: a learner who is not building the language from zero but reactivating a dormant L2 system.

Hansen's (2011) comprehensive documentation of second language attrition in Japanese contexts **[VS-26]** identifies the key characteristics of the attrition-reactivation cycle: phonological and procedural skills attrite fastest; declarative knowledge (vocabulary recognition, metalinguistic rules) is more stable over dormancy; comprehension recovers faster than production in reactivation. For pedagogical design, this profile implies that re-entry SSW learners benefit more from reactivation-focused retrieval practice — triggering existing dormant memory traces — than from instruction sequences designed for true beginners. The FSRS algorithm is well-suited to this population: presenting items at the boundary between recognition and recall, rather than beginning with rote introduction of "new" forms, aligns with the reactivation dynamics of dormant L2 knowledge.

The pedagogical risk in treating re-entry learners as beginners is twofold: (1) beginning-level content is demotivating for learners who retain partial competence and correctly perceive they are being underestimated; (2) beginning-level pacing is inefficient, because reactivation of dormant knowledge is far faster than initial acquisition. A learner who "knows" that です is the copula but cannot yet produce it fluently needs retrieval practice and automatisation — not re-explanation. The Nugget Nihongo default placement pathway should distinguish between true beginners and re-entry learners at onboarding, and adjust starting deck position accordingly.

#### Bahasa Indonesia as L1 — Interface with Contrastive Analysis

The Bahasa Indonesia L1 system creates a specific and well-documented phonological and morphosyntactic transfer profile for Japanese acquisition. The contrastive analysis of Indonesian and Japanese phonology, pitch accent, and morphological typology is fully developed in §5.5 [CA cluster] and is not reproduced here. What §15.6 adds is the *learner-profile* dimension: the specific features of the Indonesian SMA classroom context in which Japanese was originally acquired, which shapes the transfer pattern.

Indonesian SMA Japanese instruction is heavily focused on hiragana and katakana literacy, basic script recognition, and production of standard classroom routines (自己紹介, 日常会話). It typically does not include keigo instruction, kanji beyond N5-level, or any content resembling SSW-sector vocabulary. The re-entry learner therefore presents with: intact kana literacy (recoverable rapidly), fragmented N5-level vocabulary (partially dormant), no kanji depth beyond elementary, no workplace register competence, and a phonological system shaped by Indonesian mora timing and the absence of pitch accent distinction. For contrastive analysis specifics, see §5.5 [CA-01 ff.].

#### The SSW Applicant Demographic: A Composite Profile

Drawing together investment theory (§15.4), workplace register demands (§15.5), and learner profile research, the composite SSW applicant demographic can be characterised as follows. Japan Foundation (2022) survey data **[VS-27]** confirms that Indonesian Japanese learners are concentrated in the 18–34 age bracket and are predominantly urban or semi-urban in origin. The typical SSW applicant is approximately 22–35 years old, mobile-first in their internet and study behaviour (consistent with the 95% smartphone internet access rate documented in §8.10), studying in the evening or during commute time, with a JFT-Basic or JLPT N4 examination booked within the next 3–6 months. Study sessions are short (20–40 minutes), pressure-sensitive (abandonment is triggered by stress or examination anxiety), and socially isolated — there is typically no classroom community, no instructor, and no speaking partner. The investment is high and the stakes are concrete.

This profile differs categorically from the university student in a Japanese studies program who is the implicit default user of most academic SLA research. It also differs from the heritage learner. Any pedagogical intervention — including Nugget Nihongo's card design, onboarding flow, and progress feedback — must be tested against this specific user profile, not against the academic or heritage learner norm. The Individual Differences literature (§13 [ID cluster]) documents the specific anxiety, aptitude, working memory, and mindset profiles that modulate this learner population's engagement with SRS-based study — those dimensions are not reproduced here but are directly applicable to the SSW composite profile described in this subsection.

*Cross-references: §5.5 [CA cluster] contrastive linguistics Indonesia↔Japanese — cross-reference only. §13 [ID-01 ff.] — FLCA, aptitude, working memory, mindset profiles — cross-reference only. §17 [AL cluster — forthcoming] — for adult learning theory and andragogy applied to this learner profile, see §17 (Agents 26–27, forthcoming).*

**Design Implication:** The platform's default learner profile is not a university student enrolled in a Japanese studies program. It is a 25-year-old Indonesian adult with 2 years of SMA Japanese (3–6 years dormant), studying on an Android phone during a 40-minute commute, with a JFT-Basic exam booked in 3 months and an SSW visa application contingent on the result. Every UX and content decision — card length, reading time estimation, session duration defaults, error feedback framing, vocabulary scope — should be tested against this profile. Re-entry learner reactivation dynamics, not beginner acquisition sequences, should govern placement logic and initial deck structure.
