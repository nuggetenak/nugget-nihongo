## SCOPE & SUBSECTIONS YANG DIPERLUKAN

### §9.10.1 — Orthographic Depth Hypothesis
**Core question:** Mengapa Japanese reading fluency membutuhkan waktu jauh lebih lama untuk berkembang daripada Italian atau Spanish?

**Citation seeds:**
- Frost, R., Katz, L., & Bentin, S. (1987). Strategies for visual word recognition and orthographic depth. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance, 13*(1), 104–115. https://doi.org/10.1037/0096-1523.13.1.104
- Katz, L., & Frost, R. (1992). The reading process is different for different orthographies: The orthographic depth hypothesis. In Frost & Katz (Eds.), *Orthography, phonology, morphology, and meaning* (pp. 67–84). North-Holland/Elsevier. *(no DOI — book chapter)*
- Seymour, P. H. K., Aro, M., & Erskine, J. M. (2003). Foundation literacy acquisition in European orthographies. *British Journal of Psychology, 73*(3), 417–433. https://doi.org/10.1348/000712603322503285

**Klaim kunci:** Orthographic depth (opacity of the writing system) directly predicts reading acquisition time. Japanese adalah extreme deep orthography case: kanji requires whole-word visual recognition (lexical route), while kana can use phonological decoding (sublexical route). Indonesian learners — from shallow Latin orthography — face maximum depth challenge.

### §9.10.2 — Dual Route Model of Visual Word Recognition
**Core question:** Bagaimana otak membaca kanji vs. kana secara mekanistis berbeda?

**Citation seeds:**
- Coltheart, M., Rastle, K., Perry, C., Langdon, R., & Ziegler, J. (2001). DRC: A dual route cascaded model of visual word recognition and reading aloud. *Psychological Review, 108*(1), 204–256. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.108.1.204
- Wydell, T. N., & Butterworth, B. (1999). A case study of an English-Japanese bilingual with monolingual dyslexia. *Cognition, 70*(3), 273–305. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0010-0277(99)00016-5

**Japanese application:** Kanji words are recognized via *lexical route* (direct visual-to-meaning lookup — pattern recognition, whole-word). Kana are processed via *sublexical route* (phonological decoding, mora-by-mora). This has direct implications for card design: kanji-focus cards should emphasize whole-word recognition training, not character-by-character decoding.

### §9.10.4 — Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN) & Reading Speed
**Core question:** Bagaimana kita mengukur perkembangan menuju automaticity dalam script recognition?

**Citation seeds:**
- Wolf, M., & Bowers, P. G. (1999). The double-deficit hypothesis for the developmental dyslexias. *Journal of Educational Psychology, 91*(3), 415–438. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.91.3.415
- Georgiou, G. K., Papadopoulos, T. C., & Parrila, R. (2020). Comparing the contribution of RAN components to reading in different orthographies: A review. *Journal of Learning Disabilities, 53*(1), 3–16. https://doi.org/10.1177/0022219419865492

**Relevance:** RAN (speed of naming familiar symbols) predicts reading fluency development across orthographies. For kanji, rapid naming is a proxy for lexical route automatization. Potential application for Nugget Nihongo: recognition speed as a FSRS parameter input (beyond accuracy alone).

### §9.10.6 — Working Memory × Kanji Reading
**Core question:** Bagaimana WM capacity mempengaruhi perkembangan reading fluency untuk triple-script system?

**Citation seeds:**
- Harrington, M., & Sawyer, M. (1992) — cited above; dapat digunakan di §9.10.5 dan §9.10.6 jika relevan di kedua konteks (cukup satu entry, cross-reference di kedua subseksi)
- Cross-reference ke §13.3 [ID-15–ID-21] Working Memory cluster — jangan re-cite Baddeley atau Miyake & Friedman; cross-reference ID-15–ID-21 saja

**Design implication:** WM capacity limits untuk multi-script reading (switching hiragana/katakana/kanji mid-sentence) adalah greater than single-script. This justifies deck structure that builds one script to automaticity before introducing the next — justified by WM research convergence between §9 addendum dan §13.3.
